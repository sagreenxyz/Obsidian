# Chapter 7, Calculations Used in Patient Assessments

To achieve proficiency in the calculations used for patient assessments, a student must master three primary domains: volumetric conversions for intake and output, temperature scales, and weight/length measurements. Below are the essential main points and the specific nuances required for mastery based on the clinical standards presented.

### Main Points for Mastery

#### 1. Volumetric Conversions (Intake and Output)

* **Standard Equivalents:** Students must memorize and fluidly apply the following:
* **1 ounce (oz) = 30 milliliters (mL)**
* **1 cup (c) = 240 milliliters (mL)**


* **Intake Calculation:** This involves summing all fluids consumed by the patient. In a clinical setting, this includes water, juice, coffee, milk, broth, and items that are liquid at room temperature, such as gelatin (Jell-O) and ice cream.
* **Output Calculation:** This involves summing all fluids eliminated or drained from the patient, including urine, emesis (vomit), wound drainage, and surgical drain output.
* **24-Hour Totals:** Students must be able to aggregate totals from different shifts (e.g., 0700–1500, 1500–2300, and 2300–0700) to provide a complete 24-hour clinical picture.

#### 2. Temperature Conversions

* **Fahrenheit to Celsius:** Subtract 32 from the Fahrenheit temperature, then divide by 1.8.
* Formula: (F - 32) / 1.8 = C


* **Celsius to Fahrenheit:** Multiply the Celsius temperature by 1.8, then add 32.
* Formula: (C × 1.8) + 32 = F



#### 3. Weight and Length Conversions

* **Weight:** 1 kilogram (kg) = 2.2 pounds (lb).
* **Length:** 1 inch (in) = 2.5 centimeters (cm).
* Students must be able to convert in both directions (e.g., kg to lb for medication dosing and lb to kg for standard documentation).

#### 4. Intravenous (IV) Infusion Calculations

* **Volume Infused:** Determining the total amount of fluid a patient received via IV over a specific timeframe.
* **Complex Infusions:** Including Intravenous Piggyback (IVPB) medications (e.g., antibiotics in 100 mL bags) and blood products (e.g., 1 unit of packed red blood cells) as part of the total intake.

### Nuances to Master

#### 1. Precision in Rounding

The most common source of error in clinical calculations is failing to follow specific rounding instructions. Mastery requires:

* **Tenths vs. Whole Numbers:** Identifying when to round to the nearest tenth (common for temperature and weight) versus the nearest whole number (often required for I&O or specific exam instructions).
* **Intermediate Steps:** Carrying decimals through the calculation and only rounding at the final step to ensure accuracy.

#### 2. Fraction-to-Decimal Conversion

Clinical problems often present intake in fractions (e.g., 1/3 cup or 1 1/4 cups).

* **1/3 Cup Nuance:** Converting 1/3 to 0.33. When multiplied by 240 mL, this yields 79.2, which rounds to **79 mL**. (Note: Using 80 mL is a common error).
* **Mixed Fractions:** Converting 1 1/4 cups to 1.25 before multiplying by the 240 mL constant.

#### 3. Managing Discontinuous IV Fluids

Students must account for changes in IV status throughout a shift:

* **Infiltrations:** If an IV stops due to infiltration or is discontinued, only calculate the volume for the hours it was actually running.
* **Held Fluids:** If primary IV fluids are held to administer blood products, the student must subtract that time from the primary fluid total and add the volume of the blood product.

#### 4. Hidden Intake Items

Mastery requires recognizing that items like ice cream and gelatin are counted as fluid intake, whereas solid foods are not. Additionally, specialized fluids like IVPB medications and surgical flushes must be included in the "Intake" column of an I&O sheet even if they are not "oral" intake.

#### 5. Calculation Methodologies

Proficiency involves being comfortable with multiple mathematical approaches:

* **Dimensional Analysis:** Setting up conversion factors in a row to cancel out units.
* **Proportion Method:** Setting up ratios (e.g., 1 oz : 30 mL :: 7 oz : X mL).
* **Formula Method:** Specifically for IV rates and temperature.

#### 6. Test-Taking Strategy

* **Units of Measure:** Always ensure the final answer is in the requested unit (e.g., converting oz to mL before adding it to other mL totals).
* **Sanity Checks:** Recognizing if a number "makes sense" (e.g., 100 kg should be 220 lb; if the result is smaller than 100, the conversion was likely done in reverse).
