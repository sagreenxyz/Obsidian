# Chapter 9, Interpretation of the Licensed Prescriber's Orders

To master the interpretation of licensed prescriber's orders, a student must understand the legal responsibilities of a nurse, the technical components of a valid order, and the specific protocols for safety and communication. Below are the main points and the critical nuances required for proficiency.

### Main Points for Mastery

**1. Components of a Complete Medication Order**
A nurse must never administer a medication unless the order is 100% complete. A valid order must include:

* **Patient Information:** Full name, medical record number, and date of birth.
* **Order Details:** Date and time the order was written.
* **Medication Specifics:** Drug name (generic or proprietary), exact dosage, and route of administration (e.g., PO, IV, SC).
* **Scheduling:** Frequency of administration and, for PRN medications, the specific indication (e.g., "for pain" or "for fever").
* **Prescriber Authentication:** The signature of the ordering provider.

**2. Legal and Ethical Responsibilities of the Nurse**
The nurse is the final "check" in the medication administration process and is ultimately responsible for any errors that occur.

* **Verification:** Nurses must compare the prescriber’s order with the Medication Administration Record (MAR).
* **Clinical Knowledge:** A nurse is legally required to know the medication’s actions, contraindications, and the safe minimum and maximum dosage ranges.
* **Allergy Checks:** It is the nurse's duty to verify that the patient has no known allergies to the ordered drug before administration.
* **Refusal to Administer:** If an order is incomplete, incorrect, or unsafe, the nurse must clarify it with the prescriber rather than proceeding.

**3. Communication Protocols (Verbal and Telephone Orders)**
Verbal and telephone orders are high-risk and should be restricted to emergency situations (e.g., respiratory arrest).

* **The VORB Process:** The nurse must **Write it down**, **Read it back**, and **Receive verification**.
* **Documentation:** The nurse must record the prescriber’s name, a notation that it was a verbal/telephone order (e.g., VORB), and their own signature.

**4. Time and Dosage Calculations**

* **Military Time:** Mastery of the 24-hour clock is essential for safety. (e.g., 0700 is 7:00 a.m., 1800 is 6:00 p.m., and 2400 is 12:00 midnight).
* **Frequency Calculations:** Determining the number of doses in a 24-hour period (e.g., q3h equals 8 doses).
* **Unit-Dose System:** Understanding that these are pre-measured, ready-to-administer doses prepared by the pharmacy or manufacturer.

### Nuances for Proficiency

**1. The Strict Sequence of Verbal Orders**
A critical nuance is the order of operations when receiving a verbal order. A nurse should never "read back" before "writing down." You must write the order as you hear it to ensure you are reading back exactly what you recorded, which the prescriber then verifies.

**2. Identifying "Incomplete" vs. "Complete" Orders**
Many orders appear complete but lack one critical element.

* **Route Omission:** Common errors include orders like "Insulin 10 units before breakfast" or "Metoprolol 50 mg daily." These are invalid because they do not specify the route (SC, IV, or PO).
* **PRN Indication:** A PRN order (as needed) is legally incomplete without the reason for use (e.g., "Morphine 2 mg IV q4h" is incomplete; it must say "q4h prn for pain").
* **Time Ambiguity:** An order written as "Diazepam 5 mg at 8" is invalid because it does not specify 0800 or 2000.

**3. Approved vs. Dangerous Abbreviations**

* **Approved:** ac (before meals), pc (after meals), prn (as needed), STAT (immediately), and qh (hourly).
* **Discouraged/Dangerous:** "bid" (twice daily) is discouraged by the Institute for Safe Medication Practices (ISMP) because it is prone to misinterpretation. Abbreviations like "u" for units or "MSO4" for morphine should be avoided and written out in full to prevent life-threatening errors.

**4. EHR vs. Manual Systems**

* **Electronic (eMAR):** Provides faster medication delivery, access to online resources, and eliminates handwriting errors, but the nurse still holds the responsibility to verify the order.
* **Handwritten (MAR):** While lower in cost and requiring less training, they significantly increase the risk of medication errors due to poor legibility.

**5. Non-Emergent Verbal Orders**
A nuance often missed in clinical practice is that a nurse should **refuse** a verbal order if there is no emergency. If a prescriber is simply "in a hurry" to leave the unit, the nurse must insist the order be entered into the electronic health record or written down by the prescriber first. Accepting a verbal order in a non-emergency is an unacceptable safety risk.
