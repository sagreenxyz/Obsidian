# Chapter 12, Parenteral Dosages

To master the topic of parenteral dosages, a student must understand the foundational principles of injectable medication administration, the technical characteristics of the equipment used, and the precise mathematical applications required for safe patient care.

### Main Points: Foundational Knowledge

* **Parenteral Routes of Administration:** Parenteral refers to medications administered by injection. The primary routes include:
* **Intravenous (IV):** Used for the most rapid action (STAT orders) because medication enters the bloodstream directly.
* **Intramuscular (IM):** Injected into muscle tissue.
* **Subcutaneous (SQ/SC):** Injected into the fatty tissue below the skin.
* **Intradermal (ID):** Injected just under the epidermis.


* **Clinical Indications:** The parenteral route is preferred over oral (PO) or other routes when:
* A patient is unconscious.
* The patient has a nonfunctioning gastrointestinal system.
* The patient is experiencing frequent emesis (vomiting).
* Rapid medication action is required.


* **Medication Packaging:**
* **Vials:** Plastic or glass containers with self-sealing rubber tops. They can contain liquid or powder and may be single-dose or multi-dose.
* **Ampules:** All-glass containers that must be broken to access the medication (often identified by specific label markings).
* **Mix-O-Vials:** A specialized closed system with two compartments separated by a rubber stopper; the top contains diluent and the bottom contains powdered medication.
* **Prefilled Syringes:** Ready-to-use syringes containing a specific volume and dose.


* **Syringe Selection and Calibration:**
* **3-mL Syringe:** The standard for most IM and SQ injections, typically calibrated in 0.1-mL increments.
* **Tuberculin (TB) Syringe:** A narrow 1-mL syringe used for small, precise volumes (calibrated in hundredths).
* **Insulin Syringe:** Specifically calibrated in **units**, not milliliters, and used exclusively for insulin.
* **Large-Capacity Syringes (5-mL, 10-mL, etc.):** Used for larger volumes; calibrations may represent 0.2-mL or whole milliliter increments.



### Nuances for Mastery

* **Precise Measurement Technique:** The only accurate way to measure medication in a syringe is at the point where the **rubber plunger edge makes contact with the barrel**. Measuring from the raised section or the bottom ring of the plunger tip will result in dosage errors.
* **Handling Mix-O-Vials:** There is a strict sequence for preparation:
1. Apply pressure to the top of the vial to release the internal rubber stopper.
2. Agitate the vial to thoroughly dissolve the powder into the diluent.
3. Only then should the nurse prepare the syringe, inject air, and withdraw the dose.


* **Prefilled Syringe Safety:** If a prefilled syringe contains more medication than ordered, the excess must be **discarded before administration**. Discarding it after the procedure increases the risk of the patient receiving an overdose.
* **Required Conversions:** Mastery requires automatic conversion between units.
* **Weight:** Always convert pounds (lb) to kilograms (kg) by dividing by 2.2 before calculating weight-based dosages (mg/kg).
* **Mass:** Be prepared to convert milligrams (mg) to micrograms (mcg) (1 mg = 1000 mcg) or grams to milligrams to ensure the "Desired" and "Have" units match in formulas.


* **Rounding Rules:** * Dosages should be expressed as decimals, even if the syringe has fraction markings.
* Small volumes (under 1 mL) are often rounded to the nearest hundredth (0.01), while larger volumes are typically rounded to the nearest tenth (0.1).


* **Administrative Speed:** For medications given via IV push, the rate of administration is as critical as the dose. If a drug is ordered as "4 mg, with each 1 mg given over 2 minutes," a nurse must calculate both the total volume and the total time (in this case, 8 minutes).
* **Label Literacy:** Students must identify "Expiration" details upon reconstitution. For example, some medications are only stable for 48 hours once mixed; the nurse must label the vial with the exact date and time of expiration.
* **Route Exclusion:** It is a common error to confuse "parenteral" with other non-oral routes. **Sublingual, buccal, and transdermal** routes are NOT parenteral because they do not involve injection into body tissues.
* **Calculation Methods:** Proficiency in multiple methods is expected:
* **Formula Method:** (Desired / Have) × Quantity = x.
* **Dimensional Analysis:** Setting up an equation so that all units cancel out except for the one being solved for (e.g., x mL).
* **Ratio and Proportion:** Ensuring units are in the same position on both sides of the equation (e.g., mg : mL :: mg : mL).
