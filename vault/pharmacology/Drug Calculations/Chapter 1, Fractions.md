# Chapter 1, Fractions

To achieve proficiency in this topic, a student must command both the fundamental mathematical operations and the specific procedural nuances that ensure accuracy. Based on the study material, here are the essential main points and the nuanced skills required for mastery.

### Main Points for the Student

* **Fraction Classification:**
* **Proper Fractions:** The numerator is smaller than the denominator (e.g., 5/6).
* **Improper Fractions:** The numerator is larger than or equal to the denominator (e.g., 3/2 or 9/2).
* **Mixed Numbers:** A combination of a whole number and a fraction (e.g., 2 3/8).
* **Complex Fractions:** Fractions that contain a fraction in the numerator, the denominator, or both.


* **Conversion Procedures:**
* **Mixed Number to Improper Fraction:** Multiply the denominator by the whole number, add the numerator to that product, and place the result over the original denominator.
* **Improper Fraction to Mixed Number:** Divide the numerator by the denominator. The quotient becomes the whole number, and the remainder becomes the new numerator over the original denominator.


* **Finding the Lowest Common Denominator (LCD):**
* The LCD is the smallest whole number that is evenly divisible by every denominator in the problem.


* **Basic Operations:**
* **Addition and Subtraction:** Can only be performed once denominators are identical. Only the numerators are added or subtracted; the denominator remains the same.
* **Multiplication:** Multiply the numerators together and the denominators together. Mixed numbers must be converted to improper fractions first.
* **Division:** Requires inverting the divisor (the second fraction) and then performing multiplication.


* **Reduction to Lowest Terms:** * A fraction is in its lowest terms when the numerator and denominator cannot be divided by any common whole number other than 1. This must be the final step for almost every calculation.

### Nuances for Mastery

* **Generating Equivalent Fractions:** To change a fraction to an equivalent one with a specific LCD, you must divide the LCD by the original denominator and then multiply that quotient by the original numerator. Mastery involves performing this step accurately for multiple fractions simultaneously before attempting addition or subtraction.
* **Complex Fraction Resolution:** To solve a complex fraction, it must be rewritten as a standard division problem. For example, a complex fraction is reduced by treating the main fraction bar as a division symbol, inverting the denominator's fraction, and multiplying.
* **Strategies for Unknown LCDs:** If an LCD is not immediately obvious, master two specific strategies:
1. Multiply the two denominators together.
2. Multiply the largest denominator by 2, 3, or 4 to see if the resulting number is divisible by the other denominators.


* **Advanced Multiplication (Canceling):** To simplify calculations and ensure the result is in the lowest terms, master "canceling" factors between numerators and denominators (both within a single fraction and across different fractions) before performing the final multiplication.
* **Handling Mixed Numbers in Addition:** When adding mixed numbers, the whole numbers and the fractions can be processed separately, but if the fractional sum results in an improper fraction, it must be converted to a mixed number and its whole number added to the total whole number sum.
* **The Subtrahend vs. Minuend:** In subtraction, mastery requires correctly identifying the "subtrahend" (the number being subtracted) and the "minuend" (the number from which another is subtracted) to avoid sign errors.

### Mastery of Test-Taking Logic

* **Keyword Sensitivity:** Be alert for "priority" words in question stems, such as *first, primary, early, best, most, only, always,* and *never*. In clinical or nursing contexts, these words shift the focus from what is "correct" to what is "most appropriate."
* **Recall vs. Recognition:** Do not look at the multiple-choice options immediately. Read the stem, mentally calculate the answer, and then look for the option that matches your result. This prevents being misled by "distractors" (incorrect but plausible-looking answers).
* **Pattern Recognition:** Ignore the frequency of a specific answer choice (e.g., choosing "C" three times in a row). There is typically no pattern to correct answer distributions.
* **Grammatical Clues:** If an answer choice does not grammatically complete the sentence started by the question stem, it is likely a distractor.
