# Chapter 14, Reconstitution of Medications

Based on the detailed performance data and the specific requirements of this topic, here are the essential points and nuances a student must master to be proficient in the reconstitution of medications.

### Main Points for Mastery

* **Interpreting Drug Labels for Reconstitution:** You must be able to locate and extract three critical pieces of information from a medication label:
* The type of diluent required (e.g., sterile water, bacteriostatic water, or normal saline).
* The specific volume of diluent to be added.
* The resulting concentration (dosage strength) once the medication is mixed (e.g., 225 mg/mL or 500,000 units/mL).


* **Mathematical Calculation Methods:** Proficiency in calculating the volume to be administered (mL) based on a physician’s order (mg, mcg, or units) using one of the following methods:
* **Proportion Method (Ratio:Proportion):** Setting up a ratio of known concentration to the unknown volume (e.g., 100 mg : 1 mL :: 300 mg : $x$ mL).
* **Dimensional Analysis:** Setting up an equation that cancels out units to arrive at the desired volume (e.g., $x$ mL = 1 mL / 100 mg × 300 mg / 1).


* **Calculating Expiration Dates and Times:** Mastery of determining how long a medication remains stable after it has been reconstituted. This involves identifying the stability period on the label and adding it to the exact date and time of reconstitution.
* **Managing Multiple Concentration Options:** Some powder medications (like Penicillin G) provide a table with multiple diluent options. You must understand that adding different amounts of diluent will result in different final concentrations and select the correct one for your calculation.
* **Determining Total Vial/Bottle Contents:** Understanding how to calculate the total amount of medication in a bottle and how many full doses are available for the patient (e.g., calculating that a 60 mL bottle of 125 mg/5 mL contains 1500 mg total).

### Nuances to be Mastered

* **Diluent Volume vs. Total Volume:** A critical nuance is that the volume of diluent added is often less than the final total volume of the medication due to "powder displacement" (the powder takes up space). Calculations must always be based on the **final concentration** stated on the label, not the volume of liquid added.
* **Storage-Dependent Stability:** Pay close attention to how storage conditions affect expiration. A medication may be stable for only 24 hours at room temperature but for 7 to 14 days if refrigerated. Master the ability to choose the correct expiration date based on where the drug is stored.
* **IM Injection Volume Limits:** In clinical practice, the volume of an intramuscular (IM) injection is limited (usually to 3 mL or less depending on the site). If a reconstitution calculation results in a volume that is too large, you must master selecting a different diluent amount from the label to create a more concentrated (smaller volume) dose.
* **Specific Rounding Rules:** * For volumes greater than 1 mL, typically round to the nearest **tenth** (0.1 mL).
* For volumes less than 1 mL, calculations often require rounding to the nearest **hundredth** (0.01 mL).
* Always verify if the specific problem requires rounding at the final step (e.g., 4.25 mL rounding to 4.3 mL).


* **Unit Conversions within Reconstitution:** You must master converting units before starting the reconstitution formula, such as:
* Grams to Milligrams (1 g = 1000 mg).
* Ounces to Milliliters (1 oz = 30 mL).
* Milligrams to Micrograms (1 mg = 1000 mcg).


* **Labeling Requirements:** Mastering the "nurse's responsibility" to physically write the date of reconstitution, the time, and the expiration date/time on the vial immediately after mixing to ensure patient safety and drug potency.
