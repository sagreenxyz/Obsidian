# Chapter 13, Dosages Measured in Units

To master the administration of medications measured in units, a student must understand both the broad procedural requirements and the precise nuances that ensure patient safety.

### Main Points of Mastery

* **Insulin Syringe Exclusivity:** Insulin syringes are calibrated specifically for insulin and must never be used to administer other medications, such as heparin, even if those medications are also measured in units. Conversely, standard milliliter (mL) or tuberculin syringes should never be used to administer insulin, as they cannot accurately measure unit doses.
* **Available Syringe Sizes:** Standard insulin syringes are supplied in 30-unit, 50-unit, and 100-unit capacities. The 30-unit and 50-unit versions are often referred to as "Lo-Dose" syringes.
* **The "Clear Before Cloudy" Rule:** When mixing two types of insulin in one syringe, the clear (Regular/Short-acting) insulin must always be drawn into the syringe before the cloudy (NPH/Intermediate-acting) insulin to prevent contaminating the clear vial with the cloudy suspension.
* **High-Alert Status:** Insulin is a "high-alert" medication. This means it carries an increased risk of causing significant patient harm if used in error. Nurses must prioritize accuracy and often require a second-person witness for dose verification.
* **Unit-Based Calculations:** Medications like Heparin, Dalteparin, Epoetin, and Penicillin V are often ordered in units or International Units (IU) but administered in milliliters. Students must be proficient in dimensional analysis or the ratio-proportion method to convert these units into volume.
* **Insulin Pens:** These devices are favored for their portability and ease of use. They eliminate the need for vials and syringes for every dose; the user simply dials the prescribed number of units on the pen.

### Critical Nuances for Clinical Success

* **Air Injection Sequence:** Before drawing up mixed insulin, air equal to the dose must be injected into both vials to maintain pressure. The nuance is the order: inject air into the cloudy (NPH) vial first, then inject air into the clear (Regular) vial. The syringe should remain in the clear vial to immediately withdraw the medication.
* **Syringe Selection Strategy:** To ensure the highest accuracy, use the smallest syringe that can accommodate the dose. For example, a 31-unit dose should be prepared in a 50-unit syringe rather than a 100-unit syringe.
* **Calibration Recognition:** On a standard 100-unit U-100 syringe, each calibration line represents 2 units. In contrast, smaller syringes (like the 30-unit or 50-unit) may have lines representing 1 unit. Mistaking these increments is a common source of error.
* **Concentration Awareness:** Most insulin is U-100 (100 units per mL), but U-500 (500 units per mL) exists for patients with high insulin resistance. A U-100 syringe must never be used to measure U-500 insulin; a specific U-500 syringe is required.
* **Understanding Pre-Mixed Ratios:** * **Novolin 70/30** contains 70% NPH (Intermediate) and 30% Regular (Short-acting).
* **Humalog Mix 75/25** contains 75% lispro protamine (Intermediate) and 25% lispro (Rapid-acting).


* **Error Management:** If a medication error occurs (e.g., a dose higher than ordered is given), the absolute first priority is to **assess the patient** for life-threatening conditions like hypoglycemia. Reporting to a supervisor or provider is the secondary step.
* **Specific Monitoring:** For certain unit-based medications, specific laboratory values must be watched. For epoetin (used for anemia), the nurse must monitor hemoglobin levels and blood pressure.
* **Rounding in Calculations:** When calculating mL volume for unit-based meds, follow specific rounding instructions. Generally, volumes less than 1 mL are rounded to the hundredth (e.g., 0.75 mL), while volumes greater than 1 mL are often rounded to the tenth (e.g., 1.6 mL). Always include a leading zero for decimals less than one (e.g., 0.5 mL, not .5 mL).
* **Abbreviation Nuance:** The abbreviation "AU" in orders refers to "both ears" (each ear). Mastering these Latin-derived abbreviations is essential to preventing errors in medication route.
