# Chapter 4, Ratios

Based on the performance details and rationales provided, here are the main points and nuances a student must master regarding ratios.

### Main Points for Mastery

**1. Converting Percentages to Ratios**

* **The Process:** To change a percentage to a ratio, you must first express the percentage as a proper fraction reduced to its lowest terms.
* **Formatting:** The numerator of that fraction becomes the first number of the ratio, followed by a colon, and the denominator becomes the second number.
* **Example:** 50% becomes 1/2, which is expressed as the ratio 1:2.

**2. Converting Ratios to Decimals**

* **The Process:** This involves dividing the first number of the ratio by the second number.
* **Method:** Long division is the standard approach for this conversion.
* **Example:** For the ratio 7:28, you divide 7 by 28 to get 0.25.

**3. Converting Ratios to Percentages**

* **The Process:** First, express the ratio as either a proper fraction or a decimal fraction. Then, multiply that value by 100 and add the percentage sign (%).
* **Example:** 12:48 is 0.25; multiplying by 100 results in 25%.

**4. Converting Ratios to Proper Fractions**

* **The Process:** Write the first number of the ratio as the numerator and the second number as the denominator.
* **Critical Step:** The fraction must always be reduced to its lowest terms to be considered the correct expression.
* **Example:** 8:32 becomes 8/32, which reduces to 1/4.

**5. Converting Decimals to Ratios**

* **The Process:** Convert the decimal into a proper fraction and reduce it to its lowest terms.
* **Formatting:** The numerator is the first number of the ratio, and the denominator is the second.
* **Example:** 0.65 becomes 65/100, which reduces to 13/20, resulting in a 13:20 ratio.

**6. Ratio Conversions from Complex Fractions**

* **The Process:** When presented with a division of two fractions (e.g., 25/1000 ÷ 5/100), you must perform the division first—often by cross-multiplying the numerators and denominators.
* **Final Step:** Once you have the resulting fraction, reduce it to lowest terms before writing it as a ratio.

### Nuances and Mastering the Topic

To achieve proficiency, a student must pay close attention to the following nuances:

* **Reduction is Mandatory:** Many incorrect options in ratio problems are simply the unreduced version of the correct answer (e.g., 8:32 vs. 1:4). Mastery requires the habit of always checking if a fraction or ratio can be further divided by a common denominator.
* **The Significance of "Lowest Terms":** The rationales repeatedly emphasize that "reduced to lowest terms" is a requirement for the final answer. Failing to do this is a common pitfall.
* **Precision in Long Division:** When converting ratios to decimals, precision in long division is vital, especially when dealing with three decimal places (e.g., converting 4:32 to 0.125).
* **Understanding Place Value:** When converting decimals like 0.024 or 0.025 to ratios, you must correctly identify the fractional equivalent (24/1000 or 25/1000) before reducing.

Mastery of the content is supported by specific test-taking behaviors highlighted in the material:

* **Component Identification:** If a question is complex, break down the options into components to identify which parts are definitely wrong.
* **Ignore Patterns:** Do not worry if you choose the same letter or number (e.g., "C" or option 3) several times in a row. There is usually no intentional pattern to the answers.
* **Educated Guessing:** Never leave a question blank. Eliminate the answers you know are wrong to increase your odds, then make the best choice possible.
