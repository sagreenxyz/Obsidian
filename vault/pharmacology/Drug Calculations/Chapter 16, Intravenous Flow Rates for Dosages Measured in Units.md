# Chapter 16, Intravenous Flow Rates for Dosages Measured in Units

To master the calculation of intravenous flow rates for medications measured in units (such as Heparin, Insulin, and Vasopressin), a student must be proficient in several core mathematical concepts and clinical nuances.

### Main Points for Mastery

* **Dimensional Analysis:** This is the primary method for calculating flow rates. You must be able to convert between different units of measurement (e.g., units/hr to mL/hr or units/kg/hr to mL/hr) by setting up equations where unwanted units cancel out.
* **Weight-Based Calculations:** Many high-alert medications are dosed based on the patient's weight.
* **Conversion:** You must always convert the patient's weight from pounds (lb) to kilograms (kg) using the standard conversion factor: **1 kg = 2.2 lb**.
* **Dosage Determination:** Multiply the prescribed dose (e.g., 0.1 units/kg/hr) by the patient’s weight in kg to find the hourly unit requirement before converting to a milliliter-per-hour rate.


* **Concentration Ratios:** You must identify the "available" concentration provided by the pharmacy (e.g., 25,000 units in 250 mL). This ratio is essential for determining how many milliliters are needed to deliver a specific number of units.
* **Calculating Infusion Pump Rates (mL/hr):** Electronic infusion pumps are programmed in mL/hr. The student must consistently be able to solve for $x$ mL/hr given a specific unit-per-hour order and a known concentration.
* **Calculating Units Received (Units/hr):** Conversely, a student must be able to look at a pump currently running at a specific mL/hr and determine exactly how many units per hour the patient is receiving based on the bag's concentration.
* **Bolus Dosing:** For medications like Heparin, an initial "bolus" (a one-time loading dose) is often required. This is calculated as units/kg and is separate from the continuous maintenance drip.

### Nuances and Critical Details

* **Rounding Rules:** * **Weight:** Weights in kilograms are often rounded to the nearest tenth.
* **Final Flow Rates:** mL/hr rates are typically rounded to the nearest tenth (e.g., 11.25 becomes 11.3), though some instructions may specify rounding to a whole number.
* **Bolus Doses:** Heparin boluses are frequently rounded to the nearest 100 units for clinical safety.


* **Protocol Adjustments (Titration):** Mastering this topic requires knowing how to adjust a rate based on laboratory results (like PTT for Heparin or blood glucose for Insulin).
* **Incremental Changes:** You must distinguish between "increasing the rate **to**" a certain amount versus "increasing/decreasing the rate **by**" a certain amount.
* **Mathematical Recalculation:** When a protocol dictates an adjustment (e.g., "decrease drip by 2 units/kg/hr"), you must subtract that from the current weight-based dose and then perform the full conversion back to mL/hr.


* **Unit Conversions (Liters to Milliliters):** Occasionally, medication concentrations or unit-to-volume ratios may be provided in liters (e.g., 1 unit = 0.0025 L). You must remember to convert liters to milliliters (1 L = 1000 mL) to program the pump correctly.
* **High-Alert Medication Safety:** Heparin and Insulin are "high-alert" medications. A single decimal point error (e.g., 1.5 mL/hr vs. 15 mL/hr) can be fatal. Precision in calculation and careful reading of the question "stem" (the specific data provided) is the most critical non-mathematical skill.
* **Grammatical Fit:** Always reread the final answer within the context of the question. If the question asks for "units/hr" and you provide "mL/hr," the answer is clinically incorrect regardless of the math.
