# Chapter 8, Safety in Medication Administration

To achieve proficiency in the safety of medication administration, a student must master both the fundamental protocols and the specific technical nuances that prevent errors. Below are the comprehensive main points and critical nuances derived from the study material.

### Main Points for Medication Safety

* **Factors Contributing to Medication Errors:**
* **Look-alike/Sound-alike (LASA) Medications:** Confusing drugs with similar names.
* **Communication Failures:** Use of inappropriate or "error-prone" abbreviations.
* **Numerical Mismanagement:** Misuse of leading/trailing zeros and decimal points.
* **Calculation Errors:** Mathematical mistakes in dosage or infusion rates.
* **Environmental Factors:** Fast-paced units, low staffing, sicker/older patient populations, and technology that demands excessive nurse attention.


* **The Rights of Medication Administration:**
* The core "rights" (Patient, Drug, Dose, Route, Time) must be verified.
* **Technology Assistance:** Scanning a client’s identification band and medication packets is the primary technological method for verifying these rights. Automated dispensing systems and IV pumps do not replace the nurse's responsibility to verify these rights manually.


* **Priority Actions Following an Error:**
* **Client Assessment First:** The absolute priority after identifying a medication error is to assess the client for side effects or adverse reactions.
* **Reporting:** Only after the client is stable and assessed should the nurse notify the supervisor and health care provider. An incident report is required but is never the first priority.


* **Patient Identification Protocols:**
* Always use at least two identifiers (e.g., name and date of birth).
* For confused patients, the most accurate methods are reviewing the ID band and asking a family member. Never rely on roommates, personal belongings, or other staff members for identification.


* **Automated Medication-Dispensing Systems:**
* **Benefits:** They reduce errors by linking to the pharmacy, providing both generic and trade names, and ensuring the nurse only accesses medications for a specific patient.
* **Limitations:** These systems do **not** eliminate the need for the nurse to perform dosage calculations.


* **Special Populations and Home Care:**
* **Pediatrics:** Weight is the priority for ensuring proper dosage (mg/kg).
* **Geriatrics/Home Care:** Priority is providing detailed, large-print medication directions.
* **Omission Prevention:** Pill organizers are the best tool for clients who forget doses. If the barrier is financial (expensive meds), a referral to a social worker or case manager is the priority intervention.



### Nuances to be Mastered

* **The "Zero" Rules:**
* **Leading Zeros:** Always use a zero before a decimal (e.g., **0.2 mg**, not .2 mg) to prevent it from being read as a whole number.
* **Trailing Zeros:** Never use a zero after a decimal (e.g., **2 mg**, not 2.0 mg). A decimal point can be easily missed, turning 2.0 into 20.


* **Tall Man Lettering:**
* Specific portions of look-alike drug names are capitalized to highlight differences (e.g., **DOP**amine vs. **DOBUT**amine). This system is mandated by the FDA to reduce confusion.


* **Error-Prone Abbreviations (The "Do Not Use" List):**
* **MSO4 and MS:** Can be confused for Morphine Sulfate or Magnesium Sulfate. These must be written out in full.
* **MgSO4:** Must be written as "Magnesium Sulfate."
* **hs:** Can be mistaken for "half-strength." Use "at bedtime."
* **po:** While generally accepted, clear communication is always preferred to avoid any ambiguity.


* **IV Pump Safeguards and Troubleshooting:**
* If a "Smart Pump" issues an alert (e.g., "Dose exceeds recommended limit"), the first action is to **reprogram the pump from step one** to ensure no data entry error occurred. Only after verifying the programming should the nurse contact the prescriber. Never bypass safeguard parameters.


* **Navigating Automated Dispensing Risks:**
* **Drawer Safety:** Many systems use open compartments. A nurse must still perform the "three checks" of medication administration because the pharmacy may have stocked the wrong medication in an open drawer.
* **Emergency Overrides:** Overriding the system removes the safety layer of pharmacy verification and significantly increases the risk of error.


* **Clinical Judgment and Refusals:**
* If a patient refuses a medication because it "looks different," the nurse's first action is to **re-verify the rights of administration** and the order. Only after verification and patient education should a refusal be documented.
* **Surgical Nuance:** If a diabetic patient is NPO (nothing by mouth) for surgery, a nurse should never independently adjust the insulin dose. They must contact the prescriber for specific instructions.


* **Hazardous Materials:**
* If a hazardous medication is spilled, personal protection and following facility policy for cleaning (not just using paper towels) is the first priority to prevent exposure.


* **Legal Liability:**
* The nurse who administers the medication is legally liable for any error, regardless of whether the error originated with the prescriber or the pharmacist. The "last check" is the nurse's responsibility.
