# Chapter 11, Oral Dosages

To be proficient in oral dosage calculations, a student must master the mathematical formulas, unit conversions, and clinical application details found within this topic. Below are the comprehensive main points and nuanced details required for mastery.

### Main Points for Mastery

**1. The Formula Method (D/A × Q = x)**

* **Desired (D):** The dose prescribed by the healthcare provider (e.g., 250 mg).
* **Available (A):** The dosage strength available on the medication label (e.g., 500 mg).
* **Quantity (Q):** The unit of measure that contains the available dose (e.g., 1 tablet, 5 mL, or 1 capsule).
* **Result (x):** The actual amount to be administered to the patient.

**2. Dimensional Analysis**

* Students must be able to solve dosage problems without relying solely on formulas by setting up equations where units cancel out. This is particularly useful for multi-step problems involving weight-based dosing or multiple unit conversions.

**3. Essential Unit Conversions**
Calculations cannot be performed until the "Desired" and "Available" units are identical. Essential conversions include:

* **Weight (Metric):** 1 gram (g) = 1,000 milligrams (mg); 1 milligram (mg) = 1,000 micrograms (mcg).
* **Weight (Body):** 1 kilogram (kg) = 2.2 pounds (lb).
* **Volume:** 1 ounce (oz) = 30 milliliters (mL).

**4. Liquid Medication Concentrations**

* Calculating volume for liquids requires identifying the concentration (e.g., 125 mg per 5 mL).
* Students must calculate the total volume for a single dose based on the mg/mL ratio provided by the pharmacy.

**5. Weight-Based Pediatric and Adult Dosing**

* Step 1: Convert the patient’s weight from pounds to kilograms (divide by 2.2).
* Step 2: Calculate the total daily dose (mg × kg).
* Step 3: Divide the total daily dose by the number of doses scheduled per day (e.g., "twice daily" or "every 6 hours") to find the amount per single dose.

**6. Understanding Medication Forms**

* **Tablets:** Scored tablets can be split (e.g., 0.5 or 1.5 tablets), while non-scored tablets generally should not be.
* **Capsules:** These are administered as whole units.
* **Troches (Lozenges):** Must be allowed to dissolve in the mouth and are not intended for immediate ingestion.
* **Enteric-Coated:** Designed to dissolve in the small intestine, not the stomach; these must never be crushed.
* **Suspensions:** Liquid forms containing undissolved particles that must be shaken gently before administration to ensure equal distribution.

### Nuances and Critical Details

**1. Leading Zeros and Decimals**

* **The Leading Zero Rule:** For any dose less than a whole number, a leading zero must be used (e.g., 0.5 mL, not .5 mL) to prevent life-threatening medication errors.
* **Rounding Rules:** Students must follow specific rounding instructions. Typically, liquid doses are rounded to the nearest tenth (0.1), while total daily volumes or specific whole-unit questions may require rounding to the nearest whole number.

**2. "Per Dose" vs. "Per Day"**

* A common pitfall is failing to distinguish between the total amount of medication a patient receives in 24 hours versus the amount they receive in a single administration. If a medication is 40 mEq daily in two divided doses, the student must divide the final calculation by 2.

**3. PRN and STAT Dose Integration**

* Calculations often involve a "STAT" (immediate) dose followed by "PRN" (as needed) doses. Mastery requires tracking the cumulative total administered over a shift or a 24-hour period to ensure it does not exceed the "maximum dose" ordered by the provider.

**4. Weight-Change Triggers**

* Some orders are conditional based on weight change (e.g., "administer an extra dose for weight gain exceeding 2 lb"). Students must be able to convert kg to lb to determine if the clinical trigger for the extra dose has been met.

**5. Reconstitution Logic**

* For powdered medications, the student must look for the "reconstituted concentration" (e.g., "once reconstituted, 5 mL contains 125 mg") rather than the total weight of the powder in the bottle.

**6. Ratio and Proportion Setup**

* When using ratio and proportion, the "known" ratio (what the pharmacy provides) must be on one side of the equation, and the "unknown" (what you need to give) must be on the other (e.g., 80 mg / 7.5 mL = 240 mg / x mL). Always ensure the numerators have the same units and the denominators have the same units.
