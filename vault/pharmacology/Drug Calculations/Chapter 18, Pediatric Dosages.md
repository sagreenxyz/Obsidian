# Chapter 18, Pediatric Dosages

Based on the performance details and rationales provided, here are the essential concepts and nuances a student must master regarding pediatric medication and fluid administration.

### Main Points for Mastery

* **Weight-Based Calculations:** The cornerstone of pediatric dosing is the patient's weight in kilograms. You must be proficient in converting pounds (lb) to kilograms (kg) using the standard conversion factor: **1 kg = 2.2 lb**.
* **Body Surface Area (BSA):** For high-risk medications (like chemotherapy), dosages are calculated based on BSA in square meters ($m^2$). The standard formula involves multiplying the height in centimeters by the weight in kilograms, dividing the product by 3600, and taking the square root of the result.
* **24-Hour Maintenance Fluid Requirements:** Fluids for pediatric patients are calculated using a specific weight-based scale:
* **0–10 kg:** 100 mL/kg
* **10–20 kg:** 1000 mL + 50 mL/kg for every kg over 10 kg
* **>20 kg:** 1500 mL + 20 mL/kg for every kg over 20 kg


* **IV Flow Rate Calculations:**
* **mL/hr:** Total volume divided by the total time in hours.
* **gtt/min (Drip Rate):** (Total volume in mL × Drop factor) / Time in minutes.


* **Safe Dose Range Verification:** Before administration, a nurse must calculate the minimum and maximum safe doses based on the manufacturer’s or provider’s recommended range (e.g., 10–15 mg/kg) and compare the prescribed dose to this range.

### Nuances and Critical Thinking

* **Rounding Protocols:**
* Weights in kilograms are typically rounded to the nearest tenth.
* Final doses in milliliters (mL) or milligrams (mg) are often rounded to the nearest tenth or hundredth depending on the equipment used (e.g., tuberculin vs. standard syringe).
* BSA results are usually rounded to the nearest hundredth or tenth as specified.
* IV drip rates (gtt/min) must be rounded to the nearest whole number, as you cannot count a fraction of a drop.


* **Height and Weight Unit Alignment:** When calculating BSA, if the height is provided in inches, it must be converted to centimeters (1 inch = 2.5 cm). If the weight is provided in pounds and you are using the kg/cm formula, the weight must be converted to kilograms first.
* **Dose Frequency vs. Total Daily Dose:** Mastery requires distinguishing between a "per dose" amount and a "per day" amount. If a medication is ordered "mg/kg/day divided every 8 hours," the total daily milligrams must be divided by three to find the amount for a single administration.
* **Action on Unsafe Orders:** A critical nursing nuance is knowing when *not* to administer a drug. If a prescribed dose falls outside the calculated safe therapeutic range (either too high or too low), the nurse must hold the medication and contact the healthcare provider for clarification. You cannot modify the dose yourself to bring it into the safe range.
* **Unit Conversion Awareness:** Always pay close attention to the units requested (e.g., mcg vs. mg). If a dose is calculated in micrograms but the medication is labeled in milligrams, you must convert (1 mg = 1000 mcg) to ensure accuracy.
* **Preadministration Concentration Math:** Even after calculating the required milligrams, you must perform a final calculation to determine the volume (mL) to be drawn up based on the available concentration (mg/mL) on the medication vial or bottle.
