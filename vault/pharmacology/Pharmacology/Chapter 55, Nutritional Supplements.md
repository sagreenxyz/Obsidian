# Chapter 55, Nutritional Supplements

To master the topic of nutritional supplements, a student must understand the distinctions between enteral and parenteral nutrition, the specific types of formulations available, and the critical nursing interventions required to prevent life-threatening complications.

### 1. Enteral Nutrition

Enteral nutrition involves providing nutrients through the gastrointestinal (GI) tract. It is preferred for patients with at least a partially functioning GI system.

**Main Points to Know:**

* **Feeding Routes:** * **Nasogastric (NG):** From the nose to the stomach.
* **Nasojejunal:** From the nose to the jejunum.
* **Gastrostomy (G-tube):** Surgically inserted directly into the stomach.
* **Jejunostomy (J-tube):** Surgically inserted into the jejunum.


* **Formulation Types:**
* **Polymeric:** Closely resembles "normal" dietary intake; contains complex nutrients (proteins, carbs, fats). Preferred for patients with fully functional GI tracts.
* **Elemental:** Contains dipeptides or crystalline amino acids; used for patients with impaired GI function (e.g., malabsorption).
* **Modular:** Provides a single nutrient (e.g., Microlipid for fat, Moducal for carbs).
* **Altered Amino Acid:** Tailored for metabolic disorders like phenylketonuria (PKU).


* **Complications:** Diarrhea is the most common adverse effect. Serious complications include aspiration pneumonia (indicated by shortness of breath or adventitious lung sounds) and dumping syndrome (caused by rapid bolus feedings).

**Nuances to Master:**

* **Placement Verification:** For small-bore tubes, an X-ray is essential before the first feeding. Subsequent checks should use gastric aspirate pH (a pH ≤ 5.5 indicates correct stomach placement). Auscultation by pushing air is no longer considered reliable.
* **Residual Management:** Gastric residuals should be checked before each feeding. If the residual is more than 50% of the previous feeding volume, the feeding must be held and the provider notified.
* **Drug Interactions:** Enteral feedings can significantly reduce the absorption of phenytoin. Feedings should be held for 2 hours before and after administration.
* **Positioning:** Always elevate the head of the bed to at least 45 degrees during and for 30 minutes after feeding to prevent aspiration.

### 2. Parenteral Nutrition (PN)

Parenteral nutrition involves administering nutrients intravenously, bypassing the GI tract entirely.

**Main Points to Know:**

* **Peripheral Parenteral Nutrition (PPN):**
* Used for short-term support (usually <14 days).
* Maximum calorie limit is 2,000 kcal/day.
* Dextrose concentration must be ≤ 10% to prevent vein damage.


* **Central Parenteral Nutrition (CPN/TPN):**
* Used for long-term support (>7–10 days) or high caloric needs (>2,000 kcal/day).
* Administered via the subclavian or internal jugular vein.
* Can accommodate higher dextrose concentrations (25%–50%).


* **Nutritional Components:**
* **Dextrose:** The primary calorie source (3.4 kcal/g).
* **Amino Acids:** Essential for protein synthesis (0.9 g/kg recommended for healthy adults).
* **Lipids:** Provide essential fatty acids and high calories (9 kcal/g).



**Nuances to Master:**

* **Phlebitis:** This is the most devastating complication of PPN. If a red line or warmth appears at the site, the nurse must immediately stop the infusion and remove the catheter.
* **Metabolic Monitoring:** Hyperglycemia is a frequent risk due to high dextrose content. Symptoms include headache, polyuria, and polydipsia. Rebound hypoglycemia can occur if the infusion is stopped abruptly.
* **Lipid Intolerance:** Specific adverse reactions to lipid emulsions include cough, fever, chest pain, and dyspnea.
* **Sterile Technique:** Because of the high glucose content, TPN lines are high-risk for infection and sepsis. Strict sterile asepsis is required during dressing and tubing changes.

### 3. Essential Calculations and Nutrient Facts

* **Caloric Values:** * Fat: 9 kcal/gram.
* Protein: 4 kcal/gram.
* Dextrose: 3.4 kcal/gram.


* **Concentrations:** A 10% dextrose solution contains 10 grams of dextrose per 100 mL of fluid. Therefore, a 500 mL bag provides 50 grams of dextrose (50g x 3.4 kcal/g = 170 kcal).
* **Fatty Acid Deficiency:** Signs include hair loss, scaly dermatitis, and delayed wound healing.

### 4. Patient Assessment and Allergies

* **Allergy Cross-Sensitivity:** Patients allergic to eggs may have a cross-sensitivity to lipid formulations. Those allergic to whey or egg whites should avoid certain protein modular formulas.
* **Oral Assessment:** Before starting oral supplements, the priority is assessing the patient's gag reflex and ability to swallow.
* **Therapeutic Outcomes:** Success is measured by weight gain, improved skin turgor, increased total protein levels, and adequate urinary output (>30 mL/hr).
