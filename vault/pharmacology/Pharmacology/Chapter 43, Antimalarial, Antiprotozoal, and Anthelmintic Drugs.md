# Chapter 43, Antimalarial, Antiprotozoal, and Anthelmintic Drugs

To master the pharmacology of antimalarial, antiprotozoal, and anthelmintic medications, a student must understand the life cycles of the parasites, specific drug mechanisms, and the critical safety profiles associated with these treatments.

### Antimalarial Drugs

**Main Points:**

* **Transmission and Life Cycle:** Malaria is caused by the *Plasmodium* parasite and is transmitted via infected mosquitoes, contaminated needles, blood transfusions, or from mother to fetus via the placenta. It is not spread through physical contact.
* **Treatment Phases:**
* **Exoerythrocytic Phase:** This occurs outside the red blood cells. Primaquine is the primary drug effective during this phase and is used to provide curative therapy for *P. vivax* and *P. ovale*.
* **Erythrocytic Phase:** This is the blood phase. Drugs like chloroquine, hydroxychloroquine, mefloquine, and pyrimethamine work during this stage.


* **Drug Resistance:** Resistance is a significant challenge, particularly with *Plasmodium falciparum*. Quinine or combinations like pyrimethamine and sulfadoxine are used for highly resistant strains.
* **Prophylaxis:** Travelers to endemic areas often take atovaquone/proguanil or mefloquine. Chloroquine-resistant areas require specific prophylactic regimens like pyrimethamine.

**Nuances to Master:**

* **G6PD Deficiency:** Primaquine and pyrimethamine are strictly contraindicated in patients with glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency as they can trigger life-threatening hemolytic anemia.
* **Cardiovascular Effects:** Quinine decreases the excitability of cardiac and skeletal muscles, necessitating caution in patients with heart conditions. Chloroquine can increase digoxin levels, leading to potential toxicity.
* **Specific Contraindications:** Chloroquine and hydroxychloroquine are contraindicated in patients with optic neuritis or psoriasis. Primaquine is contraindicated in patients with rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, or agranulocytosis.
* **Side Effects and Adherence:** Chloroquine can cause personality changes and seizures. Primaquine causes a harmless but potentially alarming dark discoloration of the urine. Quinine causes photosensitivity and visual disturbances.

### Antiprotozoal Drugs (Non-malarial)

**Main Points:**

* **Metronidazole:** A versatile drug used for bacterial, protozoal (giardiasis, amebiasis), and helminthic infections. Key side effects include a metallic taste, neutropenia, and peripheral neuropathy.
* **Pentamidine:** Primarily used for *Pneumocystis jirovecii* pneumonia, common in AIDS patients. It can cause significant hypotension, nephrotoxicity, and pancreatitis.
* **Atovaquone:** Used for *Pneumocystis* infections. It is known to cause metabolic disturbances like hyperkalemia, hyponatremia, and hypoglycemia.
* **Iodoquinol:** Used for intestinal amebiasis. It can cause systemic toxicity affecting the nerves and vision.

**Nuances to Master:**

* **The Disulfiram-Like Reaction:** Metronidazole must never be taken with alcohol. The interaction causes tachycardia, hypotension, nausea, and diaphoresis.
* **Warfarin Interaction:** Metronidazole enhances the effect of warfarin, significantly increasing the risk of bleeding.
* **Glucose and Electrolyte Monitoring:** Pentamidine causes initial hypoglycemia followed by hyperglycemia. Atovaquone requires baseline blood glucose and white blood cell count monitoring to check for leukopenia.
* **Nephrotoxicity Synergy:** When pentamidine is used with other nephrotoxic drugs like vancomycin, the risk of kidney failure increases exponentially.

### Anthelmintic Drugs

**Main Points:**

* **Common Helminthic Infections:**
* **Ascariasis:** Giant roundworms (*Ascaris lumbricoides*), usually found in the small intestine.
* **Enterobiasis:** Pinworms (*Enterobius vermicularis*), residing in the large intestine.
* **Taeniasis:** Beef tapeworms, often contracted from undercooked beef.


* **Specific Medications:**
* **Pyrantel:** Indicated for roundworms and pinworms.
* **Praziquantel:** The drug of choice for fluke infections (schistosomiasis).
* **Albendazole:** A broad-spectrum agent effective against cestodes, nematodes, and trematodes.



**Nuances to Master:**

* **Drug Interactions:**
* **Cimetidine:** Increases serum levels of praziquantel, which can lead to severe headaches and pyrexia.
* **Dexamethasone/Praziquantel:** Both can increase the plasma levels of albendazole.
* **Theophylline:** Pyrantel can increase theophylline levels in pediatric patients, risking toxicity.


* **Contraindications:** Pyrantel should be avoided in patients with liver disease due to potential toxicity. Praziquantel is contraindicated in ocular cysticercosis (tapeworm infection of the eye).
* **Adverse Effects:** Praziquantel frequently causes drowsiness. Pyrantel is associated with insomnia and GI distress.

### General Nursing Considerations

* **Baseline Assessments:** Always perform a visual acuity test for chloroquine, bladder function assessment for metronidazole, and liver function tests for pyrantel.
* **Administration:** Instruct patients to take these medications with at least 250 mL (8 oz) of water to mitigate renal and hepatic strain.
* **Sun Safety:** Patients on quinine must use sunscreen and protective clothing due to high photosensitivity.
