# Chapter 28, Diuretic Drugs

### Main Points for Mastery

**1. Mechanism of Action and Potency**

* **Basic Function:** Diuretics primarily work by blocking the reabsorption of sodium and chloride, which in turn prevents the passive reabsorption of water.
* **Site of Action:** The potency of a diuretic is determined by its site of action within the nephron. Drugs acting where the most sodium is reabsorbed (like the proximal convoluted tubule or the loop of Henle) are more potent than those acting in the distal tubule.
* **Physiologic Effects:** Successful administration leads to decreased plasma volume, decreased extracellular fluid volume, decreased cardiac output, and decreased blood pressure.

**2. Major Diuretic Classes and Indications**

* **Loop Diuretics (e.g., Furosemide, Bumetanide):** These are potent and rapid-acting. They are the drug of choice for acute pulmonary edema and edema associated with heart failure, liver disease, or nephrotic syndrome.
* **Thiazide and Thiazide-like Diuretics (e.g., Hydrochlorothiazide, Metolazone, Chlorthalidone):** These are often first-line therapy for hypertension and are used for various types of edema.
* **Potassium-Sparing Diuretics (e.g., Spironolactone, Triamterene, Amiloride):** These are relatively weak and are often used in combination with other diuretics to prevent potassium loss. Spironolactone specifically acts as an aldosterone antagonist.
* **Osmotic Diuretics (e.g., Mannitol):** Primarily used to reduce intracranial pressure (cerebral edema) and to promote the excretion of toxic substances.
* **Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors (CAIs) (e.g., Acetazolamide):** Used for open-angle glaucoma (to reduce intraocular pressure), high-altitude sickness (relieving headache and nausea), and edema resistant to other drugs.

**3. Electrolyte and Metabolic Impact**

* **Potassium Imbalance:** Loop and thiazide diuretics typically cause hypokalemia (low potassium). Conversely, potassium-sparing diuretics put patients at risk for hyperkalemia (high potassium).
* **Glucose and Uric Acid:** Thiazides can lead to hyperglycemia (elevated blood glucose) and hyperuricemia, making them a poor choice for patients with gout.
* **Acid-Base Balance:** CAIs like acetazolamide cause the excretion of bicarbonate, which can lead to metabolic acidosis.

**4. Clinical Administration and Monitoring**

* **Timing:** Diuretics should be taken in the morning to prevent nocturia (nighttime urination) and subsequent sleep disruption.
* **Safety Assessments:** Nurses must monitor intake and output (I&O), daily weights, blood pressure, and lung sounds to evaluate effectiveness.
* **Renal Function:** Monitoring serum creatinine and creatinine clearance (BUN/Cr) is vital, as kidney health dictates drug choice and dosage.

### Nuances to Master

**1. Renal Function Thresholds**

* **The 25-50 mL/min Rule:** Thiazide diuretics (like HCTZ) are generally ineffective when creatinine clearance is less than 30 to 50 mL/min. However, **metolazone** is a unique thiazide-like diuretic that remains effective down to a creatinine clearance of 10 mL/min.
* **Loop Diuretics in Renal Failure:** Loop diuretics like furosemide are safe and effective even in patients with severely impaired renal function (creatinine clearance less than 25 mL/min).

**2. Critical Drug Interactions**

* **NSAIDs:** Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (like ibuprofen) can inhibit renal prostaglandins, thereby decreasing the diuretic and antihypertensive activity of drugs like furosemide.
* **Lithium:** Most diuretics can decrease the renal excretion of lithium, leading to lithium toxicity.
* **Digoxin:** Because loop and thiazide diuretics cause hypokalemia, they significantly increase the risk of digoxin toxicity.
* **Cyclosporine:** Acetazolamide can interact with cyclosporine, increasing its serum concentration and requiring a dose reduction of the immunosuppressant.

**3. Specific Contraindications and Risks**

* **Mannitol Precautions:** Mannitol is contraindicated in patients with anuria (no urine output) or active pulmonary edema (it can worsen the latter). It is also prone to crystallization at low temperatures; vials must be stored in a warmer and checked for precipitants. A filter must be used for administration.
* **Sulfa Allergies:** There is a potential cross-sensitivity between sulfonamide antibiotics and loop diuretics/thiazides. While they are often used with monitoring, they are technically listed as contraindicated or requiring high caution.
* **Hepatic Failure:** Furosemide and triamterene are metabolized in the liver and may be contraindicated in severe hepatic failure due to drug accumulation risks. Spironolactone is often preferred in these cases.

**4. Patient Teaching and Symptom Recognition**

* **Hypokalemia Signs:** Patients should be taught to recognize anorexia, nausea, lethargy, muscle weakness, and hypotension.
* **Dietary Nuances:** Patients on loop/thiazides need high-potassium foods (citrus, bananas, potatoes, tomatoes), whereas those on potassium-sparing drugs must avoid these and avoid salt substitutes (which often contain potassium).
* **Photosensitivity:** Chlorthalidone can cause photosensitivity, requiring patients to use sun protection.
* **Hypotension Assessment:** Patients should be taught to report dizziness and tingling of the fingers, which are early signs of hypotensive episodes.
