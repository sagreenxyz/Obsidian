# Chapter 24, Heart Failure Drugs

To master the pharmacology of heart failure (HF), a student must understand the mechanisms of various drug classes, their specific nursing implications, and the complex web of interactions that can lead to toxicity or treatment failure.

### Core Knowledge Points

**1. Primary Drug Classes and Mechanisms**

* **Cardiac Glycosides (Digoxin):** Acts as a positive inotrope (increases heart contraction force), a negative chronotrope (decreases heart rate), and a negative dromotrope (decreases conduction speed). It is used for HF and supraventricular dysrhythmias.
* **Phosphodiesterase Inhibitors (Milrinone):** Known as "inodilators" because they exert both positive inotropic effects and vasodilation. They are typically used for short-term management of acute HF.
* **ACE Inhibitors (Lisinopril):** The drugs of choice to initiate HF treatment. they prevent left-ventricular dysfunction and reduce sodium and water retention.
* **Angiotensin Receptor-Neprilysin Inhibitors (Valsartan/Sacubitril):** A newer class that significantly reduces cardiovascular death and hospitalizations.
* **B-type Natriuretic Peptides (Nesiritide):** Used for life-threatening, acutely decompensated HF. It causes vasodilation and can lead to significant hypotension.
* **Vasodilators (Hydralazine/Isosorbide Dinitrate):** Specifically effective in reducing hospitalization for HF in African American patients.

**2. Essential Nursing Assessments**

* **Pulse Monitoring:** Before administering digoxin, the apical pulse must be measured for one full minute. The dose should be held, and the provider notified if the heart rate is below 60 beats/min or above 100 beats/min.
* **Weight Tracking:** Patients must monitor their weight daily at the same time, in the same clothes. Significant gains (e.g., 2 lbs overnight or 5 lbs in a week) must be reported as they indicate worsening edema.
* **Telemetry:** Patients receiving intravenous milrinone require continuous cardiac monitoring due to the high risk of ventricular dysrhythmias.

**3. Digoxin Toxicity and Management**

* **Therapeutic Range:** The therapeutic serum level for digoxin is 0.5 to 2 ng/mL.
* **Symptoms:** Early signs include anorexia, nausea, vomiting, and loss of appetite. Later signs include bradycardia, headache, dizziness, confusion, and visual disturbances (notably yellow or green halos around lights).
* **Antidote:** Digoxin immune Fab is the specific antidote for severe overdose, life-threatening cardiotoxicity, or severe hyperkalemia resulting from digitalis.

### Nuances for Mastery

**1. Critical Drug-Drug Interactions**

* **The IV Precipitation Trap:** Milrinone and furosemide are chemically incompatible. If administered simultaneously through the same IV line, furosemide will precipitate, rendering the medication ineffective.
* **ACE Inhibitors and ARNIs:** A patient must stop taking an ACE inhibitor (like lisinopril) for at least 36 hours before starting valsartan/sacubitril to prevent severe reactions.
* **Potassium Sensitivity:** Digoxin and potassium have a complex relationship. Hypokalemia (low potassium) greatly increases the risk of digoxin toxicity. Conversely, digoxin toxicity itself can lead to severe hyperkalemia as the drug reduces the ability of potassium to enter cells.
* **Digoxin Level Boosters:** Verapamil, amiodarone, and quinidine can increase digoxin levels by up to 50%, often necessitating a dosage reduction to avoid toxicity.

**2. Factors Affecting Absorption and Excretion**

* **Dietary Fiber:** High-fiber foods, such as bran, bind to digoxin in the GI tract and decrease its absorption if taken together orally.
* **Hypothyroidism:** Patients with hypothyroidism excrete digoxin more slowly, meaning they require lower-than-usual doses to prevent toxic buildup.
* **Herbal Interactions:** Ginseng can increase digoxin levels, while licorice can lead to potassium loss, thereby increasing the risk of cardiac toxicity.

**3. Side Effect Profiles and Contraindications**

* **Lisinopril Specifics:** Students must watch for a dry cough and hyperkalemia. It is also the preferred drug for acute HF post-myocardial infarction.
* **Hydralazine/Isosorbide:** A major side effect is syncope (fainting) due to postural hypotension; patients must be taught to change positions slowly.
* **Milrinone and Asthma:** Milrinone can cause difficulty breathing and is generally contraindicated in patients with asthma.
* **Nesiritide Caution:** This drug should not be co-administered with other drugs that lower blood pressure, such as diuretics, because the combined effect can cause severe hypotension.

**4. Pediatric and Diagnostic Nuances**

* **Children with HF:** Unlike adults, children with HF usually present with tachycardia rather than bradycardia. They often exhibit poor growth, difficulty eating, and dyspnea with minimal exertion.
* **Hypomagnesemia vs. Hypokalemia:** While both can lead to cardiac issues, hypomagnesemia specifically presents with muscle twitching, tremors, and hyperactive reflexes.
