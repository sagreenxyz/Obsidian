# Chapter 10, Analgesic Drugs

Based on the study materials provided, a student must master a wide range of pharmacological principles, nursing assessments, and patient safety protocols regarding analgesic drugs. Below are the main points and nuances essential for proficiency in this topic.

### 1. Classification and Origin of Pain

Understanding where pain originates is critical for correct diagnosis and treatment.

* **Visceral Pain:** Originates from organs and smooth muscles (e.g., pain from gallstones).
* **Somatic Pain:** Originates from skeletal muscles, ligaments, and joints.
* **Superficial Pain:** Originates from the skin and mucous membranes (e.g., oral ulcers).
* **Deep Pain:** Occurs in tissues below the skin level.
* **Vascular Pain:** Originates from vascular or perivascular tissues.

### 2. Opioid Analgesics: Mechanisms and Effects

Opioids are the cornerstone of moderate to severe pain management but carry significant risks.

* **Primary Mechanism:** Opioids bind to opioid receptors in the central nervous system to alter the perception of pain.
* **Adverse Effects:**
* **Respiratory Depression:** The most serious side effect. It is the primary cause of death in overdose cases.
* **Gastrointestinal:** Decreased peristalsis leading to constipation, delayed gastric emptying, and nausea/vomiting (due to stimulation of the chemoreceptor trigger zone).
* **Central Nervous System:** Sedation, disorientation, euphoria, lightheadedness, and dysphoria.
* **Genitourinary:** Urinary retention.
* **Ocular:** Miosis (pinpoint pupils) is a hallmark sign of opioid overdose.
* **Integumentary:** Histamine release can cause itching and, in patients with asthma, can trigger bronchoconstriction.



### 3. Key Opioid Medications and Prototypes

* **Morphine Sulfate:** The prototype for all opioid drugs. It is used as the standard for equianalgesic dosing tables.
* **Fentanyl:** Highly potent. Transdermal patches must be changed every 72 hours, applied to non-hairy areas, kept away from children/pets, and never exposed to external heat (which increases absorption/overdose risk).
* **Codeine Sulfate:** Unique for its antitussive (cough suppressant) properties in addition to analgesia.
* **Oxycodone:** Available in extended-release (ER) forms that must never be crushed, chewed, or dissolved, as this causes rapid, toxic absorption.
* **Methadone Hydrochloride:** Used for both pain management and the treatment of opioid addiction/withdrawal because of its long half-life.
* **Hydromorphone:** A very potent opioid; errors in conversion between morphine and hydromorphone are common and dangerous.

### 4. Opioid Antagonists and Agonist-Antagonists

* **Naloxone:** A competitive antagonist used to reverse respiratory depression in acute overdose. It has a shorter duration than many opioids, so repeated doses may be necessary.
* **Naltrexone:** Used to maintain an opioid-free state in former addicts and as an adjunct for alcoholism.
* **Agonist-Antagonists (e.g., Nalbuphine, Buprenorphine, Butorphanol):** These have a "ceiling effect" where pain relief does not increase past a certain dose, which also limits the risk of respiratory depression compared to full agonists.

### 5. Non-Opioid Analgesics: Acetaminophen

* **Indications:** Mild to moderate pain and fever. It does not have the anti-inflammatory properties of NSAIDs.
* **Toxicity:** Overdose causes hepatotoxicity (liver damage). Signs include elevated liver enzymes and elevated serum bilirubin (e.g., levels above 1.2 mg/dL).
* **Antidote:** Acetylcysteine is the specific antidote for acetaminophen toxicity.

### 6. Nursing Assessments and Priorities

* **Respiratory Monitoring:** If a patient's respiratory rate is low (e.g., 8–10 breaths/min), the nurse’s priority is to withhold the dose, assess responsiveness/neurologic status, and prepare naloxone.
* **Vital Signs:** Baseline vitals must be recorded before administration, especially for IV routes where the onset is rapid.
* **Constipation Management:** Patients should be taught to increase fluid intake (up to 3000 mL/day), increase dietary fiber, and use stimulant laxatives or stool softeners around the clock.

### 7. Nuances for Mastery

To achieve mastery, a student must understand these specific, often-tested details:

* **Equianalgesic Dosing:** A student must be able to calculate conversions between different drugs and routes (e.g., 10 mg parenteral morphine is equivalent to 1.5 mg parenteral hydromorphone or 15 mg oral oxycodone).
* **Breakthrough Pain:** Pain that occurs between scheduled doses of long-acting opioids. The correct intervention is to provide a supplemental short-acting opioid, not to give the long-acting dose early.
* **Around-the-Clock (ATC) vs. PRN:** For chronic or postoperative pain, ATC administration is superior to PRN (as-needed) because it maintains a steady drug level and prevents pain "cycling."
* **Contraindications:** Asthma is a significant contraindication for opioids due to both respiratory depression and histamine-induced bronchospasm.
* **Epidural Safety:** Nurses must educate patients that an epidural involves a thin plastic catheter, not a needle remaining in the spine, to alleviate fears of paralysis.
* **Anxiety and Pain:** Anxiety exacerbates the sensation of pain. Therapeutic communication (e.g., "Tell me more about your fears") is a necessary component of pain management.
* **Herbal Nuances:** Feverfew (*Chrysanthemum parthenium*) is used for migraines but can cause side effects like joint pain, muscle stiffness, and nausea.
* **Fibromyalgia:** This condition is linked to reduced norepinephrine; therefore, SNRIs like Milnacipran are specific treatments rather than standard opioids.
