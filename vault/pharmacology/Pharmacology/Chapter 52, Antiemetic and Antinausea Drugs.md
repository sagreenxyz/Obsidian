# Chapter 52, Antiemetic and Antinausea Drugs

To master the pharmacology of antiemetic and antinausea medications, a student must understand the various drug classes, their specific mechanisms of action, administration protocols, and the critical safety nuances that differentiate them.

### Main Points for Student Mastery

#### 1. Pharmacological Classes and Mechanisms of Action

Students must distinguish which receptors these drugs block and where they exert their effects:

* **Anticholinergics (e.g., Scopolamine):** Block acetylcholine (ACh) receptors in the vestibular nuclei and reticular formation. They are primarily used for motion sickness.
* **Antihistamines (e.g., Meclizine, Diphenhydramine, Dimenhydrinate):** Block H1 receptors, preventing ACh from binding to receptors in the vestibular nuclei. They are used for motion sickness and vertigo.
* **Antidopaminergics (e.g., Promethazine, Prochlorperazine):** Block dopamine receptors in the chemoreceptor trigger zone (CTZ). They are often used for psychotic disorders, intractable hiccups, and general nausea.
* **Prokinetics (e.g., Metoclopramide):** Block dopamine receptors in the CTZ and stimulate ACh receptors in the GI tract to increase motility. This promotes gastric emptying.
* **Serotonin Blockers (e.g., Ondansetron, Dolasetron):** Block 5-HT3 receptors in the GI tract, CTZ, and vomiting center. These are the gold standard for chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV) and postoperative nausea.
* **Tetrahydrocannabinoids (e.g., Dronabinol):** Have inhibitory effects on the reticular formation, thalamus, and cerebral cortex. They are used as second-line therapy for CINV and as appetite stimulants.
* **Neurokinin 1 Receptor Antagonists (e.g., Aprepitant):** Inhibit substance P-neurokinin 1 receptors in the brain. They are used specifically to augment the effects of serotonin blockers during high-dose chemotherapy.

#### 2. Clinical Indications and Selection

* **Chemotherapy (CINV):** Serotonin blockers (Ondansetron) are first-line. For highly emetogenic drugs like cisplatin, aprepitant or corticosteroids (dexamethasone) are added for synergy. Dronabinol is used if other antiemetics fail.
* **Motion Sickness:** Anticholinergics (Scopolamine) and Antihistamines (Dimenhydrinate, Meclizine).
* **Pregnancy:** Ondansetron and Metoclopramide are commonly used. Ginger is an herbal option but carries specific risks.
* **Postoperative Nausea:** Metoclopramide (to increase motility) and Serotonin blockers.

#### 3. Contraindications and Critical Safety

* **Glaucoma:** Anticholinergics (Scopolamine) and Antihistamines are contraindicated in narrow-angle glaucoma because they can increase intraocular pressure.
* **GI Obstruction:** Metoclopramide is strictly contraindicated in patients with absent bowel sounds or mechanical obstruction, as increasing motility could cause bowel rupture.
* **Bone Marrow Suppression/Seizures:** Antidopaminergics like prochlorperazine should be avoided.
* **Cardiac History:** Serotonin blockers (Ondansetron) can prolong the QT interval; cardiac rhythm monitoring is required for patients with pre-existing dysrhythmias.

### Nuances and Master-Level Knowledge

#### 1. Administration Route Specifics

* **Promethazine (Phenergan):** The intravenous (IV) route provides the fastest relief but is extremely hazardous. It must be diluted; intra-arterial injection can lead to severe tissue damage and amputation. If a patient reports burning at the IV site, the infusion must be stopped immediately.
* **Hydroxyzine (Vistaril):** Must only be given via the Oral or Intramuscular (IM) route. IV, subcutaneous, or intra-arterial administration can cause gangrene and thrombosis.
* **Diphenhydramine (Benadryl):** When given IV, it should be administered undiluted at a rate of 25 mg/min.
* **Scopolamine Patches:** Must be applied to a hairless area behind the ear at least 4 hours before travel and changed every 3 days. Rotation of sites is necessary.

#### 2. Metoclopramide (Reglan) Particulars

* **Timing:** For maximum effect on GI motility, it should be taken 30 minutes before meals and at bedtime.
* **Interactions:** Its effects are decreased when taken with opiates or anticholinergics.
* **Adverse Effects:** Long-term use is discouraged due to the risk of irreversible tardive dyskinesia (involuntary neurological movements). It can also cause hypotension, necessitating blood pressure monitoring.

#### 3. Pregnancy and Herbal Considerations

* **Ginger:** While effective for nausea, it has abortifacient properties and should be avoided or used with extreme caution during pregnancy. It also interacts with warfarin, increasing the risk of bleeding.
* **Promethazine:** Generally avoided in pregnancy due to potential teratogenic effects (Category C).

#### 4. Patient Teaching and Side Effect Management

* **Anticholinergic "Drying" Effects:** Patients taking scopolamine or antihistamines will experience dry mouth, urinary retention, and constipation. Mastery involves teaching patients to use hard candy for dryness and increase fiber for constipation.
* **CNS Depression:** Most antiemetics (especially antihistamines and antidopaminergics) cause drowsiness. Patients must avoid alcohol and activities requiring mental alertness, like driving.
* **Dronabinol Assessment:** Because it affects the brain's thalamus and cortex, the nurse must assess the patient’s motor and cognitive abilities before administration.
* **Synergy in Chemo:** Students should know that combining Ondansetron with Dexamethasone or Lorazepam blunts the memory of nausea and provides a synergistic antiemetic effect.
