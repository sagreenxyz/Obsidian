# Chapter 5, Medication Errors: Preventing and Responding

To master the prevention and management of medication errors, a student must understand the systemic nature of these events, the specific protocols for reconciliation, and the technical nuances of documentation and administration.

### Main Points for Mastery

* **The Systemic Perspective:** The majority of medication errors result from weaknesses within the healthcare "system" rather than individual shortcomings. A non-punitive culture is essential for reporting, as "finger-pointing" is counterproductive to safety improvements.
* **The Medication Process:** Errors can occur during procuring, prescribing, transcribing, dispensing, administering, or monitoring. Notably, the majority of preventable adverse drug events (approximately 50%) begin at the **prescribing** stage.
* **Medication Reconciliation:** This is a vital three-step process conducted at every transition of care (e.g., admission, transfer between units, or discharge):
1. **Verification:** Collecting a comprehensive list of all current medications, including over-the-counter (OTC) drugs and herbal supplements.
2. **Clarification:** Professional review of the list to ensure medications and dosages are appropriate for the patient.
3. **Reconciliation:** Investigating any discrepancies and documenting relevant changes.


* **High-Alert Medications:** Certain drug classes—such as insulin, anticoagulants (e.g., Warfarin), chemotherapeutic agents, inotropic drugs, and cardioplegic solutions—are classified as "high-alert." While they may not be involved in more frequent errors than other drugs, the potential for patient harm when an error occurs is significantly higher.
* **Responding to Errors:** If an error occurs, the **priority action is always to assess the patient** for adverse reactions or changes in status. Once the patient is stable, the nurse must notify the healthcare provider and the nurse manager.
* **Incident Reporting:** All errors, including "near misses" (errors caught before reaching the patient or those that resulted in no harm), must be reported. Incident reports should include factual data: the dose given, the patient's response, and the time the provider was notified.

### Essential Nuances and Technical Details

* **The "Zero" Rules:** To prevent transcription and reading errors, nurses must **never use a trailing zero** (e.g., write "5 mg" instead of "5.0 mg") because it can be misread as 50 mg. Conversely, one must **always use a leading zero** for decimal doses (e.g., "0.25 mg" instead of ".25 mg") to ensure the decimal point is not overlooked.
* **Look-Alike/Sound-Alike (LASA) Vigilance:** Mastery requires recognizing drug pairs frequently confused due to similar naming, such as:
* **Celebrex** (anti-inflammatory) vs. **Celexa** (antidepressant).
* **Metformin** (diabetes) vs. **Metronidazole** (antibiotic).


* **Prohibited Abbreviations:** Certain abbreviations are dangerous and should be written out in full:
* **QD** (daily) and **QOD** (every other day) should be avoided.
* **D/C** can mean both "discharge" and "discontinue" and should be clarified.
* **OS** (left eye) can be confused with "os" (by mouth).


* **Patient Identification:** Always use at least **two patient identifiers** (e.g., name and date of birth) and verify them against the patient’s armband. Simply asking a patient their name is insufficient.
* **Pediatric Vulnerabilities:** Children are at a much higher risk for dosing errors because their medications require complex mathematical calculations based on weight in kilograms. Risks are highest in neonatal/pediatric ICUs and emergency departments.
* **Documentation Protocol:** While the facts of a medication error and the patient's response are documented in the medical record, the **incident report itself is a separate administrative document** and should never be mentioned or filed within the patient's permanent chart.
* **Patient Advocacy:** If a patient questions a medication (e.g., "This pill looks different"), the nurse must stop and verify the prescription. While pharmacies may use different manufacturers, the patient's concern is a primary defense against administration errors.
