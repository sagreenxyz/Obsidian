# Chapter 18, Adrenergic Drugs

To master the topic of adrenergic drugs (sympathomimetics), a student must understand the physiological triggers of the sympathetic nervous system, the specific receptor sites, and the clinical application of various agonists.

### Main Points for Mastery

**1. Adrenergic Receptor Classification and Location**

* **Alpha 1:** Located in the eyes (mydriasis/dilation), blood vessels (vasoconstriction), bladder sphincter, and sex organs. Stimulation leads to increased blood pressure and uterine/bladder contraction.
* **Beta 1:** Primarily located in the heart and kidneys. Stimulation increases heart rate (positive chronotropic), contractility (positive inotropic), and conductivity (positive dromotropic), and triggers renin secretion.
* **Beta 2:** Located in the smooth muscles of the bronchioles, arterioles, and visceral organs. Stimulation leads to bronchodilation and vasodilation.

**2. Key Emergency and Vasoactive Drugs**

* **Epinephrine:** The drug of choice for cardiac arrest and anaphylaxis. It increases blood pressure and heart rate. A 1:10,000 concentration contains 0.1 mg of medication per 1 mL.
* **Dopamine:** The primary choice for hypovolemic shock. It increases myocardial contraction and cardiac output.
* **Dobutamine:** Used for cardiac decompensation in heart failure by increasing cardiac contractility.
* **Norepinephrine:** Used primarily for hypotension and shock; however, it is contraindicated in patients with existing hypertension.

**3. Respiratory Adrenergics**

* **Albuterol:** A short-acting beta 2 agonist used for acute bronchodilation. Tachycardia and jitteriness are expected side effects, not reasons to hold treatment during respiratory distress.
* **Salmeterol:** A long-acting agent for asthma maintenance, not acute relief. It is typically dosed as one puff every 12 hours.
* **Inhaler Protocol:** When using a bronchodilator and a corticosteroid together, the bronchodilator must be used first to open airways, followed by a 5-minute wait before the corticosteroid.

**4. Specialized Adrenergics**

* **Midodrine:** Used for symptomatic orthostatic hypotension. Doses should be "front-loaded" in the morning, and the last dose must be taken no later than 18:00 (6:00 p.m.) to prevent supine hypertension and insomnia.
* **Phenylephrine:** An alpha 1 agonist used as an adjunct to local anesthesia to cause vasoconstriction, slowing anesthetic absorption.
* **Oxymetazoline:** A topical nasal decongestant also used to treat epistaxis (nosebleeds) through vasoconstriction.
* **Naphazoline/Dipivefrin:** Ophthalmic agents used for conjunctival congestion or to dilate pupils for diagnostic exams.

### Nuances to be Mastered

**1. Priority Nursing Actions for IV Infusions**
For vasoactive drugs like dopamine, the highest priority is monitoring the IV site hourly for infiltration/extravasation. If infiltration occurs, the **first** action is to stop the infusion immediately. The site may then be used to inject phentolamine to counteract vasoconstrictive damage.

**2. Geriatric Considerations**
Older adults (especially those over 70) are at the greatest risk for adverse reactions due to declining heart efficiency, diminished stroke volume, and decreased renal filtering. Students must know to advise elderly patients to avoid strenuous activities in high heat (like jogging in summer), as their cardiovascular systems cannot compensate for the resulting stress.

**3. Specific Contraindications and Interactions**

* **Hypertensive Crisis:** Adrenergic drugs must never be administered with Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs), as this can cause a life-threatening spike in blood pressure.
* **Mitral Valve Regurgitation:** Alpha 1 agonists (like norepinephrine or phenylephrine) should be avoided because vasoconstriction increases preload and impedes forward blood flow, potentially leading to heart failure.
* **Severe Hypertension:** Adrenergic drugs are generally contraindicated in patients with significantly elevated blood pressure.

**4. Patient Education Nuances**

* **Oral Irritation:** Patients using inhalers must be taught to rinse their mouths thoroughly after use to prevent mucosal irritation.
* **Midodrine Administration:** Patients should take their first dose in the morning with forced fluids *before* getting out of bed to prevent orthostatic syncope.
* **Eye Drop Safety:** To prevent contamination, the tip of the dropper must never touch the eye or any other surface.

**5. Assessing Therapeutic Outcomes**
Success is measured by objective data: a return to normal vital signs (BP >120/80, HR 60–100), improved urinary output, pink/warm extremities, and clear breath sounds. Subjective statements like "I feel fine" are considered vague and insufficient for evaluating drug efficacy.
