# Chapter 7, Over-the-Counter Drugs and Herbal and Dietary Supplements

### Main Points for Mastery

* **Safety and Regulation of OTC Drugs:**
* **Criteria for OTC Status:** For a drug to be sold over-the-counter, it must be easy to use and monitor, have a high therapeutic index (a wide safety margin), and have a favorable adverse event profile with limited drug-drug interactions.
* **Labeling and Claims:** Manufacturers can claim a product has an effect (e.g., improves memory) but cannot promise a specific cure on the label. Patients must read labels to identify active ingredients, warnings, and when to consult a provider.
* **Risks of Self-Medication:** Long-term use of OTC drugs can lead to the postponement of professional medical consultation, resulting in the ineffective management of chronic diseases or the masking of life-threatening symptoms.


* **Common OTC Medications and Their Risks:**
* **Acetaminophen:** The primary risk is hepatotoxicity (liver toxicity). The maximum recommended dose is 4 grams per day. Overdoses are common because acetaminophen is a frequent "hidden" ingredient in multi-symptom cold medications.
* **NSAIDs (e.g., Ibuprofen):** Chronic use or high doses can lead to gastrointestinal ulcers, myocardial infarction, and stroke. Overuse of ibuprofen is also associated with renal toxicity.
* **Cough and Cold Products:** These often contain sympathomimetics that interact with medications for high blood pressure and cardiac disorders. They also carry a risk of substance misuse (e.g., "robotripping" with dextromethorphan).
* **Antacids:** Long-term use frequently causes constipation and can alter the absorption of other medications.


* **Pediatric Safety:**
* **Age Restrictions:** The FDA recommends against giving OTC cough and cold products to children under 2 years old due to risks of oversedation, seizures, tachycardia, and death.
* **Aspirin:** Must be avoided in children and teenagers due to the risk of Reye’s Syndrome.
* **Dosing Errors:** Parents give inaccurate doses up to 50% of the time. Dosing should be based on weight rather than age, and only the measuring device provided with the specific medication should be used.


* **Herbal and Dietary Supplements:**
* **General Misconception:** "Natural" does not mean safe. Herbal products can have potent side effects and significant drug interactions.
* **Terminology:** * **Alternative Medicine:** Using nontraditional remedies instead of conventional medicine.
* **Complementary/Integrative Medicine:** Using both nontraditional and conventional medicine simultaneously.


* **Grandfathered Products:** These are products considered ineffective but not unsafe, which are allowed to remain on the market.

### Nuances to be Mastered

* **Specific Drug-Herb-Food Interactions:**
* **Anticoagulants (Warfarin/Antiplatelets):** Use of **Ginkgo, Chamomile, Ginger, or Garlic** significantly increases the risk of bleeding and hemorrhage.
* **SSRIs/Antidepressants:** Taking **St. John’s Wort** while on prescription antidepressants can lead to life-threatening **Serotonin Syndrome**.
* **Immunosuppressants:** Patients with organ transplants must avoid **Echinacea**, as it interferes with drug therapy meant to prevent organ rejection.
* **Oral Contraceptives:** **Saw Palmetto** can change hormone levels, potentially leading to contraceptive failure.
* **Grapefruit:** It inhibits the metabolism of many drugs, including those for **erectile dysfunction and estrogen replacement therapy**, leading to toxic drug accumulation.
* **Renal Excretion:** **Cranberry** can decrease the elimination of drugs that are renally excreted, increasing toxicity risks in patients with reduced renal function.


* **Condition-Specific Contraindications:**
* **Liver Disease/Hepatitis:** Use of **Kava** is strictly contraindicated due to its high risk of liver toxicity.
* **Diabetes:** **Ginger and Garlic** can alter blood glucose control, requiring closer monitoring of patients on antidiabetic medications like glyburide.
* **Chronic Illness:** Patients with heart, liver, or renal failure are at the highest risk for adverse OTC effects because their ability to metabolize or excrete drugs is compromised.


* **Patient Education Nuances:**
* **Terminology:** When assessing a patient, using the term "home remedies" may be more effective than "herbal therapy" for eliciting a complete history.
* **Storage:** Adult and children’s medications should be stored in separate areas to prevent accidental double-dosing or administration of adult-strength medicine to a child.
* **Pharmacist Role:** While pharmacists are resources, they may not have the patient's full medical history; therefore, patients with chronic conditions should always consult their primary healthcare provider before starting any new OTC or herbal regimen.
