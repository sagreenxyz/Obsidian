# Chapter 12, Central Nervous System Depressants and Muscle Relaxants

To master the topic of CNS depressants and muscle relaxants, a student must understand the pharmacological classifications, mechanisms of action, safety protocols, and the physiological stages of sleep.

### **1. Stages of Sleep and Sleep Disorders**

* **Non-REM Sleep Stages:** * **Stage 1 (Dozing):** Feelings of drifting off.
* **Stage 2 (Relaxation):** The person is easily awakened.
* **Stage 3 (Deep Sleep):** Difficult to wake; vitals like respiratory rate, pulse, and blood pressure may decrease.
* **Stage 4 (Deepest Sleep):** Very difficult to wake; this is the stage where sleepwalking (somnambulation) and bedwetting typically occur.


* **REM Sleep Phenomena:** * **REM Rebound:** Occurs after stopping sedative-hypnotics, resulting in an abnormally large amount of REM sleep and frequent, vivid dreams.
* **REM Interference:** Caused by prolonged use of sedative-hypnotics, which reduces cumulative REM sleep and leads to daytime fatigue.


* **Nonpharmacologic Interventions:** The primary way to reset the internal clock is by establishing a set sleep pattern (regular bed and wake times). Avoiding alcohol and tobacco at bedtime is also essential, as they fragment or disturb sleep.

### **2. Benzodiazepines**

* **Mechanism and Use:** These drugs act on GABA receptors to produce sedation. Midazolam is a key drug for preoperative sedation because it provides both rapid-onset sedation and amnesia. Diazepam is used for anxiety, seizures, and muscle relaxation.
* **Specific Antidote (Flumazenil):** Flumazenil is used to reverse benzodiazepine overdose. Dosing is a nuance to master:
* **Moderate Toxicity (Confusion/Drowsiness):** Max dose of 1 mg.
* **Severe Toxicity (Hypotension):** Max dose of 3 mg.


* **Drug Interactions:** * **Rifampin** increases metabolism, thereby decreasing benzodiazepine effects.
* **Azole antifungals, verapamil, and macrolide antibiotics** decrease metabolism, prolonging the drug's effects.


* **Safety and Populations:** Older adults are at a high risk for ataxia (uncoordinated movement) and falls. Nursing documentation must prioritize safety interventions, such as keeping the bed in a low position and the call light within reach.

### **3. Barbiturates**

* **Classifications by Duration:**
* **Ultra-short-acting:** Methohexital, thiopental.
* **Short-acting:** Pentobarbital, secobarbital (3–4 hour duration).
* **Intermediate-acting:** Butabarbital (6–8 hour duration).
* **Long-acting:** Phenobarbital (epileptic seizure prophylaxis) and mephobarbital.


* **Overdose Management:** There is no specific antidote for barbiturates. Treatment is supportive. **Activated charcoal** is administered (often via NG tube every 4 hours) to bind the drug in the stomach and draw it from the circulation. Furosemide may be used to promote diuresis.
* **Contraindications:** Pregnancy, drug allergy, and severe kidney or liver disease.

### **4. Muscle Relaxants**

* **Direct-Acting vs. Centrally Acting:**
* **Dantrolene:** The only muscle relaxant that acts directly on the skeletal muscle by suppressing calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum. It is the drug of choice for **Malignant Hyperthermia**.
* **Centrally Acting:** Drugs like Baclofen and Cyclobenzaprine work by depressing the central nervous system.


* **Clinical Applications:** Beyond general muscle spasms, these are used to reduce choreiform movements in Huntington disease and muscular rigidity in Parkinson disease.

### **5. Critical Nuances for Mastery**

* **Alcohol Withdrawal:** Diazepam is effective for alcohol withdrawal because benzodiazepine receptors are located in the same CNS area as receptors responsible for alcohol addiction.
* **Neonatal Care:** **Phenobarbital** is uniquely used to treat hyperbilirubinemia in neonates because it promotes the conversion of bilirubin into an excretable form. Conversely, drugs like ceftriaxone or sulfonamides are dangerous in neonates because they can increase bilirubin levels or cause fatal precipitation.
* **Nonbenzodiazepine Specifics:**
* **Zolpidem:** Highly associated with sleepwalking as a side effect.
* **Ramelteon:** Contraindicated in patients with COPD or sleep apnea because it can affect respiratory drive.
* **Eszopiclone:** Approved for long-term use (over a month), but requires a full 8-hour sleep window to avoid impairment.


* **Cultural Nuances:** * **African Americans** have a high incidence of lactose intolerance; if warm milk is recommended for sleep, it must be lactose-free.
* **Chinese patients** may require lower doses of benzodiazepines due to metabolic differences.


* **Sedative vs. Hypnotic:** A sedative reduces nervousness without causing sleep, while a hypnotic causes sleep at high doses. The classification is determined by the degree of inhibition of nerve impulse transmission to the CNS.

# Chapter 12, Central Nervous System Depressants and Muscle Relaxants

To master the pharmacology of Central Nervous System (CNS) depressants and muscle relaxants, a student must understand the distinct classifications, clinical indications, life-threatening risks, and specific nursing interventions associated with each drug class.

### Main Points for Mastery

#### 1. Benzodiazepines

* **Common Drugs:** Diazepam, Midazolam, Alprazolam, Temazepam, Clonazepam.
* **Indications:** Used for sedation, anxiety relief, sleep induction, skeletal muscle relaxation, and acute seizure disorders. Specifically, Diazepam and Chlordiazepoxide are used for alcohol withdrawal because they act on receptors associated with alcohol addiction.
* **Pharmacokinetics:** * **Temazepam:** Onset is approximately one hour; it should be taken 1 hour before bed.
* **Midazolam:** Known for inducing amnesia, making it useful for uncomfortable medical procedures.


* **Antidote (Flumazenil):** * Used for benzodiazepine overdose or to reverse IV sedation.
* **Dosing Nuance:** 1 mg is given for moderate toxicity (confusion, drowsiness); up to 3 mg is given for severe toxicity (hypotension).
* **Safety Note:** Flumazenil has a short half-life (1–4 hours). Patients may become unconscious again as the antidote wears off, requiring additional doses.



#### 2. Nonbenzodiazepine Hypnotics

* **Zolpidem:** Short half-life with a lower incidence of daytime sleepiness. A notable side effect is somnambulation (sleepwalking).
* **Zaleplon:** Indicated for patients who have difficulty with early-morning awakenings.
* **Ramelteon:** Structurally similar to melatonin; it helps with sleep onset without causing respiratory suppression.
* **Eszopiclone:** The first hypnotic approved for long-term use. Patients must allow for a full 8 hours of sleep to avoid daytime impairment.

#### 3. Barbiturates

* **Duration of Action:**
* **Ultra-short-acting (Thiopental):** Used for anesthesia during brief surgical procedures.
* **Short-acting (Pentobarbital):** Used for preoperative sedation and convulsive conditions.
* **Intermediate-acting (Butabarbital):** Used for sedation.
* **Long-acting (Phenobarbital):** Used for seizure prophylaxis and treating hyperbilirubinemia in neonates.


* **Therapeutic Range:** For Phenobarbital, the therapeutic level is 10–40 mcg/mL. Levels above 40 mcg/mL lead to toxicity (cold/clammy skin, respiratory rate below 10 breaths/min).
* **Management of Overdose:** There is no specific antidote. Priorities include maintaining an adequate airway (assisted ventilation) and using activated charcoal to prevent further absorption.

#### 4. Muscle Relaxants

* **Centrally Acting:** Baclofen and Cyclobenzaprine work via CNS depression. Side effects include drowsiness, lethargy, and blurred vision, which can interfere with rehabilitation.
* **Direct-Acting (Dantrolene):** Acts directly on skeletal muscle by suppressing calcium release. It is the drug of choice for treating malignant hyperthermia.
* **Safety:** Cyclobenzaprine (structurally related to tricyclic antidepressants) carries risks for patients with cardiovascular conditions (e.g., heart failure, low heart rate).

### Nuances and Critical Thinking

#### Drug Interactions and Contraindications

* **Benzodiazepine Interactions:** * **Metabolism Inhibitors:** Verapamil, azole antifungals, and grapefruit juice decrease metabolism, leading to prolonged effects and potential toxicity.
* **Metabolism Inducers:** Rifampin increases metabolism, resulting in decreased efficacy (e.g., "sleeping less than usual").


* **Barbiturate Interactions:** They increase the metabolism of oral contraceptives; patients must be advised to use an alternate contraceptive method.
* **Contraindications:** * **Narrow-angle glaucoma:** Benzodiazepines (like Alprazolam or Temazepam) are contraindicated as they increase intraocular pressure.
* **Respiratory Conditions:** Barbiturates (Pentobarbital) and Ramelteon are contraindicated in patients with COPD or sleep apnea due to respiratory risks.
* **Aspirin Allergy:** There is a potential cross-sensitivity between aspirin allergy and nonbenzodiazepines like Zaleplon.



#### Nursing Priorities and Patient Safety

* **Overdose Priority:** For both barbiturates and muscle relaxants (like Baclofen), the absolute priority is airway maintenance and oxygenation.
* **Geriatric and Pediatric Concerns:** These populations should start with small doses of benzodiazepines to prevent ataxia (loss of coordination) and excessive sedation.
* **Adolescent Concerns:** Monitor teenagers on benzodiazepines closely for suicidal tendencies.
* **IV Administration:** Pentobarbital infusion sites must be monitored for infiltration (swelling, redness). Rapid infusion can cause profound hypotension.

#### Cultural and Lifestyle Considerations

* **Lactose Intolerance:** Hispanics, Asians, African Americans, and Pacific Islanders have a high incidence of lactose intolerance. These patients should avoid the common practice of drinking warm milk before bed to promote sleep, as it may cause GI distress.
* **Asian Patients:** This population may require lower doses of benzodiazepines to achieve the desired effect.
* **Sleep Hygiene:** Patients should stop caffeine intake at least 6 hours before bed and avoid exercise immediately before sleep. Avoid watching television in bed to ensure the brain associates the bed only with sleep.
