# Chapter 58, Otic Drugs

To master the pharmacology and clinical application of otic drugs, a student must understand the categorization of medications, precise administration techniques, and critical safety precautions related to the anatomy of the ear.

### Main Points for Student Mastery

**1. Categorization of Otic Medications**

* **Antibacterials:** Used to treat bacterial infections like otitis externa and otitis media. Common agents include ofloxacin and ciprofloxacin.
* **Antifungals:** Specifically targeted at fungal infections of the ear (otomycosis). Examples include Cortic and chloroxylenol.
* **Steroids:** Often combined with antibiotics (e.g., hydrocortisone or dexamethasone) to reduce inflammation, itching, and edema in the ear canal.
* **Wax Emulsifiers:** Products like carbamide peroxide are used to soften and help remove impacted cerumen (earwax). These often contain glycerin as a lubricant.
* **Local Anesthetics:** Drugs such as pramoxine may be added to reduce pain and itching, though they do not treat the underlying infection.

**2. Anatomical and Pathological Knowledge**

* **Ear Structure:** Students must know the ear is divided into external (pinna/auricle), outer (auditory canal), middle (tympanic membrane, ossicles, eustachian tube), and inner (cochlea, semicircular canals) regions.
* **Otitis Media:** An infection of the middle ear often preceded by upper respiratory infections. The first-line treatment is typically systemic (oral) amoxicillin, especially in children.
* **Otitis Externa:** An infection of the outer ear canal (swimmer's ear), usually treated with topical otic drops.

**3. Proper Administration Techniques**

* **Positioning:** The patient should be in a supine or side-lying position with the affected ear facing up.
* **Pinna Manipulation:** For adults, pull the pinna **up and back**. For children younger than 3 years old, pull the pinna **down and back**.
* **Post-Instillation Care:** After drops are administered, the patient should remain on their side for at least 5 minutes. Gently massaging the **tragus** helps the medication flow deeper into the canal. A small cotton ball may be loosely inserted to prevent leakage, but it should not be jammed into the canal.

**4. Contraindications and Ototoxicity**

* **Perforated Eardrum:** If the tympanic membrane is ruptured, many medications are contraindicated because they can cause permanent hearing damage (ototoxicity). Neomycin, polymyxin B, and hydrocortisone must **never** be used with a perforated eardrum. Ofloxacin and ciprofloxacin are generally considered safe in these cases.

### Nuances for Advanced Mastery

**1. Temperature Sensitivity and Vestibular Reactions**

* **The Room Temperature Rule:** Otic drops must be administered at room temperature. If stored in a refrigerator, they should be removed 1 hour prior to use or warmed by running the bottle under warm water.
* **Consequences of Cold Drops:** Instilling cold eardrops can trigger a **vestibular-type reaction**, leading to sudden onset of severe dizziness, vertigo, nausea, and vomiting.
* **Warning Against Heat:** Never microwave eardrops or use hot water to warm them. High heat can destroy the potency of the medication and risks burning the sensitive ear canal.

**2. Medication Error Prevention (Otic vs. Ophthalmic)**

* **Cross-Contamination Risk:** Eardrops and eyedrops often come in similar packaging. Students must master the "Ear vs. Eye" distinction. While "ophthalmic" drops can sometimes be used in the ear in emergencies, **otic drops must never be used in the eye**, as they are too acidic and can cause severe damage.
* **Storage Nuance:** Medications should be kept in their original cartons, which often feature clear icons of an ear or eye to prevent confusion.

**3. Complex Wax Removal Protocols**

* **Step-by-Step Irrigation:** Mastery involves knowing the sequence:
1. Lubricate/soften the wax (often with glycerin).
2. Apply the wax emulsifier (carbamide peroxide) to release oxygen and loosen the plug.
3. Allow time for the wax to dissolve.
4. Gently flush the canal with warm water using a bulb syringe.


* **Prohibition of Tools:** Patients must be strictly cautioned against using cotton-tipped applicators (Q-tips), as these push wax deeper and risk eardrum perforation.

**4. Drug-Specific Nuances**

* **Ciprodex Mastery:** A student should know that Ciprodex is a specific combination of Ciprofloxacin (antibacterial) and Dexamethasone (steroid).
* **Systemic vs. Topical:** For severe or internal infections (like otitis media with a perforated eardrum), a combination of oral (systemic) and topical (otic) antibiotics may be required simultaneously.
* **Duration of Therapy:** Patients must be taught to complete the full course of antibiotics even if symptoms (like pain or fullness) improve within 24 to 72 hours.
