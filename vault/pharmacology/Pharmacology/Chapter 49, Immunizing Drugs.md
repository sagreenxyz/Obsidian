# Chapter 49, Immunizing Drugs

To achieve proficiency in the study of immunizing drugs, a student must master the following core concepts, specific drug details, and clinical nuances derived from the pharmacological assessment of this topic.

### 1. Core Concepts and Types of Immunity

Understanding the mechanism by which the body acquires protection is fundamental:

* **Active Immunity:** Occurs when the body’s own immune system is stimulated to produce antibodies.
* **Natural Active:** Resulting from surviving the disease itself.
* **Artificial Active:** Resulting from the administration of vaccines or toxoids (harmless forms of antigens).


* **Passive Immunity:** Occurs when antibodies are transferred directly to the host, bypassing the host's immune system. This provides temporary, immediate protection.
* **Natural Passive:** Antibodies passed from mother to fetus (placenta) or infant (breast milk).
* **Artificial Passive:** Inoculation with serum or immunoglobulins (e.g., tetanus immunoglobulin, rabies immunoglobulin, or antivenins) obtained from humans or animals.


* **Herd Immunity:** The resistance of an entire population to a disease because a large proportion of its members are immune, thereby limiting the spread of the pathogen.

### 2. Specific Immunizing Drugs and Indications

Students must know the specific purpose and target of various vaccines:

* **Hib (Haemophilus influenzae type b):** Specifically prevents bacterial meningitis in children.
* **HPV (Human Papillomavirus):** Administered as a prophylactic treatment to prevent cervical cancer and genital warts.
* **MMR (Measles, Mumps, and Rubella):** A live virus vaccine used for prevention; requires specific precautions regarding pregnancy.
* **Hepatitis B:** A recombinant/inactivated vaccine; recommended for healthcare workers, IV drug users, and those with infected partners.
* **Varicella (Chickenpox):** A live attenuated vaccine; contraindicated in immunocompromised patients.
* **Meningococcal Vaccine:**
* **Menactra:** Approved for ages 9 months to 55 years.
* **Menveo:** Approved for ages 11 to 55 years.
* **Trumenba/Bexsero:** Indicated for serogroup B in individuals aged 10 to 25.


* **Pneumococcal Vaccine:** Polyvalent versions are used for high-risk/immunocompromised pediatric patients.
* **Antivenins:** Used to neutralize venom from black widow spiders, coral snakes, and crotalids (e.g., rattlesnakes). Healthy adults are at low risk for death if treated promptly, but the very young and elderly are highly susceptible.

### 3. Contraindications and High-Risk Populations

A critical nuance is identifying who should *not* receive certain immunizations:

* **Allergies:**
* **Egg/Yeast:** Common contraindications for many vaccines (e.g., Hepatitis B and HPV are contraindicated with yeast allergies).
* **Neomycin/Gentamicin/Amphotericin B:** History of anaphylaxis to these antibiotics is a contraindication for the rabies vaccine.
* **Neomycin:** Also a contraindication for the MMR vaccine.


* **Immunocompromised Status (HIV/AIDS, Chemotherapy, Steroids):**
* Active immunizing drugs (vaccines/toxoids) are generally ineffective or unsafe because they require a functional immune system.
* **Immunoglobulins** are the preferred choice for these patients because they act immediately and do not rely on the host's immune response.


* **Pregnancy:** * **Live Vaccines (MMR, Varicella):** Contraindicated due to risk of fetal infection. Pregnancy tests are required for women of childbearing age not on birth control.
* **Hepatitis B:** Not a contraindication; should be given post-exposure even during pregnancy.


* **Asplenia:** Patients without a spleen are immunocompromised and require a two-dose series of the meningococcal vaccine rather than a single dose.

### 4. Drug Interactions and Safety Nuances

* **Salicylates (Aspirin):** Must be avoided for 6 weeks after receiving the Varicella vaccine to prevent **Reye Syndrome**, a potentially fatal condition.
* **Immunosuppressants:** Corticosteroids, chemotherapy, and other immunosuppressive drugs reduce the efficacy of vaccines.
* **Anticoagulants:** IM injections (most vaccines) must be given with extreme caution to patients on drugs like warfarin due to bleeding risks.
* **DTaP vs. Tdap:**
* **DTaP:** For children (6 weeks to 6 years).
* **Tdap (Boostrix/Adacel):** A booster for older children, adolescents, and adults. It contains a lower amount of antigen. If a child mistakenly receives Tdap, they may not be sufficiently immunized.



### 5. Adverse Effects and Management

* **Minor Effects:** Soreness at the injection site (integumentary), low-grade fever, and adenopathy (minor CNS).
* **Major Effects:** Convulsions (major CNS), dyspnea (major respiratory), and peripheral neuropathy.
* **Anaphylaxis:** The most priority assessment after any vaccination.
* **Serum Sickness:** Occurs after repeated injections of equine-derived (horse) drugs. Symptoms include facial swelling, joint inflammation, fever, itching, and breathing difficulties. It is treated with analgesics, antihistamines, and corticosteroids.
* **Injection Site Edema:** Managed with anti-inflammatory drugs (not heat or antihistamines).

### 6. Administration and Nursing Nuances

* **Rabies Post-Exposure Protocol:**
* **Non-immunized:** 5 doses on days 0, 3, 7, 14, and 28.
* **Previously immunized:** 2 booster doses on days 0 and 3.
* If a patient has a history of allergy to the rabies vaccine but is bitten by a high-risk animal (e.g., a fox), the full course is still administered alongside supportive therapy (epinephrine, diphenhydramine, steroids).


* **Influenza:** Best given via the IM route (Fluzone). The intranasal route (FluMist) is an alternative, but the vaccine is not effective if given ID or SQ. High-risk groups for complications include those with asthma, cancer, or AIDS.
* **Hepatitis B Immunoglobulin (Infant):** Must be given intramuscularly shortly after birth if the mother is Hepatitis B positive; it should never be diluted with dextrose.
* **Patient Education:** * Women receiving MMR should avoid pregnancy for 3 months.
* Tetanus boosters are generally required every 10 years.
* Soreness at the injection site is a common, expected reaction for tetanus toxoid.
