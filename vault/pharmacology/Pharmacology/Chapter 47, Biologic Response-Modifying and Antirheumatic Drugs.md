# Chapter 47, Biologic Response-Modifying and Antirheumatic Drugs

To master this topic, a student must understand the mechanisms, indications, contraindications, and specific nursing considerations for several classes of immunomodulating drugs.

### Main Points and Core Concepts

**1. Mechanisms of Biologic Response-Modifying Drugs**
These drugs primarily work against cancer cells through three mechanisms:

* Enhancing or restoring the host’s immune system defenses against the tumor.
* Exerting a direct toxic effect on tumor cells, causing them to lyse (rupture).
* Modifying the tumor’s biology to make it harder for cells to survive and reproduce.

**2. The Immune System Foundation**

* **Cell-Mediated System:** Comprised of T lymphocytes, including macrophages, T helper cells, cytotoxic T cells, and natural killer cells.
* **Humoral System:** Responsible for producing memory cells and immunoglobulins.
* **Cell Ratios:** A critical nuance is the 2:1 ratio of T helper cells to T suppressor cells in the human body.

**3. Hematopoietic Drugs**
These drugs are used to support the bone marrow during chemotherapy.

* **Erythropoietic Drugs:** Epoetin alfa and darbepoetin alfa stimulate red blood cell production.
* **Colony-Stimulating Factors (CSFs):** Filgrastim, sargramostim, and pegfilgrastim stimulate white blood cell (WBC) production.
* **Platelet-Promoting Drugs:** Oprelvekin (Neumega) is used specifically for chemotherapy-induced thrombocytopenia.
* **Side Effects:** Expected effects include alopecia (hair loss), rash, edema, and sore throat.

**4. Interferons**
Used for viral infections (like Hepatitis C), cancers, and multiple sclerosis.

* **Indications:** Interferon alfa-2a and peginterferons are used for chronic hepatitis C. Interferon beta-1a is a mainstay for relapsing multiple sclerosis.
* **Adverse Effects:** Patients often experience a "flu-like" syndrome (fatigue, fever, chills, myalgia). More severe effects include hallucinations, confusion, and cyanosis.

**5. Monoclonal Antibodies (mAbs)**
This is a diverse class with very specific indications and risks.

* **Specific Drugs:**
* **Infliximab:** Used for ankylosing spondylitis, Crohn’s disease, and RA.
* **Adalimumab:** Used for RA.
* **Rituximab:** Used for non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma.
* **Bevacizumab:** Used for metastatic colorectal cancer.
* **Belimumab:** Specifically approved for systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE).


* **Contraindications:** Generally contraindicated in patients with active infectious processes due to their immunosuppressive nature.

**6. Interleukins (Aldesleukin)**
Primarily used for metastatic renal cell carcinoma and melanoma. It is known for its high toxicity profile.

**7. Disease-Modifying Antirheumatic Drugs (DMARDs)**
Used to slow the progression of rheumatoid arthritis (RA).

* **Methotrexate:** A gold standard, typically administered once per week.
* **Leflunomide:** An oral medication that can cause diarrhea, respiratory infections, and alopecia.
* **Etanercept:** Used for RA and plaque psoriasis; administered subcutaneously.
* **Tocilizumab:** An IL-6 antagonist for severe RA, often given as an IV infusion.

### Nuances and Critical Mastery Details

**1. Life-Threatening Contraindications (Black Box Warnings)**

* **Infliximab:** Must test for latent tuberculosis (TB) before administration; it is strictly contraindicated in patients with severe heart failure or active TB.
* **Trastuzumab:** Contraindicated in patients with ventricular dysfunction or heart failure.
* **Alemtuzumab:** Contraindicated in patients who are HIV-positive or have other immunodeficiency conditions.
* **Filgrastim/Sargramostim:** Contraindicated if the patient has more than 10% myeloid blasts in the bone marrow, as these drugs may stimulate the growth of the myeloid tumor itself.

**2. Serious Adverse Syndromes**

* **Capillary Leak Syndrome:** Associated with Aldesleukin. Capillaries lose integrity, leading to massive fluid retention, rapid weight gain (e.g., 10 lbs in a week), and potential respiratory distress. Note that while water leaks, albumin and protein stay in the blood, becoming more concentrated.
* **Reactivation Risks:** Tocilizumab can reactivate the herpes zoster virus (shingles). Etanercept and Infliximab can reactivate latent TB or Hepatitis.

**3. Medication Timing and Administration**

* **Filgrastim Timing:** Must not be given within 24 hours of cytotoxic chemotherapy (either before or after). It is typically discontinued once the Absolute Neutrophil Count (ANC) rises above 10,000 cells/mm³.
* **Methotrexate Dosing:** Must be given weekly. Daily administration is a fatal medication error.
* **Oprelvekin Sites:** Subcutaneous injection sites must be rotated (thigh, abdomen, arm, hip).
* **Leflunomide:** Should be taken with food to minimize GI upset.

**4. Drug-Drug Interactions**

* **Lithium + Filgrastim:** Lithium potentiates the bone marrow-stimulating effects of filgrastim, which can lead to excessive WBC production.
* **Aldesleukin + Antihypertensives:** Can cause additive, dangerous hypotension.
* **Etanercept + Cyclophosphamide:** Increases the risk of malignancy.
* **Interferon beta-1a + Natalizumab:** Reduces the clearance of natalizumab, increasing toxicity risks.

**5. Nursing Assessments and Premedication**

* **Premedication:** Tocilizumab and Rituximab require premedication with acetaminophen and diphenhydramine (Benadryl) to prevent infusion reactions and anaphylaxis. Corticosteroids may also be used.
* **Monitoring:**
* **Bevacizumab:** Monitor WBC count (risk of leukopenia).
* **Golimumab:** Monitor Liver Function Tests (LFTs).
* **Interferons:** Monitor Creatinine, BUN, LFTs, and Urinalysis (for protein).
* **Methotrexate:** Females of reproductive age must use effective contraception due to high teratogenic risk (Pregnancy Category X). It also requires monitoring for bone marrow suppression.
