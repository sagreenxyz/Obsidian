# Chapter 46, Antineoplastic Drugs Part 2: Cell Cycle-Nonspecific and Miscellaneous Drugs

For students to master this topic, they must understand the classification, specific toxicities, nursing interventions, and unique pharmacological nuances of cell cycle-nonspecific (CCNS) and miscellaneous antineoplastic drugs.

### 1. Alkylating Agents

These drugs work by preventing cancer cells from reproducing by altering the chemical structure of their DNA.

* **Key Drugs:** Cisplatin, Cyclophosphamide, Busulfan, Mechlorethamine, Carmustine, Thiotepa.
* **Main Points:**
* **Cisplatin:** Primarily known for **nephrotoxicity** and **ototoxicity**. Students must know to monitor renal function (creatinine/BUN) and assess for tinnitus or hearing loss. It also causes peripheral neuropathy, making patients sensitive to cold.
* **Cyclophosphamide:** The hallmark adverse effect is **hemorrhagic cystitis**.
* **Busulfan:** Associated with **pulmonary fibrosis**; nurses must monitor for cough, shortness of breath, and abnormal breath sounds.


* **Nuances to Master:**
* **Functional Differences:** Cisplatin and Mechlorethamine are **bifunctional** (alkylate at two sites), while Cyclophosphamide is **polyfunctional** (involved in various alkylation processes).
* **Preventative Hydration:** To prevent nephrotoxicity from Cisplatin and hemorrhagic cystitis from Cyclophosphamide, patients require aggressive hydration (2–3 L of IV fluids, often at a rate of 200 mL/hr).
* **Equipment Precautions:** Do not use aluminum needles with Cisplatin, as it reacts with the platinum and reduces the drug's effectiveness.



### 2. Cytotoxic Antibiotics

These are CCNS drugs that interact with DNA through a process called intercalation.

* **Key Drugs:** Doxorubicin, Bleomycin, Mitoxantrone, Dactinomycin, Mitomycin.
* **Main Points:**
* **Doxorubicin:** The major dose-limiting side effect is **cardiomyopathy/cardiovascular toxicity**. Patients have a "lifetime limited dose." Sudden weight gain (e.g., 6 lbs in a week) indicates fluid retention and heart failure.
* **Bleomycin:** Known for pulmonary toxicity (pneumonitis and pulmonary fibrosis). Unlike many others, it has low bone marrow suppression but requires pulmonary function tests.
* **Mitoxantrone:** Used for prostate cancer and notably for neurologic disorders like **multiple sclerosis**.


* **Nuances to Master:**
* **Cardioprotection:** Dexrazoxane may be used with Doxorubicin to protect the heart.
* **Liposomal Forms:** Doxorubicin is available in a liposomal form, which reduces cardiotoxicity and increases therapeutic activity.
* **Drug Interactions:** Bleomycin can increase serum concentrations of **digoxin**, leading to potential toxicity.



### 3. Miscellaneous and Targeted Agents

* **Bevacizumab:** An angiogenesis inhibitor (VEGF blocker) that stops blood supply to tumors. Key side effects include **hypertension, proteinuria, and hypokalemia**.
* **Hydroxyurea:** An antimetabolite used for squamous cell carcinoma and sickle cell anemia. It is **only available in oral form**. It causes hyperuricemia and can reduce the clearance of cytarabine.
* **Imatinib:** Used for Chronic Myeloid Leukemia (CML). It is an oral Bcr-Abl tyrosine kinase inhibitor.
* **Octreotide:** Used specifically to treat **carcinoid crisis** associated with carcinoid tumors.
* **Mitotane:** An adrenal cytotoxic drug. It increases the clearance of **phenytoin**, which can lower the seizure threshold if the phenytoin dose isn't adjusted.
* **Ceritinib:** Used for lung cancer. Nuances include taking it on an **empty stomach** (2 hours before or after meals), avoiding grapefruit juice, and reporting **bradycardia**.

### 4. Hormonal Antineoplastics

* **Tamoxifen:** An estrogen antagonist. A key nuance is its use in menopausal women; nurses must monitor for increased hot flashes and vasodilation.
* **Anastrozole:** Used for breast cancer; common side effects include vasodilation and hypertension (not hypotension).

### 5. Critical Nursing Safety and Interventions

#### Extravasation Management

Extravasation is an emergency. The general protocol order is:

1. **Stop the infusion immediately.**
2. **Aspirate** residuals/blood from the catheter.
3. **Consult guidelines for the specific antidote.**
4. **Rest/Elevate** the affected limb.
5. **Document** the event thoroughly.

**Specific Antidotes:**

* **Mechlorethamine:** Sodium thiosulfate.
* **Carmustine:** Sodium bicarbonate and normal saline.
* **Doxorubicin:** Cryotherapy (ice packs) and rest. (Note: Sodium thiosulfate is NOT used for Doxorubicin).

#### Myelosuppression (Bone Marrow Suppression)

Students must recognize that most antineoplastics cause a drop in RBCs (anemia), WBCs (leukopenia/infection risk), and platelets (thrombocytopenia/bleeding risk).

* **Interventions:** Use soft-bristled toothbrushes, avoid IM injections, and monitor for occult blood.
* **Laboratory Nuance:** A thrombocyte count of 8,000/mm³ is a critical value that would require withholding drugs like hydroxyurea.

#### Oncologic Emergencies

Patients must be taught to report the following immediately:

* Fever > 100.5° F.
* New, persistent cough (indicates pulmonary toxicity).
* Blood in urine or stools.
* Swollen tongue or new mouth sores (stomatitis).
* Changes in bladder patterns or sudden urinary retention.

#### Handling and Administration

* **Safety:** Use special spill kits for even small spills. Dispose of all items in contact with the patient in specialized hazardous hampers.
* **Double-Flush:** Patient bodily secretions should be double-flushed in the commode to prevent environmental contamination and tissue damage to others.
* **Preparation:** Nurses should generally have pharmacists prepare chemotherapeutic dosages under sterile, shielded conditions.
