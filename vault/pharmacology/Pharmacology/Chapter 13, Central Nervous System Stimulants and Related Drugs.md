# Chapter 13, Central Nervous System Stimulants and Related Drugs

To master the pharmacology of Central Nervous System (CNS) stimulants and related agents, a student must understand the distinct therapeutic categories, their specific mechanisms of action, and the critical safety nuances associated with each.

### **1. Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) Medications**

* **Main Points:**
* **First-Line Therapy:** CNS stimulants are the primary treatment for ADHD. These include amphetamines (e.g., Dextroamphetamine) and phenidates (e.g., Methylphenidate).
* **Non-Stimulant Alternatives:** Atomoxetine is a selective norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor (SNRI) used for ADHD.
* **Legal Classification:** Most CNS stimulants used for ADHD are Schedule II controlled substances due to their high potential for abuse and physical/psychological dependence.


* **Nuances to Master:**
* **Pediatric vs. Adult Dosing:** For a 6-year-old, the standard Methylphenidate (Ritalin) dose is 5 mg before breakfast and lunch. In contrast, an adult dose is typically 20 mg after lunch, and Methylphenidate (Concerta) can be given as a 50 mg single dose for adults.
* **Safety Warnings:** Atomoxetine carries a specific warning for increased suicidal tendencies in children and adolescents. Unlike stimulants, it has a low risk of insomnia and is not considered addictive.
* **Physiological Effects:** Amphetamines increase heart rate, block the reuptake of dopamine, and enhance the effects of norepinephrine.
* **Drug Holidays:** Patients are often advised to take "breaks" (at least one day per week) from stimulant medications to help prevent addiction and mitigate tolerance.



### **2. Anorexiants and Obesity Management**

* **Main Points:**
* **Mechanism Categories:** Medications like Orlistat work peripherally by inhibiting the pancreatic enzyme lipase to reduce fat absorption. Others, like Phentermine, are sympathomimetic anorexiants that act on the CNS to suppress appetite.


* **Nuances to Master:**
* **Side Effect Profiles:** Orlistat is associated with unique gastrointestinal distress, specifically oily stools and fecal incontinence.
* **Administration Timing:** Stimulant-based anorexiants (e.g., Phentermine) should be taken in the morning. They must be avoided within 4 to 6 hours of bedtime to prevent interference with sleep.
* **Contraindications:** These drugs are contraindicated in patients with glaucoma, severe cardiovascular disease, uncontrolled hypertension, hyperthyroidism, mental agitation, eating disorders (anorexia/bulimia), and those who have used MAOIs within the last 14 days.



### **3. Antimigraine Medications**

* **Main Points:**
* **Triptans:** Serotonin receptor agonists (e.g., Sumatriptan) are used for acute migraine relief.
* **Ergot Alkaloids:** Drugs like Ergotamine tartrate are used for chronic or vascular headaches.
* **Clinical Presentation:** A student must recognize the migraine profile: unilateral headache, photophobia, nausea, and vomiting.


* **Nuances to Master:**
* **Vasoconstriction:** Ergot alkaloids exert their effect by blocking alpha-2 receptors, which causes vasoconstriction of dilated cerebral vessels. They do not cause vasodilation.
* **Side Effects:** Triptans are known to potentially cause a foul taste in the mouth.



### **4. Narcolepsy and Wakefulness-Promoting Agents**

* **Main Points:**
* **Hallmark Symptoms:** Mastery of narcolepsy includes recognizing cataplexy (sudden skeletal muscle weakness) and unexpected sleep episodes.
* **Pharmacotherapy:** Modafinil is a psychostimulant used to improve wakefulness in patients with excessive daytime sleepiness.


* **Nuances to Master:**
* **Benefits:** Beyond wakefulness, Modafinil is noted for its ability to brighten mood and enhance memory.



### **5. Analeptics and Respiratory Stimulants**

* **Main Points:**
* **Doxapram:** Used to treat respiratory depression by increasing both the rate and depth of respiration.
* **Caffeine:** Acts as a respiratory stimulant, particularly useful in neonatal care.


* **Nuances to Master:**
* **Neonatal Safety:** Doxapram is strictly contraindicated in newborns because it contains benzyl alcohol, which can cause "gasping syndrome."
* **Caffeine Indications:** It is used to minimize breathing difficulties in infants with bronchopulmonary dysplasia resulting from high-pressure mechanical ventilation.
* **Caffeine Contraindications:** It must be avoided or used with extreme caution in patients with peptic ulcers, cardiac dysrhythmias, or recent myocardial infarction.
* **High-Dose Toxicity:** Overdose can lead to vagal stimulation resulting in diarrhea and reflex tachycardia, as well as reduced deep tendon reflexes.
* **Dosage Mastery (Specific Strengths):**
* Caffeine (NoDoz): 100 mg per tablet.
* Vivarin: 200 mg per tablet.
* Fioricet (used for migraines): 40 mg of caffeine.
* Anacin: 32 mg of caffeine.





### **6. Mastery of Action Sites**

A student must be able to match the drug class to its primary site of action:

* **Cerebral Cortex:** Amphetamines, Phenidates, Modafinil, and Armodafinil.
* **Medulla and Brainstem:** Analeptics (respiratory stimulants).
* **Hypothalamic and Limbic Regions:** Anorexiants (appetite suppressants).
* **Cerebrovascular System:** Serotonin agonists (Triptans).

### **7. Herbal Interactions and Safety Omissions**

* **Ginseng:** Must be monitored closely when taken with Metformin, as it can cause drug interactions affecting blood glucose management.
* **Ginkgo Biloba:** Should be avoided with Aspirin and Warfarin due to increased risks involving blood clotting and complications.
* **Guarana:** Can interact negatively with Disulfiram.

# Chapter 13, Central Nervous System Stimulants and Related Drugs

To master this topic, a student must understand the pharmacological management of ADHD, narcolepsy, obesity, migraines, and respiratory depression. Below are the essential main points and the sophisticated nuances required for proficiency.

### Main Points by Therapeutic Category

**1. ADHD and Narcolepsy (CNS Stimulants)**

* **Clinical Presentation:** ADHD is characterized by a developmentally inappropriate attention span, hyperactivity, and impulsivity. Diagnosis requires symptoms to persist for at least 6 months, occur in at least two different settings (e.g., home and school), and typically have an onset before age 7. Narcolepsy involves excessive daytime sleepiness and cataplexy (sudden skeletal muscle weakness).
* **Key Medications:** * **Amphetamines/Phenidates:** Methylphenidate, amphetamine aspartate, and dextroamphetamine. These are Schedule II controlled substances with high abuse potential.
* **Non-Stimulants:** Atomoxetine is used for ADHD but lacks addictive properties and is not a controlled substance.
* **Wakefulness-Promoting Agents:** Modafinil and armodafinil are first-line for narcolepsy and shift work sleep disorder.


* **Side Effects:** Common effects include insomnia, headache, weight loss, decreased appetite, and increased heart rate/blood pressure.

**2. Anorexiants (Obesity Management)**

* **Mechanism:** These drugs suppress appetite. Phentermine is a CNS stimulant structurally related to amphetamines. Orlistat is a unique non-stimulant that works as a lipase inhibitor, blocking the absorption of dietary fat.
* **Clinical Goal:** Anorexiants should never be used as monotherapy; they must be accompanied by diet and exercise behavioral modifications.

**3. Antimigraine Drugs (SSRAs and Ergot Alkaloids)**

* **Selective Serotonin Receptor Agonists (SSRAs/Triptans):** Drugs like sumatriptan and zolmitriptan are first-line abortive therapies. They work by stimulating receptors in cerebral arteries, causing vasoconstriction.
* **Ergot Alkaloids:** Ergotamine is an older class of drug used for migraines, though it is no longer considered first-line.

**4. Analeptics (Respiratory Stimulants)**

* **Indications:** Used for neonatal apnea, drug-induced respiratory depression, and postanesthetic respiratory depression.
* **Specific Agents:** Caffeine (a methylxanthine) and doxapram. Caffeine stimulates the area of the brain that senses carbon dioxide and relaxes bronchial smooth muscle.

### Nuances for Mastery

**Medication Administration and Timing**

* **ADHD Meds:** To maximize absorption, these should be taken on an empty stomach, 30 to 45 minutes before meals. To prevent insomnia, the last dose of the day should be administered 4 to 6 hours before bedtime.
* **Drug Holidays:** Specialists often recommend periodic "drug holidays" (e.g., weekends or school breaks) to decrease the risk of addiction and maintain drug sensitivity.
* **Nasal Spray Technique:** For sumatriptan nasal spray, the patient should gently blow their nose first, occlude one nostril, insert the nozzle, and spray while inhaling. Crucially, they should breathe in through the nose and out through the mouth for 10 to 20 seconds afterward to prevent the medication from being exhaled.

**Contraindications and Safety Warnings**

* **Anorexiant Contraindications:** These drugs are strictly contraindicated in patients with glaucoma, hyperthyroidism, severe hypertension, eating disorders (bulimia/anorexia), and those who have used MAOIs within the last 14 days.
* **Analeptic Contraindications:** Caffeine and other analeptics are contraindicated in patients with peptic ulcer disease (due to increased gastric acid secretion) and serious cardiovascular conditions like end-stage heart failure or recent myocardial infarction.
* **Sumatriptan Limits:** Patients should not exceed two injections/doses of sumatriptan in a 24-hour period. Changes in mental status (confusion/slurred speech) after administration may indicate a stroke rather than a migraine and require immediate consultation.

**Drug-Drug and Drug-Herb Interactions**

* **Serotonin Syndrome:** There is a significant risk of Serotonin Syndrome (restlessness, confusion, tachycardia, hypertension) if triptans are taken within two weeks of ergot alkaloids.
* **MAOIs:** CNS stimulants and anorexiants like phentermine can cause a hypertensive crisis if taken with monoamine oxidase inhibitors.
* **Herbal Interactions:** Ginseng can interact with the antidiabetic drug metformin. Ginkgo biloba can affect blood clotting when taken with warfarin or aspirin.

**Nutritional Nuances**

* **Orlistat Side Effects:** Because it blocks fat absorption, it can cause "oily spotting," flatulence, and fecal incontinence. These are managed by limiting dietary fat intake to less than 30% of total calories.
* **Vitamin Deficiency:** Orlistat reduces the absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, and K) and beta-carotene. Patients often need supplements and should increase intake of foods like carrots.

**Legal and Addictive Classifications**

* **Schedule II:** Amphetamines and methylphenidate (high abuse potential).
* **Schedule IV:** Modafinil and phentermine (lower, but still present, abuse potential).
* **Non-Controlled:** Atomoxetine and Orlistat.
