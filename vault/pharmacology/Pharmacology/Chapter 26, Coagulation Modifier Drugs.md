# Chapter 26, Coagulation Modifier Drugs

To master the pharmacology of coagulation modifier drugs, a student must understand the different classes of medications, their mechanisms of action, specific monitoring requirements, and the critical safety precautions associated with each.

### Main Points

* **Anticoagulants (Heparin and LMWHs):**
* **Unfractionated Heparin:** Administered intravenously or subcutaneously. It requires frequent monitoring of the activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT). The primary antidote for heparin overdose is protamine sulfate.
* **Low-Molecular-Weight Heparins (LMWHs) like Enoxaparin and Dalteparin:** These have a more predictable response than heparin and do not require routine laboratory monitoring. They are administered subcutaneously, typically in the abdomen.
* **Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT):** A serious complication where platelet counts drop significantly. If HIT occurs, heparin must be stopped immediately, and a different class of anticoagulant, such as Argatroban, is usually initiated.


* **Oral Anticoagulants (Warfarin and DOACs):**
* **Warfarin:** Inhibits vitamin K synthesis. It is monitored using the Prothrombin Time (PT) and International Normalized Ratio (INR). The antidote is Vitamin K (Phytonadione).
* **Dabigatran:** A direct thrombin inhibitor used for conditions like atrial fibrillation. It is renally excreted and requires dose adjustments based on creatinine clearance.


* **Antiplatelet Drugs:**
* **Aspirin:** Used for stroke and MI prevention. Common side effects include nausea and GI distress. It is contraindicated in patients with vitamin K deficiency or active bleeding.
* **Clopidogrel:** Often used when aspirin is not tolerated; side effects can include edema, itching, and chest pain.


* **Thrombolytic Drugs (Alteplase, Tenecteplase):**
* These drugs work by dissolving existing clots (lysing) rather than just preventing new ones. They are used for acute MI, arterial embolism, and acute ischemic stroke.


* **Antifibrinolytic Drugs (Aminocaproic Acid):**
* These promote blood clotting by preventing the breakdown of fibrin. They are used to prevent excessive bleeding during surgery or in certain medical conditions.



### Nuances to Master

* **Laboratory Monitoring and Therapeutic Ranges:**
* **aPTT (Heparin):** The target therapeutic level is typically 1.5 to 2.5 times the control value (usually 45 to 70 seconds).
* **INR (Warfarin):** A therapeutic INR is generally between 2.0 and 3.0. For mechanical heart valves, the target may be higher (up to 3.5). An INR of 1.0 is considered subtherapeutic for someone on warfarin.
* **Platelets:** Monitoring for thrombocytopenia is essential for all anticoagulants and antiplatelets. A platelet count below 100,000/mm³ is a significant red flag.


* **Dietary and Herbal Interactions:**
* **Warfarin and Vitamin K:** Students must understand that patients do not need to avoid green leafy vegetables entirely; instead, they must maintain a *consistent* daily intake so the warfarin dose can be adjusted accordingly.
* **Herbal Supplements:** Ginkgo, garlic, and ginger can increase the risk of bleeding when taken with anticoagulants.
* **Drug Interactions:** Medications like amiodarone and cimetidine increase warfarin's effect, while others like carbamazepine can decrease it.


* **Specific Storage and Administration Requirements:**
* **Dabigatran Storage:** It is highly sensitive to moisture. It must be kept in its original manufacturer's bottle or blister pack with the desiccant-drying agent. It should never be placed in a daily pill organizer.
* **Enoxaparin Technique:** Must be injected into the subcutaneous tissue of the abdomen. The site should never be rubbed after injection as this can cause hematoma formation.
* **Argatroban Route:** This medication is only administered via the intravenous route.


* **Critical Safety Assessments:**
* **Mental Status:** When a patient is on thrombolytics (like Alteplase), any change in mental status is the highest priority because it may indicate a life-threatening intracranial hemorrhage.
* **Renal Function:** Drugs like Dabigatran and Fondaparinux are contraindicated or require significant dose reductions in patients with low creatinine clearance (generally below 30 mL/min).
* **Body Weight:** Fondaparinux is contraindicated in patients weighing less than 50 kg due to an increased risk of bleeding.


* **Antidote Specificity:**
* Protamine sulfate is specific for heparin/LMWH, while Vitamin K is specific for warfarin. Vitamin K will *not* reverse the effects of heparin or thrombolytics.
