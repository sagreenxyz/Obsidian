# Chapter 36, Antihistamines, Decongestants, Antitussives, and Expectorants

To master the pharmacology of upper respiratory medications, a student must understand the distinct mechanisms, indications, and safety profiles of four primary drug classes, as well as specific herbal supplements and patient-specific contraindications.

### 1. Antihistamines (H1 Receptor Antagonists)

Antihistamines work by competing with histamine for specific receptor sites. They are primarily used for allergic rhinitis, urticaria, and motion sickness.

* **First-Generation (Traditional):** Drugs like diphenhydramine and promethazine cross the blood-brain barrier. They have high sedative properties and significant anticholinergic effects.
* **Second-Generation (Nonsedating/Peripheral):** Drugs like fexofenadine, loratadine, and cetirizine work peripherally and do not readily cross the blood-brain barrier, resulting in little to no sedation.
* **Key Nuances:**
* **The "Drying Effect":** Antihistamines exert anticholinergic effects (dry mouth, urinary retention, constipation). This drying effect is therapeutically useful for runny noses but dangerous for asthma patients, as it can thicken secretions, leading to atelectasis (lung collapse).
* **Paradoxical Reactions:** While typically sedating, antihistamines can cause CNS stimulation (agitation, insomnia) in children and the elderly.
* **Allergy Testing:** All antihistamines must be discontinued at least four days before allergy skin testing to avoid false-negative results.
* **Safety:** Patients should avoid driving when starting traditional antihistamines. Taking these drugs with food can help minimize GI upset.



### 2. Decongestants

These drugs are primarily sympathomimetics (adrenergics) that cause vasoconstriction in the nasal mucosa to reduce swelling.

* **Topical vs. Oral:** Topical sprays (like oxymetazoline) work quickly but carry a high risk of **rebound congestion** if used for more than 3 to 5 days. Oral forms have no rebound risk but cause systemic effects.
* **Contraindications:** Because they are sympathomimetics, they are contraindicated in patients with hypertension, hyperthyroidism, cardiac disease, and narrow-angle glaucoma.
* **Nuances:**
* **Side Effects:** Students must monitor for palpitations, insomnia, tremors, and increased blood pressure.
* **Visualization:** They are sometimes used before nasal surgery to shrink membranes and improve the surgeon's view.



### 3. Antitussives

These medications suppress the cough reflex by acting on the cough center in the medulla.

* **Opioid Antitussives:** Codeine and hydrocodone. They are highly effective but carry risks of respiratory depression and constipation. They are contraindicated in patients with impaired renal function or increased intracranial pressure.
* **Non-opioid Antitussives:** Dextromethorphan and benzonatate. Dextromethorphan is common in OTC products but should not be taken with MAOIs (like selegiline) due to the risk of toxicity.
* **Nuances:**
* **Appropriate Use:** Antitussives should only be used for dry, non-productive coughs or when coughing is harmful to the patient (e.g., after a hernia repair to prevent abdominal strain).
* **Safety:** Benzonatate and dextromethorphan can still cause dizziness and drowsiness; patients must be cautioned against activities requiring mental alertness.



### 4. Expectorants

Expectorants, such as guaifenesin, thin and loosen respiratory secretions by increasing the amount of fluid in the respiratory tract.

* **Main Goal:** To make a productive cough more efficient so secretions can be removed.
* **Nuances:**
* **Hydration:** The most critical teaching point is for the patient to increase fluid intake (ideally to 3000 mL/day) to help the drug liquefy secretions.
* **Pregnancy:** Guaifenesin is a Pregnancy Category C drug and is often considered safer for respiratory congestion in pregnancy than many antihistamines (which are Category B but often contraindicated for simple congestion).



### 5. Herbal Supplements

* **Echinacea:** Used to stimulate the immune system. It can cause dizziness, headache, and dermatitis. It is contraindicated in patients with immunosuppression or autoimmune diseases like multiple sclerosis.
* **Goldenseal:** Used for upper respiratory infections. It is a uterine stimulant and is strictly contraindicated in pregnancy. It also decreases the effectiveness of antihypertensive medications and can cause GI distress.

### 6. Critical Nursing Considerations and Patient Safety

* **Elderly Patients:** This population is at higher risk for the "hangover effect" and sedation from antihistamines, which significantly increases fall risks.
* **Pediatric Patients:** OTC cough and cold products should be used with extreme caution. Toxicities can lead to seizures, tachycardia, and loss of consciousness.
* **Disease-Specific Contraindications:**
* **Glaucoma:** Avoid antihistamines and decongestants.
* **Asthma:** Avoid antihistamines due to the risk of thickened secretions.
* **Hypertension/Cardiac Disease:** Avoid decongestants and certain antitussives that might cause systemic stimulation.


* **Assessment:** Always assess breath sounds, cough characteristics (productive vs. non-productive), and the nature of sputum (color/viscosity) before and after administering these medications. A fever or change in sputum color during treatment should be reported to a provider immediately.
