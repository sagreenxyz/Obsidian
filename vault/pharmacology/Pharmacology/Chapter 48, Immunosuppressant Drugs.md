# Chapter 48, Immunosuppressant Drugs

### Main Points for Mastery

**Core Therapeutic Principles**

* **Lifelong Commitment:** Patients must understand that immunosuppressant therapy is typically lifelong to prevent organ rejection.
* **Adherence and Timing:** Medications must be taken at the exact same time every day to maintain therapeutic blood levels. If a dose is missed, it should be taken as soon as remembered unless it is nearly time for the next dose; the transplant physician must be notified.
* **Administration with Food:** These drugs should be taken with food to minimize gastrointestinal (GI) upset. Consistency is key—the patient should use the same type of food each time to ensure predictable absorption.
* **Prevention vs. Treatment:** While many drugs are used for prevention, only a specific subset is indicated for both the prevention and treatment of organ rejection: **Muromonab-CD3**, **Mycophenolate Mofetil**, and **Tacrolimus**.

**Infection Control and Safety**

* **Immunocompromised State:** Patients are highly susceptible to opportunistic infections. They must avoid crowds, people with active infections (like the flu), and any live vaccines.
* **Symptom Reporting:** Any sign of infection—such as fever, chills, or sore throat—requires immediate notification of the healthcare provider.
* **Oral Health:** Patients should regularly inspect their mouths for white patches, which indicate oral candidiasis (a common fungal infection during therapy).

**Drug-Specific Essentials**

* **Cyclosporine:** Has a very narrow therapeutic range and requires frequent laboratory monitoring to prevent toxicity while ensuring efficacy.
* **Azathioprine:** Primarily associated with hematologic side effects, specifically leukopenia and thrombocytopenia (low platelet counts).
* **Mycophenolate Mofetil:** Carries an FDA black box warning for increased risk of congenital malformations and spontaneous abortions.
* **Tacrolimus:** Can lead to the development of post-transplant diabetes mellitus, particularly in patients with pre-existing impaired glucose tolerance.
* **Multiple Sclerosis (MS) Drugs:** Fingolimod and Glatiramer Acetate are unique immunosuppressants specifically used to reduce the frequency of relapses in relapsing-remitting MS.

### Nuances for Advanced Mastery

**Pharmacokinetics and Interactions**

* **The "4-Hour Rule" for Sirolimus:** When prescribed with Cyclosporine, Sirolimus must be taken 4 hours after the Cyclosporine dose because Cyclosporine can interfere with its absorption.
* **St. John’s Wort:** This herbal supplement is strictly contraindicated with Cyclosporine as it induces enzymes that reduce the drug's therapeutic efficacy, leading to potential organ rejection.
* **Grapefruit Juice:** This must be avoided entirely as it interacts with many immunosuppressants, potentially increasing drug concentrations to toxic levels.
* **Allopurinol and Azathioprine:** Allopurinol (used for gout) decreases the metabolism of Azathioprine. Consequently, Azathioprine is contraindicated for use with Allopurinol due to the risk of severe toxicity.

**Advanced Administration Nuances**

* **Cyclosporine Mixing:** Oral solutions should be mixed in glass containers with milk, chocolate milk, or orange juice. They should **never** be mixed or stored in polystyrene (Styrofoam) cups, as the drug will adhere to the walls of the container. These solutions should be consumed immediately after mixing and never refrigerated.
* **Non-Interchangeability:** Cyclosporine and modified cyclosporine (e.g., Gengraf or Neoral) have different pharmacokinetics and are not interchangeable.
* **Injectable Constraints:** Glatiramer acetate is only available in injectable form. It is often dissolved in mannitol; therefore, a mannitol allergy is a contraindication.
* **IV to Oral Transition:** For Tacrolimus, when transitioning from IV to oral maintenance, the first oral dose is typically administered 8 to 12 hours after the IV infusion is stopped.

**Clinical Monitoring and Complications**

* **Cytokine Release Syndrome (CRS):** This is a severe, allergy-like reaction (potentially involving anaphylaxis) associated with Muromonab-CD3 and Basiliximab. To prevent this, patients are frequently premedicated with IV corticosteroids (like methylprednisolone).
* **Pre-Treatment Assessment:** Before starting therapy, baseline renal function (BUN, creatinine), hepatic function (ALT, AST, ALP), and cardiovascular status (blood pressure, heart rate) must be established, as these drugs can cause nephrotoxicity, hepatotoxicity, and hypertension.
* **Contraceptive Nuances:** For patients on Mycophenolate Mofetil, oral progesterone-only pills may interact with the drug. Patients should be advised to consult a gynecologist for non-hormonal or more reliable contraceptive methods and must continue using them for at least 12 weeks after therapy ends.
* **Dietary Restrictions for Liver Transplants:** Following a liver transplant, patients should avoid high-potassium foods (like bananas) and high-fat foods (like cheeseburgers) to protect the new organ and manage drug side effects.
