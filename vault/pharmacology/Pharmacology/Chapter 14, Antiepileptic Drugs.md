# Chapter 14, Antiepileptic Drugs

To master the topic of Antiepileptic Drugs (AEDs), a student must understand both the broad therapeutic principles and the highly specific pharmacological details of individual medications.

### Core Knowledge and Main Points

* **Goal of Therapy:** The primary objective is to control or maximally reduce seizure activity while maintaining the patient's quality of life by minimizing medication side effects. It is not necessarily to eradicate all seizures if the side effects become intolerable, nor is it to simply maximize dosages.
* **Mechanism of Action:** AEDs work by altering the movement of electrolytes (sodium, potassium, calcium, and magnesium) across nerve cell membranes. This results in more stabilized and less excitable cell membranes, which limits the spread of electrical discharges by suppressing the transmission of impulses between nerves.
* **Therapeutic Principles:** * Medication is specifically matched to the patient's seizure type.
* Single-drug therapy (monotherapy) must fail before multiple-drug therapy is attempted.
* Therapy is typically lifelong unless the drugs are being used prophylactically.


* **Standard Safety Protocols:** Regular monitoring of serum drug levels is required to ensure the patient remains within the therapeutic range and to prevent drug toxicity.

### Nuances and Critical Mastery

* **Drug-Specific Side Effects and Monitoring:**
* **Phenytoin (Dilantin):** Students must know that it causes gingival hyperplasia (overgrowth of gum tissue). Mastery involves knowing the intervention: frequent oral hygiene and regular dental visits to minimize this effect.
* **Lamotrigine (Lamictal):** A unique requirement is the need for visual acuity exams because vision changes and blurry vision are common adverse effects.
* **Carbamazepine (Tegretol):** This drug is unique for its "autoinduction" of hepatic enzymes, which usually occurs within the first 2 months of starting therapy.
* **Valproic Acid:** Therapeutic success is measured by the absence of seizures, but students must monitor for side effects like elevated liver enzymes.


* **Administration Nuances:**
* **Intravenous Onset Timing:** There is a critical difference in onset times between IV AEDs. Diazepam has an immediate onset (making it the drug of choice for status epilepticus), whereas IV Phenytoin has a much slower onset of 1 to 2 hours.
* **Phenytoin IV Push:** Because it is highly irritating to the veins, it must be pushed slowly at a rate not exceeding 50 mg/min and must be administered with a filter. It is never given intramuscularly.
* **Pediatric Considerations:** For children who cannot swallow pills, the suspension form is used. Students must master the preparation: shake the container well and use an oral syringe for precision, as household teaspoons are inaccurate.


* **Dietary Contraindications:** * **Grapefruit juice** must be strictly avoided with Carbamazepine as it leads to drug toxicity.
* **Milk and carbonated beverages** should be eliminated from the diet when taking Valproic acid.


* **Indication Specificity:** Mastery involves knowing which drug fits which seizure:
* **Succinimides (Ethosuximide):** Only for uncomplicated, generalized absence seizures.
* **Diazepam:** The standard for status epilepticus.
* **Lamotrigine:** Indicated for Lennox–Gastaut syndrome and focal seizures.
* **Gabapentin/Pregabalin:** Primarily used for partial/focal seizures.

# Chapter 14, Antiepileptic Drugs

To master the pharmacology of antiepileptic drugs (AEDs), a student must understand both the broad physiological principles of seizure management and the highly specific clinical requirements for individual agents.

### Core Principles and Mechanisms

* **Mechanism of Action:** AEDs do not simply "suppress the brain." They work by altering the movement of electrolytes (sodium, potassium, calcium, and magnesium) across nerve cell membranes. This results in more stabilized, less excitable membranes, which limits the spread of electrical discharge.
* **Adherence and Consistency:** Maintaining steady serum drug levels is the most critical factor in preventing seizure recurrence. Medications must be taken at the same time every day. Low blood levels typically indicate non-compliance rather than drug failure.
* **Discontinuation Risks:** AED therapy must never be stopped abruptly. Doing so can precipitate rebound seizure activity or status epilepticus. If a medication must be changed (e.g., due to hepatotoxicity), the old drug is tapered off while the new one is introduced.
* **Generic vs. Brand Name:** Patients must be monitored closely when switching between brand-name and generic preparations, as slight variations in bioavailability can cause a drop in therapeutic levels and lead to breakthrough seizures.

### Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM)

Mastery of specific therapeutic ranges is essential for identifying toxicity or subtherapeutic dosing:

* **Phenytoin:** 10 to 20 mcg/mL.
* **Carbamazepine:** 4 to 12 mcg/mL.
* **Phenobarbital:** 10 to 40 mcg/mL.
* **Valproic Acid:** 50 to 125 mcg/mL.
* **Levetiracetam:** 12 to 46 mcg/mL.
* **Primidone:** 5 to 12 mcg/mL.

### Key Individual Drug Profiles

* **Phenytoin:**
* **Side Effects:** Lethargy, cognitive changes, and "Dilantin facies" (hypertrophy of subcutaneous facial tissue).
* **Long-term Effects:** Gingival hyperplasia (enlarged gums) requiring meticulous oral care, and hirsutism (excessive hair growth).
* **Adverse Effects:** Agranulocytosis (monitor for persistent upper respiratory infections) and nystagmus (a sign of toxicity).
* **Contraindications:** High risk of fetal defects; women of childbearing age must use reliable birth control.


* **Carbamazepine:** Often the first-line treatment for generalized tonic-clonic and partial seizures, as well as trigeminal neuralgia. It interacts with grapefruit juice and verapamil.
* **Valproic Acid:** Used for absence, myoclonic, and tonic-clonic seizures. It carries high risks for hepatotoxicity (elevated liver enzymes) and pancreatitis. Patients should avoid taking it with milk or carbonated beverages to prevent early mucosal irritation.
* **Lamotrigine:** Indicated for Lennox-Gastaut syndrome. Its most serious risk is Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS), a potentially fatal skin-sloughing reaction. Doses must be titrated very slowly to minimize this risk.
* **Gabapentin:** Commonly used for neuropathic pain. The priority assessment is a thorough neurologic baseline (vision, sensory, motor functioning).
* **Levetiracetam:** Unique for its risk of neuropsychiatric symptoms, including suicidal ideation, hostility, and agitation.
* **Diazepam:** The drug of choice for status epilepticus due to its immediate onset when given intravenously.

### Nuances of Administration and Safety

* **Intravenous Phenytoin:** This is a high-alert procedure. It is very irritating to veins and incompatible with almost all fluids except normal saline. It must be given through a large-bore catheter (20-gauge or larger), filtered, and administered no faster than 50 mg/min to prevent cardiovascular collapse.
* **Fosphenytoin vs. Phenytoin:** Fosphenytoin is a water-soluble prodrug of phenytoin. It is preferred for IV use because it is better tolerated, causes less burning, and can be given at faster rates. It is the choice when a patient is NPO and requires IV maintenance.
* **Status Epilepticus Management:** In an acute seizure emergency, the nurse’s first priority is ensuring a patent intravenous line and monitoring oxygenation.
* **Drug Interactions:** Most AEDs are metabolized via the CYP450 enzyme pathway. Barbiturates and hydantoins often interfere with oral contraceptives, rendering them ineffective. Alcohol must be strictly avoided as it causes additive CNS depression.
* **Safety Precautions:** Patients starting new AEDs should refrain from driving or operating machinery until they know how the drug affects them (usually for about 6 months). They should also be taught to wear a medical alert bracelet.
* **Suicide Risk:** All patients receiving AEDs must be monitored for changes in mental status, as these medications carry an increased risk for suicidal tendencies.
