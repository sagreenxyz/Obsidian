# Chapter 8, Gene Therapy and Pharmacogenomics

### Fundamental Biological Concepts

* **DNA Structure:** Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) is composed of organic bases linked by a sugar molecule called deoxyribose. Its primary function in the human cell is protein synthesis.
* **Nucleic Acids:** There are two types of nucleic acid molecules: DNA and ribonucleic acid (RNA). While DNA carries genetic material for most organisms, RNA carries the genetic material for some viruses, such as HIV.
* **Genome:** This refers to the entirety of an organism's genetic material.
* **Alleles and Genes:** An allele is an alternative form of a gene that varies according to a specific trait.
* **Genotype vs. Phenotype:** The genotype is the specific combination of alleles at a given site on a chromosome, while the phenotype is the actual physical manifestation of that genetic combination.
* **Chromosomes:** Human somatic cells contain 23 pairs of chromosomes. Sex chromosomes determine gender: XX for females and XY for males. One member of each pair is inherited from the father’s sperm and the other from the mother’s egg.

### Protein Synthesis and Transcription

* **The Process:** Synthesis occurs in a specific sequence:
1. Uncoiling of the DNA strand.
2. Transcription: A strand of messenger RNA (mRNA) forms on the uncoiled DNA template.
3. mRNA enters the cytoplasm to be read by ribosomal RNA (rRNA), which codes sequences for specific amino acids.
4. Transfer RNA (tRNA) transports the corresponding amino acids to the site of ribosomal translation to create proteins.



### The Human Genome Project (HGP)

* **Goals:** Coordinated by government groups between 1990 and 2003, the HGP identified approximately 30,000 human genes and 3 billion base pairs.
* **Impact:** It developed tools for genetic data analysis and storage, transferred technologies to the private sector, and addressed ethical, legal, and social issues (ELSI) related to genetic research.

### Principles of Genetic Disease and Inheritance

* **Genetic Disease:** A general term for any disease caused by a genetic mechanism. Not all genetic diseases are inherited; some are acquired through mutations or external factors.
* **Genetic Predisposition:** This means an individual has specific genes that increase the likelihood of developing a disease. Crucially, lifestyle choices can often offset these risks.
* **Inherited vs. Environmental Factors:** Some disorders, like Hemophilia (a sex-linked single-gene disorder), are determined solely by inheritance. Others, such as cancer, diabetes, heart disease, and Alzheimer’s, result from a complex relationship between genetics and environmental factors.
* **Sex-Linked Traits:** These are carried on the X or Y chromosomes and are passed differently to male and female offspring. For example, the gene for Hemophilia is carried by females but typically only manifests as a disorder in males.

### Gene Therapy

* **Experimental Nature:** Gene therapy is currently an experimental technique used to treat or prevent disease by inserting genes into a patient's cells instead of using drugs or surgery.
* **Approaches:**
* Replacing a mutated gene with a healthy copy.
* Inactivating (knocking out) a mutated gene that is functioning improperly.
* Introducing a new gene to help the body fight a disease.


* **rDNA Technology:** An indirect, well-established form of gene therapy used to synthesize biologics. For example, the genome of Escherichia coli is used to manufacture human insulin. Other products include vaccines, hormones, antitoxins, and monoclonal antibodies.
* **Vectors:** Viruses are often used as "vectors" to insert therapeutic genes into cells. For instance, in treating adenosine deaminase (ADA) deficiency, a viral vector carries the gene into lymphocytes.
* **Limitations and Risks:** Risks include the induction of viral disease, immunogenic reactions in the host, and the potential for the patient to develop other life-threatening illnesses like cancer. The beneficial effects are often temporary, requiring retreatment.

### Pharmacogenomics and Pharmacogenetics

* **Definitions:** Pharmacogenomics involves using a patient's entire genetic makeup to individualize drug therapy. Pharmacogenetics focuses specifically on how single gene variations affect drug response.
* **Key Drug-Gene Interactions:**
* **Warfarin:** Patients with variations in the CYP2C9 allele are "slow metabolizers" and require lower doses to prevent life-threatening bleeding.
* **Antidepressants:** Metabolized by the CYP2D6 isoenzyme. Testing determines if a patient is a poor, intermediate, extensive, or ultrarapid metabolizer to guide dosing.
* **Trastuzumab:** Genotyping for the HER2/neu protooncogene is required to determine if a breast cancer patient is suitable for this therapy.
* **Digoxin:** Genotyping for p-glycoprotein assists in drug selection and dosing.
* **6-mercaptopurine:** Genotyping for thiopurine methyltransferase helps reduce toxicity in pediatric leukemia patients.
* **Beta Blockers and Mood Stabilizers:** Must be used cautiously in patients with CYP2D6 polymorphisms.
* **Oral Contraceptives:** Women with a Factor V gene mutation have a significantly higher risk of developing thrombosis when taking these.


* **Testing Technologies:** DNA chip and microarray tests identify risks for inherited diseases in advance and help predict a patient's response to specific drug therapies.

### Ethical and Regulatory Oversight

* **Prohibited Practices:** Eugenics, specifically the genetic manipulation of germ cells (sperm and egg), is prohibited in the United States.
* **Approval Processes:** All human clinical gene therapy trials must be approved by the FDA. Institutions must also have an Institutional Review Board (IRB) to protect participants and an Institutional Biosafety Committee (IBC).
* **Nursing Ethics:** Nurses must maintain strict patient confidentiality. They are forbidden from notifying a patient's blood relatives of a confirmed genetic risk without the patient's explicit consent.

### The Role of the Professional Nurse

* **Scope of Practice:** Registered nurses should obtain a thorough family history (covering at least three generations) and include both diseases and adverse drug reactions.
* **Generalist vs. Specialist:** While all nurses need basic genomic knowledge, tasks like genetic counseling, developing testing policies, or providing complex testing options are reserved for nurses with specialized training and certification.
* **Patient Advocacy:** Nurses ensure that informed consent has been obtained for genetic testing and support the patient's decision-making process by explaining the nature and consequences of genetic disorders.
* **Education:** Recommendations suggest that genomic education should begin at the undergraduate level and be integrated into both didactic and clinical settings.
