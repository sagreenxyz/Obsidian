# Chapter 19, Adrenergic-Blocking Drugs

To master the topic of adrenergic-blocking drugs, a student must understand the physiological effects, therapeutic applications, and critical safety interventions associated with alpha-blockers and beta-blockers. Below are the main points and nuances derived from the study material.

### Main Points for Mastery

#### 1. Alpha-Blockers: Mechanism and Indications

* **Mechanism:** These drugs cause vasodilation by blocking alpha-adrenergic receptors, leading to decreased peripheral vascular resistance.
* **Primary Uses:** * **Hypertension:** Reducing blood pressure through vasodilation.
* **Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH):** Relaxing smooth muscle in the bladder neck and prostate to increase urinary flow.
* **Pheochromocytoma:** Managing hypertension caused by this adrenal tumor (specifically using Phentolamine).
* **Raynaud’s Disease:** Treating narrowing of arteries and vasoconstriction in extremities (specifically using Phenoxybenzamine).
* **Extravasation:** Phentolamine is the drug of choice to treat tissue necrosis caused by the leakage of vasoconstricting drugs (like dopamine or epinephrine).



#### 2. Beta-Blockers: Mechanism and Selectivity

* **Cardioselective (Beta 1):** Act primarily on the heart to decrease heart rate, conduction, and contractility (e.g., Atenolol, Metoprolol, Esmolol).
* **Nonselective (Beta 1 and Beta 2):** Act on both the heart and the lungs (e.g., Propranolol, Sotalol, Carvedilol, Labetalol).
* **Primary Uses:**
* **Cardiovascular:** Hypertension, angina pectoris, heart failure, and post-myocardial infarction (MI) to increase survival.
* **Dysrhythmias:** Esmolol is used for rapid control of supraventricular tachydysrhythmias.
* **Other:** Propranolol is used for migraines, essential tremors, and pheochromocytoma.



#### 3. Critical Nursing Assessments

* **Pulse:** Always check the **apical pulse** for one full minute before administration. If the pulse is below 60 beats per minute, hold the medication and notify the provider.
* **Blood Pressure:** Monitor both **supine and standing** blood pressures to detect orthostatic hypotension.
* **Weight Monitoring:** Patients must report a weight gain of more than 2 pounds in 24 hours or 5 pounds in one week, as this may indicate developing edema or heart failure.

#### 4. Patient Teaching and Safety

* **Tapering:** Never stop adrenergic-blocking drugs abruptly. This can cause rebound hypertension, chest pain, or a myocardial infarction. Therapy must be tapered over 1 to 2 weeks.
* **Orthostatic Safety:** Patients should change positions slowly and "dangle" at the bedside to prevent fainting (syncope) and falls.
* **First-Dose Phenomenon:** A severe drop in blood pressure can occur with the very first dose of an alpha-blocker; patients should avoid hazardous activities immediately after the initial dose.

### Essential Nuances to Master

#### 1. Respiratory Contraindications

A student must distinguish between selective and nonselective beta-blockers. **Nonselective beta-blockers (like Propranolol or Carvedilol) are strictly contraindicated in patients with asthma or COPD.** Blocking Beta 2 receptors in the lungs causes bronchoconstriction, which can be life-threatening for these patients.

#### 2. Diabetes and "Masked" Hypoglycemia

Beta-blockers can interfere with the body's natural response to low blood sugar. Specifically, they **mask the tachycardia** (rapid heart rate) that usually warns a patient of hypoglycemia. Diabetic patients must be taught to monitor blood glucose more closely and look for other signs like tremors or irritability.

#### 3. Drug-Specific Routes and Interactions

* **Phentolamine for Extravasation:** This must be administered via **subcutaneous injection** around the site of the IV leak, not intravenously or intramuscularly.
* **Sotalol:** Unlike many other cardiac medications, Sotalol is only available in **oral form**.
* **Erectile Dysfunction Drugs:** Taking alpha-blockers (like Tamsulosin or Phentolamine) with medications for erectile dysfunction (like Sildenafil) can cause **profound, life-threatening hypotension**.
* **Warfarin Interaction:** Tamsulosin can compete for protein-binding sites with Warfarin, leading to an increased risk of bleeding.

#### 4. Lifestyle and Environmental Factors

* **Heat Exposure:** Patients must be warned that hot climates, saunas, and hot tubs cause further vasodilation. When combined with adrenergic blockers, this significantly increases the risk of hypotension and fainting.
* **Dietary Irritants:** Patients should avoid **caffeine** (espresso, coffee, chocolate) as it can cause cardiac irritability and counter the effects of the medication.
* **Alcohol:** Alcohol intake should be avoided or strictly moderated because it enhances vasodilation and worsens hypotension.
* **GI Health:** Adrenergic blockers can cause constipation; students should recommend increased fluid and fiber intake rather than medications like aspirin, which can irritate the stomach.
