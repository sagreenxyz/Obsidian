# Chapter 35, Men's Health Drugs

To master the pharmacology of men's health drugs, a student must understand the distinct classes of medications used for testosterone replacement, benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), and erectile dysfunction (ED), along with their critical safety profiles and nursing implications.

### 1. Androgen Therapy and Anabolic Steroids

Androgens, such as testosterone and its derivatives, are primarily used for replacement therapy, while anabolic steroids are used for tissue-wasting conditions.

* **Physiological Roles of Testosterone:** It is responsible for the development of male secondary sexual characteristics, bone and tissue development, and nitrogen retention (anabolic metabolism). Crucially, it stimulates erythropoiesis (the production of red blood cells); thus, low levels can lead to anemia.
* **Administration Nuances:**
* **First-Pass Effect:** Oral testosterone is largely ineffective because it is rapidly metabolized by the liver before reaching systemic circulation.
* **Routes:** Intramuscular injections (e.g., testosterone cypionate) provide a longer duration of action. Transdermal patches for hypogonadism are often applied specifically to the scrotal skin, while reservoir patches should be applied to clean, dry, shaved areas like the back, abdomen, or thighs (avoiding bony areas like the shoulders).


* **Anabolic Steroid Abuse:** Medications like oxymetholone and nandrolone are Schedule III controlled substances. They are frequently abused by athletes to increase muscle mass (hypertrophy).
* **Critical Risks and Monitoring:**
* **Peliosis of the Liver:** Androgens can cause blood-filled cavities to form in the liver. Liver function tests (LFTs) must be monitored.
* **Cardiovascular Health:** These drugs can raise cholesterol levels, requiring lipid panel monitoring. There is a **Black Box Warning** for pulmonary emboli and heart attacks; symptoms like chest pain or dyspnea require immediate notification of a provider.
* **Fluid Retention:** Weight gain during therapy is often actually fluid retention, not muscle mass.



### 2. Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH) Medications

Treatment for BPH involves either shrinking the prostate tissue or relaxing the muscles to improve urine flow.

* **5-Alpha Reductase Inhibitors (Finasteride, Dutasteride):**
* **Mechanism:** They inhibit the enzyme that converts testosterone to DHT, eventually shrinking the prostate.
* **The 6-Month Rule:** Students must know that finasteride takes approximately six months to show therapeutic effects.
* **Handling Precautions:** These drugs are highly teratogenic. Women of childbearing age must wear gloves when handling tablets to prevent skin absorption and fetal abnormalities (often verified via fetal ultrasonography if exposure occurs).
* **Dual Use:** Finasteride is also used to treat male pattern baldness, though it may cause a loss of libido.


* **Alpha 1-Adrenergic Blockers (Tamsulosin, Terazosin):**
* **Mechanism:** These relax the smooth muscle of the prostate and bladder neck to provide immediate symptomatic relief.
* **Nuance:** Tamsulosin is more specific to the prostate and causes less systemic hypotension than terazosin or doxazosin.
* **Safety:** Nurses must assess blood pressure before administration, as these drugs can cause syncope, dizziness, and hypotension.



### 3. Erectile Dysfunction (ED) Medications

ED drugs are primarily phosphodiesterase (PDE) inhibitors that promote vasodilation.

* **The Nitrate Contraindication:** This is the most critical safety point. Sildenafil (Viagra), vardenafil, and tadalafil must **never** be taken with nitrates (e.g., nitroglycerin, isosorbide mononitrate). The synergistic effect causes severe, treatment-resistant hypotension.
* **Dosing and Timing:** Sildenafil should be taken approximately one hour before sexual activity. It is also used to treat pulmonary hypertension.
* **Adverse Effects:**
* **Priapism:** Patients must report any erection lasting longer than four hours to prevent permanent tissue ischemia.
* **Renal/Hepatic Impairment:** Patients with high creatinine levels or liver dysfunction require lower doses.


* **Alternative Agents:** Alprostadil is a prostaglandin analog administered via intraurethral suppository or intracavernous injection for patients who cannot use oral PDE inhibitors.

### 4. General Nursing Mastery and Nuances

* **PSA Levels:** In patients treated for BPH or prostate cancer with finasteride, a successful response is indicated by a decrease in Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA) levels (typically below 2.5 to 3 ng/mL) and a steadier urinary stream.
* **Herbals:** Saw palmetto is a common supplement used for BPH, but it can interfere with finasteride and should be reviewed during the drug history.
* **Baldness Treatments:** While finasteride is used for men, minoxidil (a topical peripheral vasodilator) can be used for baldness in both men and women.
* **Contraindications:** Testosterone therapy is strictly contraindicated in patients with prostate cancer, as it can worsen the disease.
