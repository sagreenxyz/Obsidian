# Chapter 50, Acid-Controlling Drugs

### Main Points for Students

**1. Classification of Acid-Controlling Drugs**

* **Antacids:** Neutralize existing stomach acid but do not prevent its secretion. They are categorized by their primary metallic component: aluminum, magnesium, calcium, or sodium.
* **H2 Receptor Antagonists (H2RAs):** Compete with histamine for binding sites on parietal cells, reducing the volume and concentration of gastric acid (e.g., cimetidine, famotidine).
* **Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs):** Irreversibly bind to the H+/K+ ATPase enzyme system (the "proton pump"), blocking the final step of acid secretion (e.g., omeprazole, lansoprazole).
* **Miscellaneous Agents:** Includes sucralfate (mucosal protectant), misoprostol (prostaglandin analog), and simethicone (antiflatulent).

**2. Pharmacotherapy for Specific Conditions**

* **H. pylori Infection:** The first-line treatment requires a combination therapy for 10–14 days, typically consisting of a PPI and two antibiotics (usually clarithromycin and either amoxicillin or metronidazole).
* **Stress Ulcers:** Critically ill patients (e.g., those on ventilators or in the ICU) are at high risk for stress-related mucosal damage and are routinely prescribed H2RAs or PPIs as prophylaxis.
* **Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD) and Erosive Esophagitis:** PPIs are considered the first-line therapy for the healing and maintenance of these conditions.

**3. Critical Administration Guidelines**

* **Timing:** PPIs should be taken 30 to 60 minutes before meals for optimal absorption and action. Sucralfate must be taken on an empty stomach to be activated by the acidic environment.
* **Drug-Drug Spacing:** Antacids can significantly interfere with the absorption of other medications (e.g., quinolone antibiotics, isoniazid). Generally, other drugs should be administered at least 1 to 2 hours before or after antacids.
* **Tube Administration:** For patients with nasogastric or percutaneous tubes, capsules (like lansoprazole) may be opened and mixed with apple juice, while powders must be thoroughly dissolved in water to prevent tube blockage.

### Nuances to Master

**1. Metallic Side Effects and Contraindications of Antacids**

* **Bowel Impact:** Aluminum and calcium-based antacids cause constipation. Magnesium-based antacids cause diarrhea. Consequently, many products combine aluminum and magnesium to neutralize these effects.
* **Renal Failure:** Magnesium-containing antacids are strictly contraindicated in renal failure due to the risk of hypermagnesemia. Conversely, aluminum-containing antacids are sometimes used in these patients as phosphate binders.
* **Systemic Risks:** Sodium bicarbonate can lead to metabolic alkalosis and should be avoided in patients with heart failure or hypertension due to its high sodium content. Calcium-based antacids carry a risk of kidney stones and "milk-alkali syndrome."

**2. Adverse Effects Specific to Drug Classes**

* **H2RAs:** Cimetidine is unique for its potential to cause endocrine effects, such as gynecomastia and impotence. Famotidine and cimetidine can cause confusion and disorientation, particularly in older adults or the critically ill. Rapid IV infusion of H2RAs can cause hypotension.
* **PPIs:** Long-term use is associated with serious complications, including osteoporosis (increased risk of spine and hip fractures), magnesium depletion, and achlorhydria, which may lead to gastrointestinal infections like C. diff.
* **Misoprostol:** While effective for preventing NSAID-induced ulcers, it is an abortifacient and is strictly contraindicated during pregnancy (Pregnancy Category X).

**3. Factors Affecting Drug Efficacy**

* **Environment:** Sucralfate requires an acidic environment to dissociate and form its protective barrier; therefore, it should not be administered simultaneously with antacids, which raise gastric pH.
* **Dietary Stimulants:** Caffeine and alcohol are potent gastric stimulants that increase hydrochloric acid secretion. Their consumption can render acid-controlling medications, particularly H2RAs, less effective.
* **Enteric Coatings:** Because antacids increase gastric pH, they can cause enteric-coated medications to dissolve prematurely in the stomach rather than the small intestine, leading to GI upset or reduced drug effectiveness.

**4. Clinical Assessments**

* **Organ Function:** Before administering H2RAs like cimetidine, nurses must assess renal and hepatic function (e.g., checking serum creatinine levels), as impaired function increases the risk of adverse effects.
* **Vitals:** During the IV administration of famotidine, blood pressure must be monitored closely to detect sudden hypotension.
* **Nutritional Physiology:** It is essential to understand that intrinsic factor (a glycoprotein) is necessary for the gastric absorption of Vitamin B12. Acid suppression therapy does not typically interfere with this, but long-term PPI use can affect the absorption of minerals like magnesium.
