# Chapter 38, Antibiotics Part 1

Based on the study materials provided, a student must master the following main points and nuances to understand the fundamentals of antibiotic therapy.

### Main Points for Mastery

* **Classifications of Antibiotic Therapy:**
* **Prophylactic Therapy:** Administered as a preventive measure before an infection starts, commonly used prior to high-risk procedures like surgery (e.g., intravenous ceftriaxone before a cholecystectomy).
* **Empiric Therapy:** Treatment initiated after the onset of infection but before the specific causative organism is identified. It is based on the most likely pathogens (e.g., using Ticarcillin-clavulanic acid for serious intra-abdominal infections).
* **Definitive Therapy:** Tailored treatment initiated once the specific pathogen has been identified through culture and sensitivity testing. This approach helps reduce the risk of antibiotic resistance.


* **Mechanisms of Action (MOA):**
* **Penicillins:** These destroy bacteria by interfering with cell wall synthesis. They inhibit transpeptidases and activate autolysins, leading to cell lysis and death.
* **Tetracyclines:** These are bacteriostatic agents that inhibit bacterial protein synthesis by binding to the 30S bacterial ribosome.
* **Sulfonamides:** These provide a synergistic antibacterial effect and achieve high concentrations in the kidneys, making them a primary choice for urinary tract infections (UTIs).


* **Superinfections and Normal Flora:**
* Antibiotics can destroy the body's normal bacterial flora, which usually inhibits the overgrowth of fungi and yeast.
* When this flora is eliminated, organisms like **Clostridium difficile** can overgrow, leading to antibiotic-associated diarrhea characterized by watery stools, abdominal pain, and fever.


* **Specific Drug Indications:**
* **Sulfamethoxazole-trimethoprim (SMZ-TMP):** Primarily used for UTIs and infections caused by organisms like E. coli, Klebsiella, and S. aureus.
* **Erythromycin (Macrolide):** A drug of choice for treating Legionnaires’ disease.
* **Meropenem (Carbapenem):** Effective for bacterial meningitis because it has the ability to cross the blood-brain barrier.
* **Dicloxacillin:** A penicillinase-resistant drug used specifically for infections caused by penicillin-destroying enzymes (penicillinase) produced by bacteria like staphylococci.



### Nuances to be Mastered

* **Pharmacokinetics of Amoxicillin:** It is vital to distinguish between different timing parameters for amoxicillin:
* **Elimination Half-life:** 1 to 1.5 hours.
* **Onset of Action:** 0.5 to 1 hour.
* **Peak Effect:** 1 to 2 hours.
* **Duration of Action:** 6 to 8 hours.


* **Drug-Drug and Drug-Food Interactions:**
* **Tetracyclines:** These bind to magnesium ions, which reduces the drug’s therapeutic action.
* **Ampicillin:** Concurrent administration with calcium citrate can diminish the absorption of oral ampicillin.
* **Clavulanic Acid:** This is not an antibiotic on its own but a beta-lactamase inhibitor added to drugs like ampicillin to enhance their effectiveness by protecting them from bacterial enzymes.


* **Identifying Allergic Reactions vs. Side Effects:**
* **Anaphylactic Reactions:** Characterized by flushing, itching, hives, anxiety, throat and tongue swelling, and a rapid/irregular pulse.
* **C. difficile Infection:** Often mistaken for a general side effect, this specific superinfection presents with abdominal cramps and frequent watery stools.
* **G6PD Deficiency:** In patients with this deficiency, antibiotic administration can lead to hemolysis (red blood cell destruction).


* **Nomenclature and Awareness:**
* Not all penicillins are easily identified by their name. For example, the brand name **Zosyn (Piperacillin/tazobactam)** does not include the "-cillin" suffix, which can lead to it being overlooked when checking for patient allergies.


* **Spectrum of Activity:**
* Extended-spectrum penicillins like Ticarcillin-clavulanic acid have one of the broadest antimicrobial spectra and are considered a generation beyond aminopenicillins (like ampicillin or amoxicillin).

# Chapter 38, Antibiotics Part 1

To succeed in this topic, a student must master the classification, mechanisms of action, nursing priorities, and patient education requirements for major antibiotic classes, including Sulfonamides, Penicillins, Cephalosporins, Tetracyclines, and Macrolides.

### Main Points for Mastery

#### 1. General Nursing Principles and Assessments

* **Allergy History:** Obtaining a complete history of medication allergies is the absolute priority before administering any antibiotic, as reactions can lead to fatal anaphylaxis.
* **Superinfections:** Students must recognize signs of *Clostridium difficile* (watery diarrhea, abdominal pain, and high fever) often caused by long-term antibiotic use. Fidaxomicin is a specific macrolide used to treat this.
* **Immune Response Variations:** In elderly patients or those who are immunocompromised (e.g., transplant recipients, radiation patients), typical signs of infection like fever may be absent; atypical signs like confusion or urinary incontinence should be monitored.
* **Evaluation of Therapy:** Treatment effectiveness is monitored via decreased fever and fatigue. Adverse effects like jaundice (indicating liver involvement) require immediate referral.

#### 2. Sulfonamides (e.g., SMZ-TMP, Sulfasalazine)

* **Indications:** Primarily used for Urinary Tract Infections (UTIs) and Ulcerative Colitis (Sulfasalazine).
* **Hydration:** Patients must increase fluid intake to 2000–3000 mL/day to prevent crystalluria and kidney stone formation.
* **Photosensitivity:** Education must include avoiding midday sun and wearing protective clothing/sunscreen.
* **Laboratory Monitoring:** Nurses must monitor Complete Blood Counts (CBC) for blood dyscrasias, including anemia, thrombocytopenia, and agranulocytosis.
* **Interactions:** Sulfonamides increase the hypoglycemic effect of sulfonylureas (oral diabetes medications), requiring close blood sugar monitoring.

#### 3. Penicillins (e.g., Amoxicillin, Penicillin V Potassium)

* **Anaphylaxis Management:** If a patient develops wheezing, throat swelling, or a fast pulse, Epinephrine is the immediate drug of choice.
* **Contraception:** Penicillins can decrease the effectiveness of oral contraceptives; patients must use a backup birth control method.
* **Classifications:** "Antistaphylococcal penicillins" (like nafcillin or oxacillin) are specifically resistant to the penicillinase enzyme produced by staphylococci.

#### 4. Cephalosporins (e.g., Cefazolin, Cefotetan, Ceftriaxone)

* **Surgical Prophylaxis:** First-generation cephalosporins (Cefazolin) are the preferred choice for preventing infection in surgical patients.
* **Cross-Sensitivity:** Patients with a known penicillin allergy may have a cross-sensitivity to cephalosporins. Nurses must verify the nature of the previous reaction before administration.
* **Carbapenems:** Imipenem/cilastatin carries a specific risk for drug-induced seizure activity.

#### 5. Tetracyclines (e.g., Doxycycline, Demeclocycline)

* **Mechanism:** These are bacteriostatic drugs that inhibit protein synthesis by binding to the 30S bacterial ribosome.
* **Contraindications:** Must not be given to children under 8 years old due to permanent tooth discoloration, nor to pregnant women due to fetal skeletal defects.
* **Dietary Restrictions:** Absorption is significantly reduced if taken with dairy products, antacids, or iron supplements.

#### 6. Macrolides (e.g., Erythromycin, Azithromycin)

* **Drug Comparison:** Azithromycin is preferred over Erythromycin because it has a longer duration of action (allowing for once-daily dosing) and significantly fewer gastrointestinal side effects.
* **Toxicity Monitoring:** Nurses must assess baseline hearing (ototoxicity), liver function (hepatotoxicity), and cardiac function (risk of palpitations and ECG changes).

### Critical Nuances to Master

* **The Disulfiram-Like Reaction:** Cefotetan (a cephalosporin) interacts with alcohol (ethanol). Patients must avoid alcohol during therapy and for at least 3 days (72 hours) after the last dose to avoid severe stomach cramps, vomiting, and hypotension.
* **Chelation:** Tetracyclines undergo "metallic chelation." They bind to calcium, magnesium, and iron, rendering the antibiotic ineffective. Even simple items like salt crackers (if they contain certain ions) or milk should be avoided near dosing times.
* **Specific Contraindications (G6PD):** Patients with Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency are at high risk for hemolysis (destruction of red blood cells) if they take Sulfonamides.
* **Sulfonamide Cross-Reactivity:** While many patients with a "sulfa" allergy can take diuretics or oral hypoglycemics, they must strictly avoid Celecoxib (a COX-2 inhibitor), as it contains a sulfonamide moiety.
* **Penicillin Interactions:** Rifampin can inhibit the efficacy of penicillin, while Probenecid can actually be used to prolong and increase penicillin levels in the blood by decreasing renal elimination.
* **Dosing Nuances:** SMZ-TMP is typically dosed twice daily, not four times daily. Azithromycin’s unique pharmacokinetic profile allows for a short course of treatment because it stays in the tissues longer.
