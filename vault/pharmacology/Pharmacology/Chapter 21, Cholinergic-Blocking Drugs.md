# Chapter 21, Cholinergic-Blocking Drugs

To master the topic of cholinergic-blocking drugs (also known as anticholinergics or parasympatholytics), a student must understand their systemic physiological effects, specific clinical applications, and the critical safety precautions required for patient care.

### Main Points for Mastery

**1. Mechanism of Action and Physiological Effects**
Cholinergic blockers work by competing with acetylcholine for binding at muscarinic receptors in the parasympathetic nervous system. This results in:

* **Cardiovascular:** Increased heart rate (positive chronotropic effect).
* **Gastrointestinal:** Decreased motility (peristalsis) and decreased gastric secretions.
* **Genitourinary:** Relaxation of the bladder detrusor muscle and constriction of the internal sphincter, leading to urinary retention.
* **Glandular:** Decreased salivation and sweating.
* **Ocular:** Dilation of the pupils (mydriasis) and paralysis of the ciliary muscle (cycloplegia), causing blurred vision.
* **Respiratory:** Bronchodilation and decreased bronchial secretions.

**2. Specific Drug Indications**

* **Atropine:** Used preoperatively to reduce secretions, for symptomatic bradycardia, and for ophthalmic examinations.
* **Scopolamine:** Primarily used for the prevention of motion sickness (transdermal patch).
* **Oxybutynin and Tolterodine:** Used for overactive bladder (OAB) and urinary frequency.
* **Dicyclomine:** Used for functional GI disorders like Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS).
* **Glycopyrrolate:** Used preoperatively to reduce salivation and respiratory secretions.

**3. Contraindications**
These drugs should be avoided or used with extreme caution in patients with:

* Angle-closure glaucoma (increases intraocular pressure).
* GI or GU tract obstruction (e.g., BPH, paralytic ileus).
* Myasthenia gravis.
* Acute ulcerative colitis or toxic megacolon.
* Tachycardia or myocardial ischemia.

**4. Adverse Effects (The Anticholinergic Profile)**
Students should recognize the classic side effects: dry mouth (xerostomia), blurred vision, constipation, urinary retention, and tachycardia. In older adults, CNS effects such as restlessness, irritability, and disorientation are common.

**5. Toxicity and Overdose Management**
An overdose (atropine poisoning) presents with "hot as a hare, red as a beet, dry as a bone, blind as a bat, and mad as a hatter."

* **Antidote:** Physostigmine (used for extreme delirium or agitation, though controversial due to risks of seizures and asystole).
* **Supportive Care:** Continuous ECG monitoring, activated charcoal for recent ingestion, and fluid therapy for shock.

### Nuances for Advanced Mastery

**1. Drug Selection Nuances (Old vs. New)**
Newer drugs for overactive bladder (e.g., Solifenacin, Darifenacin, Fesoterodine, Trospium) have a lower incidence of dry mouth compared to older drugs like Oxybutynin. This is because they have greater pharmacologic specificity for the bladder muscarinic receptors over salivary gland receptors.

**2. Scopolamine Patch Application**
The transdermal patch must be applied to a non-hairy area behind the ear at least 4 to 5 hours before the activity that causes motion sickness. It lasts for 72 hours. Patients must be warned that alcohol significantly increases the sedative effects of this medication.

**3. Therapeutic Thresholds and Dosing**

* **Tolterodine:** In patients with impaired hepatic function or those taking CYP3A4 inhibitors (like erythromycin), the dose must be reduced by half.
* **Atropine Dosing:** Higher doses are more likely to cause CNS effects like disorientation and hallucinations.

**4. Critical Patient Teaching Points**

* **Heat Stroke Risk:** Because these drugs inhibit sweating, patients must avoid strenuous exercise in high temperatures.
* **Managing Side Effects:** Teach patients to use sugar-free gum or candy for dry mouth and to increase fiber and fluid intake to prevent constipation.
* **Safety:** Patients should wear sunglasses outdoors due to light sensitivity (mydriasis) and avoid driving until they know how the drug affects their vision and alertness.

**5. Nursing Interventions & Assessment**

* **Compatibility:** Atropine and glycopyrrolate are often compatible with opioids like morphine and can be mixed in the same syringe for preoperative use.
* **Priority Assessments:** Before administering, always assess heart rate, bowel sounds, and the ability to urinate. If a patient cannot urinate after taking the drug, a bladder scan is the priority assessment before invasive interventions like catheterization.
* **OAB Treatment Hierarchy:** Behavioral modification therapy (scheduled voiding and fluid timing) should be tried first before starting medications like oxybutynin.

**6. Special Populations**

* **Elderly:** Highly susceptible to heat stroke and "anticholinergic psychosis" (delirium).
* **Down Syndrome:** These patients may have an increased sensitivity to the effects of anticholinergics, potentially worsening symptoms.
* **Heart Surgery:** Anticholinergics increase the workload on the heart and should be used cautiously postoperatively.
