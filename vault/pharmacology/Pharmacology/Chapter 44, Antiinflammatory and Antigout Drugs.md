# Chapter 44, Antiinflammatory and Antigout Drugs

To master the topic of antiinflammatory and antigout drugs, a student must understand the mechanisms of action, clinical indications, adverse effect profiles, and complex drug interactions associated with these medications.

### Main Points for Mastery

**1. Nonsteroidal Antiinflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs)**

* **Mechanism of Action:** NSAIDs work through the arachidonic acid pathway. They primarily block the chemical activity of the enzyme cyclooxygenase (COX). Mastery requires distinguishing between the prostaglandin pathway (mediated by COX) and the leukotriene pathway (mediated by lipoxygenase).
* **The Ceiling Effect:** Unlike opioid analgesics, NSAIDs exhibit a therapeutic ceiling. Increasing the dose beyond a certain point does not increase the analgesic effect but significantly increases the risk of toxicity and adverse effects.
* **General Indications:** Used for the treatment of rheumatoid arthritis, osteoarthritis, ankylosing spondylitis, acute pain, and primary dysmenorrhea. Some NSAIDs, like indomethacin, have specific uses such as promoting the closure of a patent ductus arteriosus in premature infants.
* **Key Adverse Effects:** * **Gastrointestinal:** Dyspepsia, anorexia, and potential GI bleeding. Black, tarry stools are a critical finding indicating severe bleeding.
* **Renal:** Risk of renal toxicity and failure, which is increased in patients who are dehydrated, taking diuretics, or have liver dysfunction.
* **Ototoxicity:** Often manifesting as tinnitus.


* **Specific Drug Classes:**
* **Acetic Acid Derivatives:** Indomethacin (used for gout and PDA closure), Ketorolac (limited to 5 days of use due to renal/GI risks; comparable to opioids for post-op pain), and Etodolac.
* **Propionic Acid Derivatives:** Ibuprofen (common, but high side-effect profile), Naproxen (preferred for patients on ACE inhibitors), and Oxaprozin.
* **Enolic Acid Derivatives:** Nabumetone (better tolerated GI-wise because it is relatively non-acidic).
* **COX-2 Inhibitors:** Celecoxib (specifically inhibits COX-2, reducing the risk of GI ulcers, though it can cause dizziness, headache, and sinus irritation).



**2. Salicylates (Aspirin)**

* **Clinical Utility:** Used for pain, inflammation, and fever. Its unique antiplatelet property makes it vital for thromboprevention in patients at risk for stroke or myocardial infarction.
* **Administration Nuances:** Enteric-coated tablets should be swallowed whole to protect the stomach mucosa and prevent ulcers; they must never be crushed or chewed. A vinegar odor in the bottle indicates chemical breakdown, and the tablets should be discarded.
* **Contraindications:** Aspirin is contraindicated in patients with vitamin K deficiency, severe peptic ulcers, rhinitis, and asthma. It must be avoided in children/teenagers with viral infections to prevent Reye’s syndrome.

**3. Antigout Medications**

* **Allopurinol:** Used for chronic gout to reduce uric acid production and prevent kidney stones. It should be taken with meals and requires high fluid intake (3L/day).
* **Colchicine:** A second-line therapy for acute gout. It inhibits cell mitosis, which can lead to leukopenia. It is contraindicated in patients with significant renal or cardiac disease.
* **Probenecid:** A uricosuric that increases the excretion of uric acid. It is notable for delaying the renal excretion of penicillin, thereby prolonging the antibiotic's effect.
* **Febuxostat:** Used for gout but carries a risk of cardiovascular side effects, requiring a thorough assessment of heart health before prescription.

### Nuances and Clinical Pearls

* **Salicylate Intoxication (Salicylism):**
* **Adults vs. Children:** In adults, the most common signs of chronic intoxication are tinnitus and hearing loss. In children, the primary symptoms are CNS-related, such as dizziness and drowsiness.
* **Acute Toxicity:** Can lead to hyperventilation (respiratory alkalosis), profuse sweating (hyperthermia), hypoglycemia, and life-threatening pulmonary edema.
* **The Done Nomogram:** This tool is used specifically to estimate the severity of *acute* salicylate intoxication to guide treatment; it is not used for chronic intoxication or dosing.


* **The Aspirin Triad:** Patients with a combination of asthma, rhinitis, and nasal polyps are at a high risk of fatal allergic reactions to aspirin.
* **Reye’s Syndrome Management:** This condition involves encephalopathy and liver damage. Treatment includes antiepileptics for seizures, IV glucose for hypoglycemia, vitamin K for clotting issues, and osmotic diuretics for cerebral edema.
* **Gastroprotection:** Misoprostol is a synthetic prostaglandin analog used to prevent NSAID-induced gastric ulcers. However, it is an abortifacient and is strictly avoided in pregnancy.
* **Pregnancy Considerations:**
* NSAIDs are generally avoided in the third trimester as they are teratogenic and can cause neonatal toxicity/increased bleeding during delivery.
* Probenecid is Category B (relatively safe).
* Colchicine is Category D (inhibits organogenesis and cell division).


* **Critical Drug Interactions:**
* **Corticosteroids + NSAIDs:** Greatly increases the risk of ulcerogenic effects.
* **ACE Inhibitors + NSAIDs:** NSAIDs can diminish the hypotensive (blood pressure lowering) effect of ACE inhibitors.
* **Cyclosporine + NSAIDs:** Increased risk of nephrotoxicity.
* **Anticoagulants + NSAIDs:** Significant increase in the risk of GI bleeding.
* **Ginkgo + Aspirin:** Synergistic effect that increases bleeding risk by interfering with platelet function.
