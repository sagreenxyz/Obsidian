# Chapter 15, Antiparkinson Drugs

### Main Points for Mastery

**Pathophysiology and Neurotransmitters**

* **The Dopamine-Acetylcholine Imbalance:** Parkinson’s disease is caused by the depletion of dopamine in the **substantia nigra** (part of the extrapyramidal system). This leads to an imbalance where the excitatory effects of **acetylcholine** go unopposed by the inhibitory effects of **dopamine**.
* **Key Brain Areas:** While the substantia nigra controls motor function and posture, the **cerebellum** is specifically responsible for muscle coordination. The **thalamus** acts as a relay station for brain impulses.

**Pharmacological Mainstays**

* **Levodopa-Carbidopa:** This is the "gold standard" or mainstay of treatment. Levodopa acts as a biologic precursor to dopamine, while carbidopa acts as an enhancer that allows more levodopa to reach the brain. It is considered the drug of choice for older adults due to its safety and efficacy.
* **Dopamine Modulators (Amantadine):** Unique because it acts by both releasing dopamine and blocking its reuptake. Notably, it is also used as an antiviral medication for the influenza virus.
* **MAO-B Inhibitors (Selegiline):** These prevent the breakdown of dopamine by inhibiting the monoamine oxidase enzyme. They are often used as adjunct therapy with carbidopa-levodopa.
* **Dopamine Receptor Agonists (Ropinirole, Bromocriptine):** These drugs directly stimulate dopamine receptors. Ropinirole is often preferred over levodopa in some cases because it is more specific to certain receptors and may have fewer side effects.

**Gastrointestinal and Dietary Considerations**

* **MAOI Interactions:** Nonselective MAOIs interact dangerously with **tyramine-containing foods** (red wine, beer, aged cheese, yogurt), which can trigger a **hypertensive crisis**.
* **COMT Inhibitor Administration:** Drugs like entacapone should not be taken on an empty stomach to avoid significant gastrointestinal upset.

---

### Nuances to Master

**Treatment Phenomena**

* **Wearing-off Phenomenon:** This occurs when medications lose effectiveness over time as the disease progresses, despite maximum dosing. Symptoms return and remain constant.
* **On-off Phenomenon:** This refers to abrupt fluctuations in symptomatic control, where a patient alternates between periods of good mobility ("on") and poor mobility ("off"). **COMT inhibitors** are specifically used to treat this phenomenon by maintaining uniform dopamine concentrations.
* **Motor Terms:** Distinguish between **Chorea** (purposeless, rapid motions like flexing fingers or grimacing) and **Dystonia** (impaired or distorted voluntary movements of the head, neck, or feet).

**Drug-Specific Nuances and Contraindications**

* **Liver Toxicity:** **Tolcapone** (a COMT inhibitor) is contraindicated in patients with liver failure due to the risk of hepatotoxicity.
* **Prolactin Inhibition:** **Bromocriptine** has a unique dual use; beyond Parkinson's, it is used to treat prolactin-secreting tumors because it inhibits the production of the hormone prolactin.
* **Side Effect Profiles:** While carbidopa-levodopa is the mainstay, it can cause generalized dopaminergic stimulation. Newer agonists like **ropinirole** target specific receptor families (D2), which may result in fewer adverse effects.
* **Anticholinergic Effects:** Understand that while anticholinergics reduce tremors and rigidity, they do not increase dopamine levels and frequently cause "dry mouth."

**Patient Education Realities**

* **Progression vs. Cure:** It is critical to teach patients that while medications slow the progression of symptoms, they **do not cure** the disease. Symptoms will not completely disappear even if treatment is started in the earliest stages.
* **Dietary Adjustments:** Patients on MAO-B inhibitors like selegiline should be encouraged to eat natural laxatives like prunes to prevent drug-induced constipation, but must strictly avoid aged cheeses and alcohol.

# Chapter 15, Antiparkinson Drugs

### Main Points for Mastery

**Pathophysiology and Symptomatology**

* **Core Cause:** Parkinson’s disease is a progressive, degenerative neurologic disorder caused by an imbalance between two neurotransmitters: dopamine (which is deficient) and acetylcholine (which is in relative excess).
* **Classic Symptoms:** Students must recognize the primary clinical manifestations, which include resting tremors (pill-rolling), bradykinesia (slowness of movement), muscle rigidity, postural instability (shuffling gait/forward trunk flexion), mask-like facial expressions, and weakness.

**Dopamine Replacement (Carbidopa-Levodopa)**

* **Mechanism:** Levodopa is a precursor to dopamine that crosses the blood-brain barrier. Carbidopa is given alongside it to inhibit the breakdown of levodopa in the periphery, ensuring more reaches the brain and allowing for lower doses with fewer side effects.
* **Contraindications:** This combination is strictly contraindicated in patients with angle-closure glaucoma, as it can increase intraocular pressure.
* **Clinical Phenomena:**
* **On-Off Phenomenon:** Fluctuations in dopamine levels leading to periods of good symptom control alternating with periods of impaired voluntary movement (dyskinesia).
* **Wearing-Off Phenomenon:** A gradual loss of drug effectiveness over time as the disease progresses and the number of dopaminergic terminals decreases.



**MAO-B Inhibitors (Selegiline)**

* **Action:** These drugs increase levels of dopaminergic stimulation in the CNS and are often used as adjuncts to allow for a reduction in the levodopa dose.
* **Major Interaction:** They should not be taken with dextromethorphan due to the risk of serotonin syndrome.

**Dopamine Modulators (Amantadine)**

* **Action:** Acts by releasing dopamine and blocking its reuptake into nerve fibers.
* **Timeline:** Usually only effective for 6 to 12 months in the early stages of the disease.

**COMT Inhibitors (Entacapone and Tolcapone)**

* **Action:** These drugs block the enzyme catechol ortho-methyltransferase to reduce the "on-off" effects of levodopa.
* **Safety:** Tolcapone carries a black box warning for hepatotoxicity (liver failure), necessitating regular liver function monitoring.

**Anticholinergic Drugs (Benztropine and Trihexyphenidyl)**

* **Goal:** Used primarily to alleviate tremors and rigidity by opposing the effects of acetylcholine.
* **Side Effects:** Common issues include dry mouth, urinary retention, constipation, and tachycardia.

### Essential Nuances and Safety Considerations

**Dietary and Medication Timing**

* **Protein Interaction:** High-protein meals can interfere with the absorption of carbidopa-levodopa. Patients should take their medication at least 30 minutes before eating meat or dairy, or wait one hour after a protein-heavy meal.
* **The "Cheese Effect":** Selegiline, at higher doses, can act as a non-selective MAOI. Patients must avoid foods high in tyramine (aged cheeses, yogurt, beer, red wine) to prevent a hypertensive crisis.
* **GI Management:** Most antiparkinson drugs (specifically COMT inhibitors) should be taken with food or snacks to minimize gastrointestinal upset, though levodopa timing remains specific to protein intake.

**Critical Side Effects and Environmental Safety**

* **Hyperthermia Risk:** Anticholinergics like benztropine inhibit sweating. Students must teach patients to avoid excessive exercise during hot weather or taking very hot baths, as they are at high risk for overheating and hyperthermia.
* **Orthostatic Hypotension:** Many drugs, particularly amantadine and entacapone, cause a drop in blood pressure upon standing. Patients must be instructed to change positions slowly to prevent syncope and falls.
* **Urine Discoloration:** COMT inhibitors (entacapone) can turn urine a brown-orange color. This is a harmless side effect, but the patient must be reassured.
* **Edema:** Nondopamine receptor agonists like pramipexole can cause significant fluid retention (edema) and sudden weight gain, which may require discontinuation.

**Pharmacological Interactions**

* **Erythromycin:** Should be avoided with bromocriptine as both use the cytochrome P-450 pathway, leading to potential toxicity.
* **Ciprofloxacin:** Can lead to ropinirole toxicity by inhibiting its metabolism.
* **Benzodiazepines:** These can decrease the therapeutic effect of levodopa, causing an increase in Parkinsonian symptoms.
* **Vitamin B6:** High doses of pyridoxine (B6) may decrease the efficacy of levodopa.

**Nursing Assessment Priorities**

* **Reproductive Status:** Since dopamine replacement drugs cross the placenta and enter breast milk, the nurse must assess reproductive history in female patients of childbearing age.
* **Systemic Assessments:** Before starting anticholinergics, priority assessments include the genitourinary (for retention), gastrointestinal (for constipation), and cerebrovascular systems.
* **Therapeutic Response:** Success is measured not just by physical gait/tremor improvement, but also by improved mental status, concentration, and sense of well-being.
