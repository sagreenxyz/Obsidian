# Chapter 29, Fluids and Electrolytes

### Core Concepts of Fluid Balance

* **Colloidal Osmotic Pressure:** Students must understand that albumin is the primary protein responsible for approximately 70% of the colloidal osmotic pressure in the blood.
* **Fluid Concentrations:** * **Isotonic:** 0.9% Sodium Chloride (Normal Saline) and Lactated Ringer’s.
* **Hypotonic:** 0.45% NaCl (half-normal) and 0.25% NaCl (quarter-normal).
* **Hypertonic:** 3% and 5% Sodium Chloride.


* **Normal Electrolyte Ranges:** Sodium levels should be maintained between 135 and 145 mEq/L. Potassium levels range from 3.5 to 5.0 mEq/L.

### Crystalloids vs. Colloids

* **Crystalloids:** Used for fluid replacement and to increase urinary output. Normal Saline is the only fluid compatible with blood products. 5% Dextrose in water (D5W) is a crystalloid often used for slow blood loss (approx. 20%).
* **Colloids:** These are protein or non-protein substances that increase colloid oncotic pressure.
* **Albumin:** Used to expand plasma volume. A nuance to master is the volume ratio: 0.5 L of 25% albumin provides the same plasma expansion as 5 to 6 L of 0.9% sodium chloride.
* **Dextran:** A non-protein expander used for slow blood loss of 20% to 50%.
* **Hetastarch:** A synthetic colloid used to pull fluid from extravascular spaces into the intravascular area.



### Blood Product Specificity

* **Packed Red Blood Cells (PRBCs):** Specifically administered to increase oxygen-carrying capacity in anemic patients or those with blood loss up to 25%.
* **Whole Blood:** Reserved for extreme blood loss (over 25% rapid loss or 50% slow loss) because it contains plasma, which provides significant fluid volume.
* **Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP):** Used primarily to increase clotting factor levels in patients with demonstrated deficiencies.
* **Cryoprecipitate and PPF:** Indicated for managing acute, rapid bleeding.

### Potassium Mastery (Hypokalemia and Hyperkalemia)

* **Hypokalemia (Low Potassium):**
* **Early Symptoms:** Anorexia, hypotension, lethargy, muscle weakness, and confusion.
* **Late Symptoms:** Neuropathies, paralytic ileus, and cardiac dysrhythmias.
* **Nuance:** Potassium acetate is used when both potassium replacement and an increase in blood pH are needed, whereas potassium chloride is standard but does not affect pH.


* **Hyperkalemia (High Potassium):**
* **Treatment:** Sodium polystyrene sulfonate (Kayexalate) is a potassium exchange resin.
* **Emergency Intervention:** If a patient shows ECG changes/tachycardia during infusion, the priority is to stop the infusion, then potentially administer calcium carbonate (to protect the heart) and sodium polystyrene sulfonate.



### Sodium Mastery (Hyponatremia and Hypernatremia)

* **Hyponatremia:** Symptoms include stomach cramps, vomiting, diarrhea, and seizures.
* **Nuance:** Euvolemic hyponatremia is treated with "vaptans" like Conivaptan or Tolvaptan. Tolvaptan has a black box warning requiring the patient to be hospitalized for close monitoring of sodium levels during initiation.


* **Hypernatremia:** Symptoms include edema, hypertension, reddish/flushed skin, increased thirst, and elevated temperature. Treatment involves fluid restriction of salt and intravenous 5% dextrose to dilute the sodium concentration.

### Critical Nursing Nuances and Safety

* **Potassium Administration Rules:** * **NEVER** give potassium as an intravenous bolus or undiluted; it causes immediate cardiac arrest.
* Always dilute and monitor the infusion rate (standard max is 10 mEq/hr for general floors, though up to 40 mEq/hr with cardiac monitoring).
* **Urinary Output:** Always assess urinary output before and during administration. A minimum of 30 mL/hr or 400 mL/24 hours is required; if the patient is oliguric, potassium can quickly reach toxic levels.


* **Blood Transfusion Protocol:**
* The absolute first priority is confirming the patient’s identity.
* Use a large-bore IV.
* If a reaction occurs (shortness of breath, chills, fever), stop the infusion immediately.


* **Contraindications to Master:**
* **Albumin** is contraindicated in patients with heart failure (due to fluid overload risk) and severe anemia.
* **Dextran** is contraindicated in renal insufficiency and extreme dehydration.
* **Sodium polystyrene sulfonate** should not be used in patients with constipation or bowel obstruction due to the risk of intestinal necrosis.
* **Potassium-sparing diuretics** are contraindicated in patients with metabolic acidosis (pH < 7).


* **Drug Interactions:** Sorbitol should not be given with sodium polystyrene sulfonate as it can cause fatal colonic necrosis. Calcium supplements can interact with blood products.
