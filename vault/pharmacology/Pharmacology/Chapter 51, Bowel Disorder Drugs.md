# Chapter 51, Bowel Disorder Drugs

To master the pharmacology of bowel disorder drugs, a student must understand the distinct classifications, their specific mechanisms of action, and the critical clinical nuances regarding patient safety and education.

### Main Points: Drug Classifications and Mechanisms

**1. Antidiarrheals**

* **Adsorbents (e.g., Bismuth subsalicylate):** These agents coat the walls of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract and bind to causative bacteria or toxins, which are then eliminated through the stool.
* **Anticholinergics (e.g., Belladonna alkaloids):** These slow peristalsis by reducing the rhythmic contractions and smooth muscle tone of the GI tract.
* **Opiates (e.g., Loperamide, Diphenoxylate):** These reduce GI motility and increase the transit time of food, allowing for more water absorption. They are also effective at relieving pain associated with rectal spasms.
* **Probiotics (e.g., Lactobacillus acidophilus):** These replenish the body's normal bacterial flora, which is often destroyed by antibiotic therapy, thereby suppressing the growth of diarrhea-causing bacteria.
* **Antilipemics (e.g., Cholestyramine):** While primarily used for high cholesterol, they have a secondary antidiarrheal effect and can be used for patients suffering from both hyperlipidemia and mild diarrhea.

**2. Laxatives**

* **Bulk-Forming (e.g., Methylcellulose, Psyllium):** High-fiber supplements that absorb water into the intestine to increase bulk and distend the bowel to initiate reflex bowel activity. These are the only laxatives suitable for long-term use.
* **Emollient/Stool Softeners (e.g., Docusate sodium):** These lower the surface tension of GI fluids, allowing more water and fat to be absorbed into the stool to soften it.
* **Hyperosmotic (e.g., Polyethylene glycol, Lactulose):** These increase fecal water content, resulting in distention, increased peristalsis, and evacuation.
* **Saline (e.g., Magnesium hydroxide/citrate):** These increase osmotic pressure in the small intestine, drawing water into the colon to soften stool and increase watery secretions.
* **Stimulant (e.g., Senna, Bisacodyl):** These induce defecation by stimulating the nerves that innervate the intestines, increasing peristalsis.

### Nuances and Critical Mastery

**Safety and Contraindications**

* **Age-Specific Risks:** Bismuth subsalicylate (which contains an aspirin base) must be avoided in children and teenagers recovering from viral infections (like chickenpox or the flu) due to the risk of Reye’s syndrome.
* **Renal Health:** Magnesium-based saline laxatives are contraindicated in patients with renal insufficiency, as they can lead to toxic hypermagnesemia.
* **Elderly Patients:** Older adults react more adversely to hyperosmotic laxatives; their use should generally be avoided in this population to prevent severe fluid and electrolyte imbalances.
* **Specific Conditions:** Anticholinergics (Belladonna alkaloids) are strictly contraindicated in patients with narrow-angle glaucoma, GI obstruction, myasthenia gravis, or paralytic ileus.

**Medication Administration Nuances**

* **Bulk-Forming Laxatives:** Methylcellulose must be taken immediately after mixing with a full glass of water. If allowed to sit, it will solidify and can cause an esophageal obstruction.
* **Bisacodyl (Stimulant):** Tablets must be taken on an empty stomach for faster action. They should never be crushed or chewed because they are enteric-coated; taking them with milk, juice, or antacids within one hour can destroy this coating and cause severe gastric distress.
* **Lactulose:** Beyond its use as a laxative, it is unique for its ability to reduce blood ammonia levels by trapping ammonia in the gut, making it essential for treating hepatic encephalopathy. It is contraindicated for those with lactose intolerance.
* **Alosetron (IBS Treatment):** This is only for women with severe, chronic, diarrhea-predominant IBS. It has a "black box" warning for ischemic colitis. If the drug does not show therapeutic effects within 4 weeks, it must be discontinued.

**Side Effects and Patient Expectations**

* **Stool Appearance:** Bismuth subsalicylate can turn the stool and tongue tarry black or gray. This is an expected side effect and not a sign of GI bleeding.
* **Adverse Reactions to Atropine:** When diphenoxylate is combined with atropine (to discourage abuse), patients may experience anticholinergic effects like dry mouth, tachycardia, blurred vision, and urinary retention.
* **Drug Interactions:** Cholestyramine decreases the absorption of almost any drug given concurrently. Other medications must be taken at least 2 hours before or after.

### Nursing Assessment and Education

* **Long-term Use Risks:** Patients should be taught that stimulant laxatives can cause nutrient and electrolyte malabsorption if used for extended periods.
* **Lifestyle Modifications:** For diarrhea, patients should be advised to avoid caffeine (which speeds GI transit), alcohol, and spicy foods. For constipation, increasing fluid intake and fiber is essential.
* **Rectal Suppository Technique:** Ensure the patient is placed on their left side during insertion and remains there for 15 to 30 minutes to ensure proper absorption.
* **Emergency Criteria:** Students must know when to instruct a patient to seek immediate medical help. This includes the presence of bloody diarrhea, high fever, severe abdominal pain, a rigid abdomen, or signs of severe dehydration (e.g., no tears or decreased urination in children).
