# Chapter 37, Respiratory Drugs

To master the pharmacology of respiratory drugs, a student must understand the distinct classifications of medications, their specific roles in treatment (rescue vs. maintenance), and the critical safety parameters associated with each.

### 1. Beta-Adrenergic Agonists (Bronchodilators)

These drugs relax bronchial smooth muscle to dilate the airways.

* **Short-Acting Beta Agonists (SABA):** These are "rescue" medications. **Albuterol** and **levalbuterol** are the primary agents used for acute asthma attacks or status asthmaticus. Levalbuterol is often preferred for patients who already have tachycardia because it has fewer cardiac side effects than racemic albuterol.
* **Long-Acting Beta Agonists (LABA):** Medications like **salmeterol** and **arformoterol** are for maintenance, not rescue. They should be taken approximately 12 hours apart. In asthma, LABAs should never be used as monotherapy; they must be combined with an inhaled corticosteroid.
* **Side Effects:** Tachycardia, palpitations, tremors, anxiety, and hyperglycemia.
* **Nuance:** Overuse of albuterol can cause the drug to lose its beta2-specificity, leading to increased stimulation of beta1 receptors (resulting in heart palpitations and tremors).

### 2. Anticholinergics

* **Drugs:** **Ipratropium** and **tiotropium**.
* **Role:** Used primarily for maintenance in COPD and occasionally asthma. They prevent bronchoconstriction caused by the parasympathetic nervous system.
* **Side Effects:** "Dry" effects—dry mouth or throat, nasal congestion, heart palpitations, and urinary retention.
* **Nuance:** Patients should rinse their mouth after use to prevent mucosal irritation and dryness. Avoid using multiple anticholinergics (like ipratropium plus diphenhydramine) simultaneously due to additive toxicity.

### 3. Xanthine Derivatives

* **Drugs:** **Theophylline** and **aminophylline**.
* **Therapeutic Range:** The therapeutic level is **10 to 20 mcg/mL** (though some protocols now use 5–15 mcg/mL). Levels above 20 mcg/mL are considered toxic and require holding the dose and notifying a provider.
* **Toxicity/Side Effects:** Sinus tachycardia, ventricular dysrhythmias, palpitations, nausea, vomiting, and irritability.
* **Interactions & Diet (Crucial Nuances):**
* **Smoking:** Cigarette smoking increases xanthine metabolism, requiring higher doses.
* **Diet:** High-protein and low-carbohydrate diets, as well as charcoal-broiled foods, decrease theophylline levels.
* **Caffeine:** Consuming caffeine with xanthines can lead to additive cardiac and CNS stimulation.
* **Herbal:** St. John’s wort increases the metabolism of theophylline, lowering its effectiveness.
* **Contraceptives:** Oral contraceptives can increase theophylline levels, raising the risk of toxicity.


* **Overdose:** Activated charcoal is used to treat xanthine overdose.

### 4. Corticosteroids (Glucocorticoids)

These are anti-inflammatory agents used for chronic management.

* **Inhaled (ICS):** **Fluticasone**, **beclomethasone**.
* **Systemic:** **Methylprednisolone** (IV), **prednisone** (oral).
* **Side Effects:** Oral candidiasis (thrush) with inhaled forms; Cushing’s syndrome (moon face, acne, fat pads, swelling) with long-term systemic use.
* **Critical Safety (Nuances):**
* **Tapering:** Systemic steroids must never be stopped abruptly. Doing so can cause a fatal **Addisonian crisis** (nausea, weakness, fatigue, joint pain, and shortness of breath).
* **Switching:** When a patient moves from systemic to inhaled steroids, the systemic dose must be tapered slowly to allow the adrenal glands to resume function.
* **Prevention:** Patients must rinse their mouth after using inhaled corticosteroids to prevent thrush.



### 5. Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists (LTRAs)

* **Drugs:** **Montelukast**, **zafirlukast**, **zileuton**.
* **Role:** Maintenance therapy for asthma and allergic rhinitis; they prevent the inflammatory response. They are not for acute attacks.
* **Side Effects:** Nausea, diarrhea, and headache. Zileuton specifically requires monitoring of **liver enzymes**.
* **Nuances:** * Montelukast is contraindicated in patients with a **lactose allergy** (it is an inactive ingredient).
* **Phenobarbital** can decrease the efficacy of montelukast.
* Therapeutic effects may not be noticed for about one week.



### 6. Monoclonal Antibody Antiasthmatics

* **Drug:** **Omalizumab**.
* **Mechanism:** Binds to IgE to limit the release of allergic mediators.
* **Nuance:** The highest priority for nursing is monitoring for **hypersensitivity and anaphylactic reactions**.

### 7. Administration Sequence and Techniques

* **The 5-Minute Rule:** If a patient is prescribed both a bronchodilator (like albuterol) and a corticosteroid (like beclomethasone), the **bronchodilator must be used first**. Wait **5 minutes** before using the corticosteroid to allow the airways to open so the steroid can reach deeper into the lungs.
* **The 2-Minute Rule:** If two puffs of the same medication are ordered, wait 1 to 2 minutes between puffs.
* **Pediatrics:** Children who have difficulty coordinating a metered-dose inhaler (MDI) should use a **spacer** to ensure the medication reaches the lungs rather than being trapped in the mouth.

### 8. General Nursing and Contraindications

* **Contraindications:** Beta agonists are contraindicated in patients at high risk for **stroke** due to their vasoconstrictive/hypertensive effects. Theophylline should be avoided in patients with **uncontrolled cardiac dysrhythmias** (like A-fib with a high heart rate).
* **Assessments:** Before administering respiratory drugs, always assess breath sounds, respiratory rate, and oxygen saturation. For corticosteroids, a sputum test may be performed initially to rule out existing Candida infections.
