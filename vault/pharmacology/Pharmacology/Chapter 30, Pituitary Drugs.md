# Chapter 30, Pituitary Drugs

Based on the performance details provided, a student must master the following clinical knowledge regarding pituitary drugs, including their mechanisms, indications, contraindications, and critical safety nuances.

### Anterior Pituitary Drugs

#### Cosyntropin (Synthetic ACTH)

* **Primary Use:** Used primarily for the diagnosis of adrenocortical insufficiency. The test involves measuring cortisol levels in response to a cosyntropin injection.
* **Mechanism and Effects:** It stimulates the adrenal cortex to release cortisol. Cortisol has anti-inflammatory effects but also promotes renal retention of sodium.
* **Critical Nuances:**
* **Fluid Balance:** Due to sodium retention, students must monitor for edema, hypertension, and hypervolemia.
* **Immune System:** Cortisol can reduce white blood cell (leukocyte) counts, potentially masking or increasing the risk for infection.



#### Somatropin and Somatrem (Growth Hormone Analogs)

* **Indications:** Growth hormone deficiency in children and wasting syndrome (cachexia) associated with HIV.
* **Metabolic Action:** These drugs stimulate skeletal growth by mobilizing lipids from fat stores, retaining sodium, potassium, and phosphorus, and stimulating anabolic (tissue-building) processes.
* **Contraindications:**
* **Growth Plates:** Must not be used in children with closed growth plates.
* **Disease States:** Contraindicated in patients with active neoplastic disease (tumors) or acute illnesses (e.g., severe respiratory distress or asthma).


* **Monitoring and Safety:**
* **Metabolic Impacts:** These drugs cause liver glycogenolysis, leading to hyperglycemia. Glucose and calcium levels must be monitored.
* **Physical Assessment:** Students must monitor height, weight, and motor skills. Parents should be taught to report "limping" immediately, as this can indicate issues with bone mass or joint slippage.
* **Interactions:** Glucocorticoid therapy can decrease the therapeutic growth effects of somatropin.



### Posterior Pituitary Drugs

#### Vasopressin and Desmopressin (ADH Analogs)

* **Indications:** * **Diabetes Insipidus:** Both are used to prevent or control polyuria (excessive urination), polydipsia (excessive thirst), and dehydration.
* **Bleeding Disorders:** Desmopressin is specifically used for Hemophilia A and Type I von Willebrand disease because it increases blood-clotting factors.
* **Emergency Care:** Vasopressin is used for vasodilatory shock, GI hemorrhage, and esophageal varices.


* **Administration Nuances:**
* **Intranasal Application:** Nasal preparations must be applied topically to the nasal membranes, not inhaled into the airway. Inhaling the drug can cause respiratory irritation and reduce efficacy.
* **Pump Maintenance:** Nasal pumps must be primed (typically 4 presses) initially and once a week thereafter.
* **IV Safety:** Intravenous vasopressin is a potent vasoconstrictor. If it infiltrates, it can cause severe tissue necrosis. The IV site must be monitored with extreme care.


* **Monitoring and Toxicity:**
* **Water Intoxication:** Excessive antidiuretic effect can lead to water intoxication. Symptoms include headache, drowsiness, listlessness, and seizures.
* **Fluid Status:** Serum sodium levels must be monitored. Low sodium indicates a need to adjust fluid intake to avoid water intoxication.


* **Drug-Drug and Substance Interactions:**
* **Carbamazepine:** Enhances the effects of these drugs, increasing water retention.
* **Alcohol:** Reduces the effects of desmopressin, leading to increased urination and thirst.
* **Norepinephrine/Demeclocycline:** Both can decrease the antidiuretic effect of vasopressin.



### Somatostatin Analogs

#### Octreotide

* **Indications:** Used to treat symptoms of carcinoid tumors (VIPomas) such as severe watery diarrhea and flushing. It is also used for life-threatening hypotension in carcinoid crisis and for esophageal varices.
* **Adverse Effects:** Commonly causes malaise, dyspnea, arthralgia, and fatigue.
* **Gastrointestinal Nuances:** Unlike many other drugs, octreotide causes diarrhea rather than constipation. It can also cause gallbladder impairment and abdominal stress.
* **Contraindications and Cautions:**
* **Renal/Hepatic:** Must be used with caution in patients with renal impairment.
* **Diabetes:** Can cause severe hypoglycemia, particularly in patients with Type 1 Diabetes.


* **Critical Interactions:**
* **Ciprofloxacin:** Using ciprofloxacin with octreotide can lead to a prolonged QT interval on an EKG, increasing the risk for cardiac dysrhythmias.
* **Cyclosporine:** Octreotide can suppress the effects of cyclosporine, potentially leading to organ transplant rejection.



### General Nursing Mastery Points

* **Baseline Assessments:** Before administering any pituitary hormones, a nurse must obtain baseline weight, vital signs (especially blood pressure), and blood glucose levels.
* **Diagnostic Priority:** If a patient is receiving octreotide and is prescribed a new medication like ciprofloxacin, the priority diagnostic test is an Electrocardiogram (ECG) due to the cardiac risk.
* **Medication Adherence:** For related endocrine therapies (like thyroid replacement), patients who miss two or more doses in a row must consult their primary health care provider immediately rather than doubling up or skipping.
* **Safety Checks:** If a vasopressin solution contains visible particles, it is contaminated and must not be administered. Heating or shaking will not resolve this issue.
