# Chapter 4, Cultural, Legal, and Ethical Considerations

To master the material in this chapter, a student must understand the complex intersection of pharmacology, patient rights, cultural diversity, and the legal framework governing nursing practice. Below are the main points and critical nuances derived from the study material.

### Main Points for Mastery

#### 1. Phases of Clinical Drug Trials

* **Phase I:** Conducted on a small number of **healthy volunteers**. The primary goals are to determine the drug's pharmacokinetics and the **optimal dosage range**.
* **Phase II:** Involves a small number of volunteers who actually have the disease the drug is intended to treat. The focus is on monitoring the drug's effectiveness and identifying adverse effects.
* **Phase III:** Conducted on large numbers of patients with the disease. This phase establishes clinical effectiveness, safety, and a refined dosage range. An **Investigational New Drug (IND) application** is submitted to the FDA for approval only **after Phase III** is completed.
* **Phase IV:** Postmarketing studies conducted after the drug is available to the public. These studies provide further data on therapeutic effects and compare the drug with others in the same therapeutic category.

#### 2. Ethical Principles in Nursing

* **Autonomy:** Supporting a patient's right to make their own healthcare decisions and ensuring informed consent.
* **Beneficence:** The duty to actively promote good and act in the best interest of the patient (e.g., using evidence-based research to find the best interventions).
* **Confidentiality:** The duty to respect and protect privileged patient information, including not discussing patients on social media.
* **Justice:** Providing fair and equal treatment to all patients regardless of their financial status or background; prioritizing care based on the severity of need.
* **Nonmaleficence:** The fundamental duty to "do no harm" to the patient while they are under a nurse's care.
* **Veracity:** The duty to tell the truth and avoid providing false reassurances to patients regarding risks and procedures.

#### 3. Legal and Regulatory Frameworks

* **Nurse Practice Acts:** State laws that define the boundaries of nursing practice, including educational requirements, expanded nursing roles, and the legal differences between nursing and medical practice.
* **Drug Recalls:**
* **Class I:** The most serious; the drug is unsafe and likely to cause serious health effects or death.
* **Class II:** The drug may cause temporary or medically reversible health effects.
* **Class III:** The least severe; the drug is unlikely to cause significant health problems.


* **Controlled Substances (C-IV):** Medications like diazepam are controlled substances that require a warning label, are dispensed by written or oral prescription, and typically have a 6-month limit with a maximum of five refills.

#### 4. Informed Consent

Informed consent is a voluntary decision and a written document that must include the purpose of the trial, procedures involved, and all potential risks. For consent to be valid, the subject must be mentally stable, have a full understanding of the details, and provide a signature that is dated and witnessed.

### Nuances to be Mastered

#### 1. Ethnopharmacology and Genetic Polymorphism

Students must understand that "drug polymorphism" refers to how a patient's age, gender, size, and body composition—often influenced by race and genetics—affect pharmacokinetics.

* **Acetylation Rates:** * **Rapid Acetylators:** (e.g., those of Japanese descent) metabolize certain drugs (like isoniazid) more quickly and may require **higher doses** to achieve therapeutic effects.
* **Slow Acetylators:** (e.g., some European and African races) metabolize drugs more slowly, leading to higher blood concentrations and a need for **lower doses** to avoid toxicity.


* **Specific Drug Responses:** African American patients often respond best to **calcium channel blockers** for hypertension, while ACE inhibitors and Beta blockers are frequently less effective in this population.

#### 2. Cultural Assessment and Communication

* **Communication Nuances:** * **African Culture:** Maintaining direct eye contact may be viewed as rude. In many African families, the woman of the house is the primary healthcare decision-maker.
* **Asian Culture:** Patients may be uncomfortable with handshakes from the opposite sex. They often use a "hot/cold" theory where "cold" illnesses (like those with phlegm) are treated with "hot" foods (soups and teas).
* **Hispanic Culture:** Patients may seek a "curandero" for spiritual healing. They may use heat therapy (like a heating pad) to treat specific ailments like back pain.
* **Native American Culture:** Illness may be viewed as a result of evil spirits or disharmony with nature.


* **Language Barrier Strategies:** When speaking with patients with limited English proficiency, avoid using contractions (e.g., use "do not" instead of "don't") and complex verb tenses.

#### 3. Clinical Trial Safeguards

* **Blinding:** In a "single-blind" study, the researcher knows who receives the drug/placebo, but the subject does not. In a "double-blind" study, neither the researcher nor the subject knows.
* **Breaking the Code:** The code in a double-blind study is only broken in **emergency situations**, such as when a subject develops a severe adverse reaction that requires immediate treatment.
* **Placebos:** These are used specifically to eliminate **subject bias**, where a patient's perception of improvement might otherwise skew the results of the drug's efficacy.

#### 4. Liability and Documentation

A critical nuance in legal liability is distinguishing between "medication errors" (e.g., failing to clarify an unclear order) and **"failure to assess/evaluate"** (e.g., failing to document a patient's changing condition after medication administration). Failure to document is a primary area of liability.
