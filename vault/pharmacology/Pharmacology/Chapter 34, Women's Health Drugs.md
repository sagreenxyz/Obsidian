# Chapter 34, Women's Health Drugs

To master the pharmacology of women's health drugs, a student must understand the mechanisms, administration protocols, contraindications, and specific nursing interventions for several distinct drug classes.

### Main Points for Mastery

#### 1. Hormonal Contraceptives and Estrogen Therapy

* **Mechanism of Action:** Oral contraceptives prevent pregnancy primarily by inhibiting the release of gonadotropins (FSH and LH), which prevents ovulation. They also increase uterine mucus viscosity to impede sperm movement and may inhibit the implantation of a zygote.
* **Estrogen Risks and Benefits:**
* **Indications:** Treatment of atrophic vaginitis, female hypogonadism, ovarian failure, abnormal uterine bleeding, and severe vasomotor symptoms (hot flashes). It is also used palliatively for prostate cancer.
* **Endometrial Cancer:** Unopposed estrogen (estrogen given alone to a patient with an intact uterus) significantly increases the risk of endometrial hyperplasia and cancer.
* **Breast Cancer:** Estrogen combined with progestin is associated with an increased risk of breast cancer.


* **Contraindications:** Estrogen therapy and oral contraceptives are strictly contraindicated in patients with a history of thromboembolic events (Deep Vein Thrombosis, Pulmonary Embolism) or undiagnosed abnormal vaginal bleeding.
* **Progestin-Only Options:** The "minipill" is suitable for women who cannot take estrogen. It must be taken at the exact same time every day and may cause breakthrough bleeding.

#### 2. Osteoporosis Management

* **Bisphosphonates (e.g., Alendronate, Risedronate, Ibandronate):**
* **Administration:** Must be taken first thing in the morning on an empty stomach with 8 oz of plain water. The patient must remain upright for at least 30 minutes to prevent erosive esophagitis.
* **Contraindications:** Hypocalcemia and esophageal dysfunction.


* **Selective Estrogen Receptor Modulators (SERMs) (e.g., Raloxifene):**
* **Mechanism:** Stimulates estrogen receptors on bone to increase density.
* **Major Side Effect:** Hot flashes (unlike estrogen, which treats them).
* **Contraindication:** History of DVT/PE.


* **Calcitonin:** Derived from salmon; therefore, it is contraindicated in patients with a salmon allergy.
* **Denosumab:** Contraindicated in patients with renal failure or renal dysfunction.
* **Teriparatide:** Can cause vertigo, joint pain, and chest pain.

#### 3. Labor and Delivery Medications

* **Uterine Stimulants (Oxytocics):**
* **Oxytocin:** Used for labor induction and postpartum bleeding. Nurses must monitor contractions; if they occur more frequently than every 2 minutes, the infusion must be stopped to prevent uterine rupture.
* **Dinoprostone:** Used for cervical ripening prior to labor induction.
* **Methylergonovine:** Used specifically for postpartum hemorrhage and uterine atony. It must only be administered *after* the delivery of the placenta.


* **Tocolytics (Uterine Relaxants):**
* **Indomethacin:** Used for preterm labor. If the patient develops hypotension, place them in the left lateral recumbent position.
* **Magnesium Sulfate:** Used for preterm labor. Toxicity is marked by depressed reflexes and confusion. The antidote is **calcium gluconate**.
* **Conservative Measures:** Bed rest and hydration are the best initial therapies for preterm labor, especially in diabetic patients, as drugs like Terbutaline can increase blood sugar.



#### 4. Fertility Drugs

* **Clomiphene:** Stimulates the production of gonadotropins to induce ovulation.
* **Adverse Effects to Report:** Anxiety, dizziness, blurred vision, and tachycardia.
* **Contraindications:** Ovarian failure and abnormal uterine bleeding.

### Critical Nuances to Master

* **Antibiotic Interactions:** Antibiotics (especially penicillins and cephalosporins) can impair the action of oral contraceptives. Patients must use a backup method of birth control for at least one month after starting the antibiotic.
* **Smoking Risks:** Cigarette smoking while taking oral contraceptives or estrogen therapy carries a "Black Box Warning" due to the massively increased risk of serious cardiovascular events and thromboembolism.
* **Medication Spacing:** Calcium supplements interfere with the absorption of bisphosphonates. They must be spaced at least 1 to 2 hours apart.
* **Thyroid Interference:** Estradiol can interfere with the absorption of thyroid hormones (e.g., Levothyroxine), necessitating careful monitoring and potential dose adjustments.
* **Prolactin Levels:** Tricyclic antidepressants can interact with fertility drugs to increase prolactin concentrations, which can further impair fertility.
* **The Progestin Benefit:** The primary reason progestin is added to an estrogen regimen for a postmenopausal woman is specifically to reduce the risk of endometrial cancer.
* **Ospemifene:** This specific medication is effective for treating painful intercourse (dyspareunia) in postmenopausal women, rather than treating hot flashes or improving libido.
* **Soy Isoflavones:** For patients unwilling to use hormone therapy for menopause, soy can be suggested as it contains isoflavones chemically similar to estradiol.
