# Chapter 27, Antilipemic Drugs

### Main Points for the Student

**1. Primary Drug Classes and Mechanisms**

* **HMG-CoA Reductase Inhibitors (Statins):** These are the first-line therapy for hypercholesterolemia. They work by inhibiting the enzyme hydroxymethylglutaryl-coenzyme A (HMG-CoA) reductase, which is the rate-limiting step in cholesterol synthesis in the liver.
* **Bile Acid Sequestrants:** Examples include cholestyramine, colestipol, and colesevelam. They bind to bile acids in the small intestine, preventing their resorption and forcing the liver to use cholesterol to produce more bile.
* **Niacin (Nicotinic Acid):** A vitamin (B3) that, in high doses, reduces triglycerides and increases high-density lipoprotein (HDL) levels.
* **Fibric Acid Derivatives (Fibrates):** Examples include gemfibrozil and fenofibrate. They activate lipoprotein lipase, which breaks down cholesterol and is highly effective at lowering triglyceride levels.
* **Cholesterol Absorption Inhibitors:** Ezetimibe is the primary drug in this class; it works specifically in the small intestine to inhibit the absorption of dietary and biliary cholesterol.

**2. Critical Safety and Adverse Effects**

* **Rhabdomyolysis:** A life-threatening breakdown of muscle protein that can lead to acute renal failure. This is a severe adverse effect of statins.
* **Liver Toxicity:** Most antilipemics, especially statins and niacin, can cause elevations in liver enzymes. Baseline and periodic liver function tests (LFTs) are essential.
* **Gastrointestinal Effects:** Bile acid sequestrants frequently cause constipation and bloating. Statins can cause nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain.
* **Cutaneous Flushing:** Niacin is notorious for causing intense flushing of the face and neck, often accompanied by pruritus (itching).

**3. Key Contraindications**

* **Liver Disease:** Active liver disease or unexplained persistent elevations in liver enzymes are contraindications for most antilipemics (especially statins, niacin, and fibrates).
* **Pregnancy:** Many statins (e.g., simvastatin, atorvastatin) are Category X and must not be used during pregnancy due to teratogenic risks.

### Nuances to be Mastered

**1. Administration Timing and Dietary Nuances**

* **The "Evening Rule":** Statins (like atorvastatin and simvastatin) should be administered in the evening or at bedtime. This is because the liver produces the majority of cholesterol during the night.
* **Lipid Profile Prep:** Patients must fast for 12 to 14 hours before a lipid profile blood test to ensure accuracy.
* **The Grapefruit Interaction:** Consuming more than one quart of grapefruit juice daily while on statins significantly increases the risk of rhabdomyolysis by inhibiting the CYP3A4 enzyme responsible for metabolizing the drug.

**2. Specialized Administration for Bile Acid Sequestrants**

* **The "1 and 4/6 Rule":** Because these drugs bind to almost anything in the gut, other medications must be taken at least 1 hour before or 4 to 6 hours after the bile acid sequestrant to prevent absorption interference.
* **Preparation:** Powdered forms (cholestyramine/colestipol) must be mixed with at least 4 to 6 ounces of fluid. They should never be taken dry or just sprinkled on food without dilution, as this poses a risk for esophageal irritation or obstruction.

**3. Managing Side Effects**

* **Niacin Flushing:** This can be minimized by taking a small dose of aspirin (or an NSAID) 30 minutes before the niacin dose. Starting with a low dose and titrating up, or using extended-release formulations, also helps.
* **Bile Acid Sequestrant Constipation:** Students must prioritize teaching patients to increase both fluid and fiber intake to counteract the constipating effects.

**4. Clinical "Red Flags"**

* **Muscle Pain vs. Normal Aches:** Any unexplained muscle pain, soreness, or weakness while on a statin or fibrate must be reported immediately. If the urine turns "brown" (myoglobinuria), it is a sign of advanced rhabdomyolysis and requires emergency intervention.
* **Statins and Gemfibrozil:** Combining these two classes significantly potentiates the risk of myopathy and rhabdomyolysis.

**5. Population-Specific Considerations**

* **Diabetics:** Niacin can worsen glucose control and should be used with caution. Conversely, Omega-3 fatty acids (fish oil) are often beneficial for diabetics with hyperlipidemia as they don't typically interfere with glucose.
* **Organ Transplant Patients:** Colesevelam is often preferred for high-risk patients (like those with kidney transplants) because it is better tolerated and has fewer drug interactions than other classes.
* **Nutritional Deficiencies:** Long-term use of bile acid sequestrants can lead to deficiencies in fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, and K), necessitating supplementation.

**6. Herbal Interactions**

* **Garlic:** Has a significant interaction with warfarin, increasing the risk of bleeding.
* **Flaxseed:** Can interact with and alter the effectiveness of antidiabetic drugs.
