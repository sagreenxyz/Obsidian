# Chapter 11, General and Local Anesthetics

To achieve mastery of General and Local Anesthesia, a student must understand the core classifications, the specific mechanisms of various agents, and the clinical nuances regarding patient safety and physiological responses.

### Main Points for Mastery

* **Classifications of Anesthesia:**
* **General Anesthesia:** A drug-induced state characterized by a complete loss of consciousness and body reflexes, including the involuntary reflex to breathe. It alters central nervous system nerve impulses to produce deep visceral and skeletal muscle relaxation.
* **Local Anesthesia:** Eliminates pain sensation in a specific, limited area of the body without loss of consciousness or the ability to breathe independently.
* **Balanced Anesthesia:** The practice of using a combination of different drug classes (general anesthetics plus adjuncts) to achieve the desired anesthetic state.
* **Monitored Anesthesia Care (MAC):** A procedure involving local anesthesia along with sedation and analgesia. The patient remains conscious and able to protect their own airway.
* **Moderate (Conscious) Sedation:** A state where the patient can follow commands but will typically have no memory of the procedure afterward.


* **Administration Routes and Specific Agents:**
* **Inhalational General Anesthetics:** Includes volatile liquids like **isoflurane** and **sevoflurane**, and gases such as **nitrous oxide**. Nitrous oxide is the weakest general anesthetic and is often used for dental or mild procedures.
* **Parenteral (IV) General Anesthetics:** Includes **propofol** (standard for induction and maintenance) and **ketamine** (a short-acting sedative frequently used in emergency departments for procedures like setting broken bones).
* **Local Anesthetic Techniques:**
* **Infiltration:** Injection into the tissue surrounding an operative site (e.g., dental surgery or wound suturing).
* **Nerve Block:** Injection at the site where a nerve innervates a specific area (e.g., for chronic orthopedic pain).
* **Intrathecal Anesthesia:** Injection into the subarachnoid space (e.g., for abdominal or limb surgery).
* **Epidural Anesthesia:** Injection via a catheter into the epidural space (e.g., for labor, delivery, or pelvic surgery).




* **Malignant Hyperthermia (MH):**
* **Nature:** A rare, genetically linked, potentially fatal metabolic reaction to general anesthesia.
* **Triggers:** **Succinylcholine** is strictly contraindicated in patients with a personal or familial history of MH.
* **Symptoms:** Tachycardia, tachypnea, muscular rigidity, and a rapid, dangerous rise in body temperature.
* **Treatment:** **Dantrolene** is the primary muscle relaxant used to treat this condition.


* **Neuromuscular Blocking Drugs (NMBDs):**
* **Mechanism:** These agents (e.g., vecuronium, pancuronium, rocuronium) prevent nerve transmission in skeletal and smooth muscles, leading to total flaccid paralysis.
* **Antidotes:** For nondepolarizing NMBDs like vecuronium, the nurse must have **anticholinesterase drugs** available, such as **neostigmine**, pyridostigmine, or edrophonium.



### Nuances to Master

* **Order of Functional Loss and Recovery:** A critical nuance is that functions regulated by the **medulla of the brainstem**—specifically cardiac and pulmonary functions—are the **last** to be interrupted by anesthesia. In contrast, visual acuity, consciousness, and hearing sensation are the first to be lost.
* **The Role of Epinephrine Synergy:** Epinephrine is frequently co-administered with local anesthetics like lidocaine because it is a **vasoconstrictor**. This serves three specific clinical purposes:
1. Maintaining the localized activity of the anesthetic drug.
2. Reducing local blood loss during minor surgical procedures.
3. Minimizing the risk of systemic drug toxicity.


* **Sequence of Muscle Paralysis:** When NMBDs are administered, paralysis follows a specific order: small, rapidly moving muscles (the **eyes**) are paralyzed first, followed by the neck, limbs, and finally the trunk.
* **NMBD Sensitivity Factors:** A student must distinguish which conditions alter a patient's response to NMBDs:
* **Increased Sensitivity/Toxicity Risk:** Acidosis, poliomyelitis, and myasthenia gravis.
* **Decreased Sensitivity/Opposing Effects:** Hemiplegia and clostridial infections.


* **Specific Complications:**
* **Spinal Headache:** A notable complication specifically associated with centrally acting local anesthesia (intrathecal or epidural) due to injury to spinal structures.
* **Muscle Fasciculations:** These muscle spasms are a result of damage caused to the muscles by **NMBDs**, not local anesthetics.
* **Respiratory Arrest:** While local anesthesia preserves breathing, high doses of general parenteral or inhalational anesthetics can lead to respiratory depression or arrest.


* **Contraindications for Succinylcholine:** beyond MH, it is contraindicated in conditions predisposing to hyperkalemia, such as major burns, multiple trauma, upper motor neuron disease, or denervation of skeletal muscles.

# Chapter 11, General and Local Anesthetics

### Main Points for Mastery

**1. Classification of Anesthesia**

* **General Anesthesia:** Induces a state of unconsciousness, skeletal muscle relaxation, and visceral smooth muscle relaxation. It is often achieved through a "balanced approach," using a combination of drugs (adjuncts) to minimize side effects and improve recovery.
* **Local Anesthesia:** Renders a specific portion of the body insensitive to pain without causing loss of consciousness. This includes infiltration, nerve blocks, and spinal/epidural techniques.
* **Moderate Sedation:** Also known as conscious sedation (e.g., Midazolam), it allows the patient to remain relaxed and reduce anxiety during minor procedures while maintaining their own airway and responding to verbal commands.

**2. Neuromuscular Blocking Drugs (NMBDs)**

* **Depolarizing NMBDs:** Succinylcholine is the primary example. It mimics acetylcholine, causing muscle fasciculations (twitches) followed by paralysis. It is preferred for emergency intubation due to its rapid onset (<1 minute).
* **Nondepolarizing NMBDs:** These include Vecuronium, Rocuronium (intermediate-acting), and Pancuronium (long-acting). They block acetylcholine receptors without causing initial contraction.
* **Reversal:** Pyridostigmine (an anticholinesterase) is used to reverse the effects of nondepolarizing NMBDs by increasing the levels of acetylcholine at the neuromuscular junction.

**3. Malignant Hyperthermia**

* This is a life-threatening, genetically linked metabolic reaction triggered by volatile general anesthetics or succinylcholine.
* **Key Symptoms:** Rapid rise in body temperature (up to 104°F+), tachycardia, tachypnea, and muscle rigidity.
* **Treatment:** Immediate administration of **Dantrolene** (a skeletal muscle relaxant) and cardiorespiratory supportive care.

**4. Clinical Pharmacology of Common Agents**

* **Propofol:** A parenteral general anesthetic used for induction and maintenance. It is also the drug of choice for sedating mechanically ventilated patients in the ICU.
* **Ketamine:** A short-acting anesthetic often used in the emergency department for procedures like setting broken bones.
* **Midazolam:** A benzodiazepine used for moderate sedation and preoperative amnesia.
* **Lidocaine:** A local anesthetic often paired with **Epinephrine**.

**5. Nursing Assessment and Postoperative Care**

* **Respiratory Care:** Post-op actions like turning, coughing, and deep breathing every 2 hours are critical to prevent atelectasis.
* **Safety:** Patients receiving moderate sedation (like Midazolam) must be instructed not to drive for at least 24 hours.
* **Vital Signs:** Spinal anesthesia requires close monitoring of blood pressure due to potential vasodilation and hypotension.

### Nuances to Master

**1. Herbal and Drug Interactions**
Specific herbs pose distinct risks when combined with anesthesia:

* **Garlic and Feverfew:** Increase the risk of bleeding.
* **St. John's wort:** Can lead to blood pressure changes and prolonged sedation.
* **Kava:** May cause liver toxicity.
* **Ginger:** Can cause oversedation.
* **Ginseng:** May cause irritability or insomnia.

**2. The Use of Vasoconstrictors (Epinephrine)**
When lidocaine is administered with epinephrine, the epinephrine causes localized vasoconstriction. This serves two purposes: it minimizes bleeding at the site and slows the systemic absorption of the anesthetic, which prolongs the numbing effect and maintains localized activity.

**3. Electrolyte Imbalances and NMBDs**
NMBDs can become toxic or lead to complications depending on lab values. Specifically, **hypokalemia** (low potassium) and **hypocalcemia** increase the risk of toxicity. Conversely, succinylcholine can cause **hyperkalemia** because the initial muscle injury/fasciculation releases potassium into the bloodstream.

**4. Spinal Anesthesia Complications**

* **Spinal Headache:** Caused by the leakage of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) through a dural puncture.
* **Management:** Mild cases are treated with bed rest, analgesics, and caffeine. Severe or persistent headaches are treated with a **blood patch**, where a small volume of the patient's own blood is injected into the epidural space to seal the leak.

**5. Physiological Progression of Anesthesia**
According to the Overton-Meyer theory, anesthetic potency is directly related to lipid solubility. A critical nuance is the order in which functions are lost and regained:

* **Last to be affected:** The medullary center of the brain (controlling cardiac and pulmonary/vital functions).
* **First to be lost:** Consciousness, hearing, and visual acuity.
* **Recovery:** The medullary center is the first to regain function, ensuring vital stability as the patient wakes.

**6. Patient Risk Factors**

* **Alcohol Use:** Chronic alcohol consumption alters the response to anesthesia, often requiring dose adjustments and increasing the risk of withdrawal during recovery.
* **Smoking:** Nicotine paralyzes cilia, which interferes with the clearance of anesthetic gases and increases respiratory risk.
* **Liver/Renal Failure:** These conditions significantly impair drug metabolism and excretion, increasing the risk of toxicity and prolonged effects.
* **Pregnancy:** Anesthetics can have teratogenic effects, particularly in the first trimester.
