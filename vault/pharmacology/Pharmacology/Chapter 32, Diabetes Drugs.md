# Chapter 32, Diabetes Drugs

To achieve proficiency in the pharmacology of diabetes management, a student must master the foundational principles of glucose regulation, the specific characteristics of various insulin types, and the complex clinical considerations for oral and non-insulin injectable antidiabetics.

### Main Points for Student Mastery

**1. Glucose Homeostasis and Pathophysiology**

* **Hormonal Regulation:** Insulin and glucagon are the primary hormones produced by the pancreas. Insulin facilitates the transport of glucose into cells for energy and storage (glycogenesis), while glucagon mobilizes glucose from the liver when blood sugar is low.
* **Diabetes Types:** Type 1 is characterized by a lack of endogenous insulin due to beta-cell destruction, requiring lifelong insulin therapy. Type 2 involves insulin resistance and/or deficiency, often managed initially with lifestyle changes and oral medications.
* **Diagnostic Criteria:** Diabetes is indicated by a Hemoglobin A1C level greater than 6.5%, a fasting plasma glucose level of 126 mg/dL or higher, or a random/2-hour plasma glucose level of 200 mg/dL or higher.

**2. Insulin Therapy Fundamentals**

* **Rapid-Acting (Lispro, Aspart, Glulisine):** Onset is approximately 15 minutes. It is imperative that patients eat immediately after administration to prevent hypoglycemia.
* **Short-Acting (Regular Insulin):** This is the only insulin that can be administered intravenously (common in DKA). It peaks in about 2.5 hours and should be given 30 minutes before meals.
* **Intermediate-Acting (NPH):** A cloudy suspension with an onset of 1–2 hours. It often peaks in the late afternoon; if a patient receives it at 8:00 a.m., they are at high risk for hypoglycemia by 5:00 p.m. if they skip a mid-afternoon snack.
* **Long-Acting (Glargine, Detemir):** Provides basal coverage for 24 hours with no distinct peak.

**3. Oral Antidiabetic Agents**

* **Biguanides (Metformin):** The first-line drug for Type 2 diabetes. It works by decreasing hepatic glucose production and increasing insulin sensitivity. It does not typically cause hypoglycemia when used alone.
* **Sulfonylureas (Glipizide, Glyburide, Glimepiride):** These stimulate the pancreas to release insulin. They carry a significant risk of hypoglycemia.
* **Glinides (Repaglinide, Nateglinide):** Similar to sulfonylureas but with a much shorter duration. They must be taken with each meal.
* **Alpha-Glucosidase Inhibitors (Acarbose, Miglitol):** These work locally in the small intestine to delay the absorption of carbohydrates.

**4. Emergency Complications**

* **Hypoglycemia:** Early symptoms include tremors, sweating, confusion, and irritability. Treatment for conscious patients involves fast-acting carbohydrates. For unconscious patients, intravenous glucose or glucagon is used.
* **Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA):** Characterized by extreme hyperglycemia, ketones in the urine/serum, isotonic dehydration, and an acidic pH (below 7.35).
* **Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic Syndrome (HHS):** Typically seen in Type 2 patients; involves blood glucose levels above 600 mg/dL and high serum osmolality (greater than 350 mOsm/kg) without significant ketoacidosis.

### Nuances and Critical Clinical Considerations

**1. Insulin Mixing and Administration Nuances**

* **The "Clear to Cloudy" Rule:** When mixing Regular (clear) and NPH (cloudy) insulin, always draw the Regular insulin into the syringe first. This prevents contaminating the Regular vial with NPH, which would alter its onset.
* **Glargine Restriction:** Insulin glargine must never be mixed with any other insulin in the same syringe due to its low pH.
* **Injection Technique:** Injections must be subcutaneous, typically at a 45- to 90-degree angle. Sites must be rotated weekly (not daily) within one anatomical area to prevent lipodystrophy.

**2. Metformin Specifics and Safety**

* **Renal Function:** Metformin is excreted by the kidneys. It is contraindicated in patients with renal dysfunction (Creatinine Clearance < 1.4 mg/dL in females or < 1.5 mg/dL in males) due to the risk of lactic acidosis.
* **Contrast Media:** Metformin must be held on the day of any radiologic study using iodine-based contrast and for 48 hours afterward to prevent acute renal failure and lactic acidosis.
* **Side Effect Management:** GI upset (bloating, nausea, diarrhea) is common initially; taking the medication with food significantly reduces these effects.
* **Lactic Acidosis Recognition:** Students must identify muscle pain, abdominal pain, and cold/clammy skin as signs of this rare but lethal complication.

**3. Timing and "Skip a Meal, Skip a Dose"**

* **Glipizide Timing:** Unlike other sulfonylureas, glipizide should be taken specifically 30 minutes before a meal because food inhibits its absorption.
* **Glinides Flexibility:** Repaglinide and Nateglinide are meal-dependent. If a patient skips a meal, they must skip the dose to avoid severe hypoglycemia.

**4. Drug Interactions and Masking**

* **Beta-Blockers:** These are dangerous for diabetic patients because they block the sympathetic response to hypoglycemia, meaning the patient may not feel the "warning signs" like tremors or tachycardia.
* **Pramlintide (Amylin Agonist):** When adding this to an insulin regimen, the insulin dose must be reduced by 50% to prevent sudden, severe hypoglycemia.

**5. Contraindications and Specialized Populations**

* **Gastrointestinal Disorders:** Acarbose is contraindicated in patients with inflammatory bowel disease (IBD). Pramlintide and exenatide are contraindicated in patients with gastroparesis.
* **Renal Safety:** Glipizide is unique because it is safer for use in patients with renal failure compared to other oral agents.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:** Insulin is the preferred treatment. Breastfeeding mothers require very tight glycemic control, as both hyper- and hypoglycemia can significantly decrease milk production.
* **Pediatrics:** Insulin lispro is generally not used in children under 3 years of age.

**6. Emergency Positioning**

* When administering glucagon to an unconscious patient, they must be rolled onto their side. Glucagon commonly induces vomiting, and this position prevents aspiration.
