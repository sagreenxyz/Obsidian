# Chapter 20, Cholinergic Drugs

To master the pharmacology of cholinergic drugs, a student must understand the fundamental stimulation of the parasympathetic nervous system (PSNS), the specific mechanisms of direct and indirect agonists, and the critical nursing interventions required for safe administration.

### Main Points to Know

**1. Mechanism and Classification**

* **Cholinergic Agonists:** These drugs mimic the effects of acetylcholine (ACh). They are also known as parasympathomimetics.
* **Direct-Acting Agonists:** Bind directly to cholinergic receptors and activate them (e.g., Bethanechol, Pilocarpine, Succinylcholine).
* **Indirect-Acting Agonists (Cholinesterase Inhibitors):** Inhibit the enzyme acetylcholinesterase, which breaks down ACh. This results in more ACh available at the receptors. These are further divided into:
* **Reversible:** Bind to cholinesterase for a short period (e.g., Donepezil, Pyridostigmine, Physostigmine, Edrophonium).
* **Irreversible:** Have a long-lasting effect and are often found in insecticides and nerve gases.



**2. Physiological Effects ("Rest and Digest")**

* **Gastrointestinal:** Increased motility, increased secretions, and relaxation of sphincters.
* **Genitourinary:** Increased detrusor muscle tone and relaxation of the trigone and sphincter, leading to micturition (urination).
* **Cardiovascular:** Reduced heart rate (bradycardia), vasodilation, and potential hypotension.
* **Respiratory:** Bronchoconstriction and increased bronchial secretions.
* **Ocular:** Miosis (pupil constriction) and decreased intraocular pressure.
* **Glandular:** Increased salivation, lacrimation, and sweating.
* **Skeletal Muscle:** Increased contraction (nicotinic effect).

**3. Primary Clinical Indications**

* **Urinary Retention:** Bethanechol is used for postoperative and postpartum nonobstructive urinary retention.
* **Alzheimer’s Disease:** Donepezil, Galantamine, and Rivastigmine help enhance memory and learning.
* **Myasthenia Gravis:** Pyridostigmine and Neostigmine improve muscle strength.
* **Glaucoma:** Pilocarpine reduces intraocular pressure.
* **Reversal of Neuromuscular Blockade:** Physostigmine and Neostigmine reverse the effects of non-depolarizing drugs used during surgery.
* **Antidote:** Physostigmine is the antidote for anticholinergic poisoning (e.g., Atropine overdose) and tricyclic antidepressant toxicity.

**4. Contraindications**

* Active bronchial asthma or COPD (due to bronchoconstriction).
* Mechanical GI or GU obstructions.
* Bradycardia, hypotension, or coronary artery disease.
* Peptic ulcer disease (due to increased gastric secretions).
* Hyperthyroidism, epilepsy, and Parkinsonism.

### Nuances to be Mastered

**1. The "Cholinergic Crisis" vs. Toxicity**

* **Early Signs:** Excessive salivation, abdominal cramping, and nausea.
* **Advanced Symptoms (SLUDGE):** Salivation, Lacrimation, Urinary incontinence, Diarrhea, GI cramps, and Emesis.
* **Life-Threatening Signs:** Severe bradycardia (heart rate of 30), hypotension, bronchoconstriction, and dyspnea.
* **Emergency Management:** Atropine is the immediate antidote for muscarinic effects. Epinephrine may be required for severe cardiovascular reactions or bronchoconstriction.

**2. Specific Drug Nuances**

* **Edrophonium (Tensilon):** Used primarily as a diagnostic tool. If muscle strength increases after administration, it confirms Myasthenia Gravis (or a myasthenic crisis). If it worsens symptoms, the patient is in a cholinergic crisis.
* **Rivastigmine:** Unlike some other Alzheimer's drugs, this is specifically indicated for dementia associated with Parkinson’s disease.
* **Donepezil:** Requires slow titration to minimize GI upset. It is not a cure and takes about 3 weeks for onset of action, with full therapeutic effects sometimes taking up to 6 weeks. It should be taken with food.
* **Bethanechol:** In a postoperative setting, if the first dose (5–10 mg) does not induce voiding, it can be repeated hourly up to a maximum of 50 mg per cycle.
* **Memantine:** While used for Alzheimer's, it is a non-cholinergic (NMDA receptor antagonist) and has different side effects like ataxia and dyspnea.

**3. Critical Nursing Priorities and Education**

* **Timing for Myasthenia Gravis:** Pyridostigmine must be administered 30 minutes before meals. This strengthens chewing and swallowing muscles to prevent aspiration.
* **Safety and Orthostatics:** Patients must be taught to rise slowly from sitting or lying positions due to the risk of syncope and orthostatic hypotension.
* **Discontinuation:** Cholinergic drugs for dementia should be tapered; sudden discontinuation can lead to rapid cognitive decline.
* **Diagnostic Safety:** When performing an edrophonium test, the nurse must have atropine, epinephrine, and cardiac monitoring (telemetry/BP) immediately available.
* **Myasthenia Gravis Education:** Patients should wear medical alert jewelry and carry a list of medications, as medical records may not be immediately accessible during a crisis.
* **Non-Pharmacological Support:** For patients with Alzheimer's, a walking program (at least 1–2 hours weekly) has been shown to support cognitive stabilization better than increasing environmental stimuli, which may cause agitation.
