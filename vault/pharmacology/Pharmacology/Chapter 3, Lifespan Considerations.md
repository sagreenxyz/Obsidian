# Chapter 3, Lifespan Considerations

To master the topic of Lifespan Considerations in pharmacology, a student must understand how physiological changes across the human life cycle—from gestation through old age—alter the way the body handles and responds to medications.

### 1. Pregnancy and Breastfeeding

The primary concern during pregnancy is the transfer of drugs across the placenta to the fetus.

* **Drug Transfer Nuances:** Transfer is most likely to occur during the **third trimester** due to enhanced blood flow, increased fetal surface area, and an increased amount of free (unbound) drug in the mother's circulation.
* **FDA Pregnancy Categories:**
* **Category B:** Animal studies show no risk; human data is unavailable.
* **Category C:** Adverse effects reported in animal fetuses; human data is unavailable.
* **Category D:** Fetal risk is confirmed, but potential benefits may warrant use in selected cases.
* **Category X:** Fetal abnormalities are confirmed; these drugs are strictly contraindicated.


* **Clinical Nuance:** For chronic conditions like diabetes, medications must be continued to prevent gestational complications that could harm both the mother and the fetus. However, conditions like **renal impairment** during pregnancy require a reduction in drug dosage to prevent toxicity.
* **Breastfeeding:** Most medications can pass into breast milk. Assessment must be made on a case-by-case basis depending on the specific drug's properties.

### 2. Pediatric Considerations

Pediatric patients are not simply "small adults"; their immature organs significantly alter pharmacokinetics.

* **Absorption:**
* **Gastric pH:** Neonates lack acid production in the stomach (higher pH) for the first two years, which affects drug dissolution and the ability to kill intestinal bacteria.
* **Gastric Emptying:** Slower in infants and children.
* **Intramuscular:** Absorption is faster but often irregular.
* **Topical:** Children have thin skin with higher permeability, leading to increased absorption of topical drugs.


* **Distribution:**
* **Body Water:** Neonates have high total body water (70–80% for full-term; 85% for premature), requiring different dosing for water-soluble drugs.
* **Protein Binding:** Neonates have lower albumin levels and lower binding capacity. This leaves more **free drug** available, increasing the risk of toxicity unless doses are decreased.
* **Blood-Brain Barrier:** This barrier is immature in infants, allowing more drugs to access the central nervous system.


* **Metabolism and Excretion:**
* Immature liver function (reduced microsomal enzymes) and immature kidneys (reduced glomerular filtration) impair the body's ability to metabolize and excrete drugs, lengthening the drug's half-life.


* **Safety Nuances:**
* **Tetracycline:** Can cause permanent discoloration of teeth.
* **Quinolones:** May cause cartilage damage.
* **Systemic Corticosteroids:** Can cause growth suppression (inhaled versions are generally safer for asthma).


* **Dosage Calculation:** Must be done using **kilograms** (never pounds) or **Body Surface Area (BSA)**. If a calculated dose falls outside the safe range, the nurse must contact the healthcare provider immediately.

### 3. Geriatric Considerations (Patients 60+)

Aging leads to a physiological decline that generally results in increased drug sensitivity and a higher risk of adverse effects.

* **Pharmacokinetic Changes:**
* **Absorption:** Gastric pH increases (less acidic). There is reduced GI blood flow (up to 50%) and reduced absorptive surface area due to flattened villi. Gastric emptying and peristalsis are delayed.
* **Distribution:** Body fat increases while total body water decreases (males ~52%, females ~46%). This causes **lipid-soluble drugs** (like sedatives) to have a prolonged effect and **water-soluble drugs** to reach higher, potentially toxic concentrations.
* **Protein Binding:** Reduced liver mass leads to lower protein (albumin) production. Highly protein-bound drugs, such as **Warfarin** and **Phenytoin**, will have more "free" drug in the plasma, increasing toxicity risk.
* **Metabolism:** Reduced liver mass and blood flow (often due to decreased cardiac output) lead to decreased metabolism.
* **Excretion:** Reduced renal blood flow and intact nephrons lead to decreased excretion. **Creatinine clearance** (monitored via 24-hour urine) is the most accurate indicator of renal function for dosing.


* **Nuances in Sensitivity:**
* **CNS:** A less integral blood-brain barrier increases sensitivity to CNS depressants like **anxiolytics** and **tricyclic antidepressants**.
* **Cardiovascular:** Decreased cardiac output affects how drugs are distributed throughout the body.



### 4. Critical Drug-Specific Nuances and Safety

* **Polypharmacy:** The highest priority when a patient is on multiple medications is assessing for **drug interactions**.
* **The "Brown-Bag Technique":** The most effective way to get an accurate medication history is to have the patient or spouse bring all current medications in their **original containers**.
* **Adverse Effect Recognition:**
* **NSAIDs:** Closely monitor for epigastric pain, which suggests gastric ulceration.
* **Opioids:** High risk for falls, constipation, and respiratory depression.
* **Anticholinergics:** Can cause dry mouth, tachycardia, and blurred vision; use with caution in patients with constipation or prostate enlargement.
* **Calcium Channel Blockers:** (e.g., Verapamil) Frequently worsen constipation in the elderly.
* **Diuretics:** Thiazide and loop diuretics may cause potassium loss (hypokalemia), often requiring potassium supplementation.
* **Laxatives:** Can reduce the absorption of other drugs by increasing GI motility.


* **Laboratory Monitoring:**
* **Liver:** AST and ALT levels.
* **Kidney:** BUN, Creatinine, and specifically Creatinine Clearance.
* **Digoxin:** Requires frequent renal function monitoring for dose adjustment.
