# Chapter 41, Antitubercular Drugs

To master the pharmacology of antitubercular drugs, a student must understand the unique nature of *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, the complexities of multi-drug regimens, and the highly specific side-effect profiles of both first-line and second-line medications.

### Pathophysiology and Diagnosis: Main Points

* **The Organism:** Tuberculosis (TB) is caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, an aerobic, slow-growing bacterium. Because it is aerobic, it thrives in oxygen-rich environments, making the lungs the primary site of infection, though it can spread to the brain and bones.
* **Transmission:** It is transmitted via droplets (sneezing/coughing) and spreads through the blood and lymphatic systems. It is also found in cattle and birds.
* **Susceptibility:** Individuals with reduced immunity—such as those with HIV, cancer, or malnutrition—are significantly more susceptible.
* **Diagnostic Sequence:** 1.  **Mantoux Test (PPD):** A screening tool using 5 tuberculin units injected intradermally.
2.  **Chest Radiograph:** Performed if the skin test is positive to look for signs of disease.
3.  **Sputum Culture:** The definitive diagnostic test to confirm the presence of the bacteria.
4.  **Drug Susceptibility Testing:** Essential to determine which drugs will be effective and to prevent the development of multidrug-resistant TB (MDR-TB).

### Pathophysiology and Diagnosis: Nuances to Master

* **Induration vs. Erythema:** A positive Mantoux test is identified by **induration** (hardness/swelling) at the injection site, not just erythema (redness). Redness alone is an inflammatory response, not a positive screening result.
* **Latent vs. Active:** Not everyone exposed to the bacteria develops active TB; the organism can remain dormant in the body without causing symptoms.
* **Vaccination Interference:** The Bacille Calmette-Guérin (BCG) vaccine is used in some countries to prevent TB, particularly in children, but it can complicate diagnosis by causing positive skin test results.

### First-Line Antitubercular Drugs: Main Points

First-line drugs are the primary choice for treatment due to their efficacy and manageable toxicity.

* **Isoniazid (INH):** The most widely used drug for both treatment and post-exposure prophylaxis. It inhibits bacterial cell wall synthesis.
* **Rifampin:** A rifamycin antibiotic often used in combination with INH.
* **Ethambutol:** A bacteriostatic drug that inhibits the incorporation of mycolic acid into the bacterial cell wall by affecting lipid synthesis.
* **Pyrazinamide:** A key component of the standard four-drug regimen.
* **Streptomycin:** An aminoglycoside that inhibits protein synthesis.

### First-Line Antitubercular Drugs: Nuances to Master

* **Isoniazid (INH) and Vitamin B6:** INH causes a pyridoxine (Vitamin B6) deficiency. Students must know that **Pyridoxine** is administered concurrently specifically to prevent peripheral neuropathy (numbness and tingling).
* **Rifampin Discoloration:** Rifampin causes a harmless red-orange-brown discoloration of urine, sweat, tears, sputum, saliva, and feces. Patients must be warned about this to prevent unnecessary alarm.
* **The Oral Contraceptive Interaction:** Rifampin makes oral contraceptives ineffective. Women of childbearing age **must** use an alternative form of birth control while on this medication.
* **Ethambutol and Vision:** The primary concern with Ethambutol is **optic neuritis**. Periodic eye examinations are mandatory to monitor for blurred vision or red-green color blindness. It is contraindicated in children under 13 because they may not be able to report these visual changes accurately.
* **Pyrazinamide and Gout:** This drug can raise uric acid levels (hyperuricemia). It is contraindicated in patients with acute gout or severe hepatic disease.
* **Streptomycin Toxicities:** As an aminoglycoside, it is notoriously nephrotoxic and ototoxic. Baseline hearing assessments and creatinine clearance tests are required before and during therapy.

### Second-Line Drugs and Complex Situations: Main Points

Second-line drugs are used when first-line drugs fail or when the patient has MDR-TB.

* **Cycloserine:** Used for active pulmonary and extrapulmonary TB (like bone TB) after first-line failure.
* **Bedaquiline:** One of the newer drugs specifically for multi-drug resistant cases.
* **Rifabutin:** Specifically indicated for preventing or delaying *Mycobacterium avium-intracellulare* complex in patients with advanced HIV.

### Second-Line Drugs and Complex Situations: Nuances to Master

* **Psychiatric Risks:** Cycloserine is known to cause seizures and **psychotic behavior**, making it a high-risk drug for patients with pre-existing psychiatric conditions.
* **Cardiac Risks:** Bedaquiline can prolong the **QT interval**. It is contraindicated in patients with heart block or arrhythmias like atrial fibrillation.
* **Pregnancy Safety:** Isoniazid, Rifampin, and Pyrazinamide are generally considered safe (Category C), and Ethambutol is Category B. However, Cycloserine and Streptomycin are typically avoided due to teratogenic risks.

### General Nursing and Therapeutic Mastery

* **Multi-Drug Rationale:** Treatment almost always involves at least four drugs to prevent the development of resistant strains. Using a single drug is a major risk factor for MDR-TB.
* **Adherence and Duration:** Because mycobacteria grow slowly and can be dormant, treatment lasts for months (often 6 to 24 months). Patients must be taught that "feeling better" is not a reason to stop the medication.
* **Timing and Absorption:** Antacids should be avoided because they significantly decrease the absorption of drugs like Isoniazid. While many medications are traditionally taken on an empty stomach, the EAQ guidance suggests taking antituberculars after meals if the patient experiences gastrointestinal ulcers or severe upset.
* **Effectiveness Indicators:** Clinical improvement is usually seen within 2 weeks. Key signs of effective treatment include weight gain, a decrease in cough, a reduction in fever, and improved chest radiographs.
* **Liver Function:** Nearly all first-line drugs (except Ethambutol and Streptomycin) are hepatotoxic. Students must monitor liver enzymes and check for jaundice (yellowing of skin/sclera). Drugs should be discontinued if liver enzymes are significantly elevated.
