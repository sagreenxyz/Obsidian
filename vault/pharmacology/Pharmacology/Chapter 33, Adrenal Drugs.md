# Chapter 33, Adrenal Drugs

Based on the performance details provided, a student must master the following main points and nuances regarding adrenal medications, specifically glucocorticoids and mineralocorticoids.

### Main Points for Mastery

**1. Drug Classifications and Indications**

* **Glucocorticoids:** These include drugs like prednisone, methylprednisolone, dexamethasone, and hydrocortisone. They are primarily used for their anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressant properties. Common indications include cerebral edema, systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), asthma, and organ transplant rejection prevention.
* **Mineralocorticoids:** Fludrocortisone is the primary synthetic mineralocorticoid. It is used as partial replacement therapy for adrenocortical insufficiency in Addison’s disease and for salt-losing adrenogenital syndrome.
* **Antiadrenal Drugs:** These drugs suppress the adrenal cortex and are used to treat adrenal malignancy, Cushing’s syndrome, and metastatic breast cancer.

**2. Major Pathologies: Addison's vs. Cushing's**

* **Addison’s Disease (Hyposecretion):** Characterized by a lack of adrenal hormones. Symptoms include weight loss, hypoglycemia, hyponatremia (low sodium), hyperkalemia (high potassium), and dehydration due to decreased aldosterone.
* **Cushing’s Syndrome (Hypersecretion or Long-term Steroid Use):** Characterized by "moon face," "buffalo hump," obesity in the trunk area (belly fat), facial erythema, and muscle weakness. It often results in hyperglycemia and hypokalemia.

**3. Contraindications and Critical Precautions**

* **Infections:** Because corticosteroids suppress the immune system, they are contraindicated in patients with septicemia, systemic fungal infections (like Candidiasis), and varicella (chickenpox).
* **Diabetes Mellitus:** Corticosteroids elevate blood glucose levels, making them dangerous for patients with uncontrolled diabetes.
* **Glaucoma:** These drugs can increase intraocular pressure, worsening glaucoma.
* **Heart Failure:** Due to sodium and water retention, corticosteroids can cause fluid volume overload, necessitating frequent monitoring of breath sounds for respiratory congestion.

**4. Adverse Effects and Management**

* **Gastrointestinal:** Corticosteroids are ulcerogenic. They should be taken with food or milk to minimize GI upset. In high-risk cases, proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) may be prescribed to prevent gastric erosion.
* **Musculoskeletal:** Long-term use can lead to osteoporosis, loss of muscle bulk, and growth suppression in children (requiring baseline height and weight monitoring).
* **Dermatological:** Patients may experience skin thinning, easy bruising, and delayed wound healing.
* **Psychological:** Potential for insomnia, personality changes, and impaired stress responses.

### Nuances to be Mastered

**1. The "Adrenal Crisis" and Tapering**
One of the most critical nuances is the danger of abrupt withdrawal. Stopping glucocorticoids suddenly can cause the adrenal glands to fail to produce endogenous cortisol, leading to an adrenal crisis (characterized by hypotension, fatigue, and nausea). Medications must always be tapered slowly.

**2. Circadian Rhythm and Administration Timing**
To minimize adrenal suppression and mimic the body's natural release of cortisol, oral glucocorticoids should be administered in the early morning. Taking them at night can interfere with the natural circadian rhythm and lead to further adrenal suppression.

**3. Specific Drug Nuances**

* **Betamethasone:** This is the drug of choice in premature labor to accelerate fetal lung maturation.
* **Inhaled Glucocorticoids (e.g., Beclomethasone):** Patients must rinse their mouths with warm water after use to prevent local fungal infections (oral thrush) because the drug suppresses local immunity.
* **Intraarticular Injections:** After a steroid injection into a joint, the area should be rested for 48 hours, and cold packs can be used for the first 24 hours to minimize pain.

**4. Complex Drug-Drug Interactions**

* **Loop Diuretics:** When combined with corticosteroids, there is a risk of severe potassium depletion (hypokalemia) and hypocalcemia.
* **Oral Contraceptives:** These can increase the half-life of adrenal drugs, meaning they stay in the system longer and increase the risk of side effects like osteoporosis and insomnia.
* **Anticholinesterases:** In patients with Myasthenia Gravis, simultaneous use with corticosteroids can cause significant muscle weakness.
* **NSAIDs/Aspirin:** Using these with steroids increases the risk of gastric ulcers and GI bleeding.

**5. Monitoring and Assessment**

* In patients with heart failure, the priority assessment is **breath sounds** (to check for fluid in the lungs) rather than peripheral pulses or capillary refill.
* In children, the priority is tracking **height and weight** to monitor for growth suppression.
* Laboratory monitoring must prioritize **blood glucose** and **potassium levels**, as these are most frequently altered by steroid therapy.
