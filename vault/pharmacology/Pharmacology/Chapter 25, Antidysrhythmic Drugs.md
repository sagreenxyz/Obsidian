# Chapter 25, Antidysrhythmic Drugs

To master the topic of antidysrhythmic drugs, a student must understand the specific classifications, the unique mechanisms of action for various agents, critical nursing assessments, and the complex nuances of drug interactions and patient safety.

### Main Points for Mastery

**1. Classification of Antidysrhythmics**

* **Class I (Sodium Channel Blockers):**
* **Class Ia (e.g., Quinidine, Procainamide):** Delay repolarization and increase the duration of the action potential.
* **Class Ib (e.g., Lidocaine):** Accelerate repolarization and decrease the duration of the action potential. Specifically used for ventricular dysrhythmias.
* **Class Ic (e.g., Flecainide, Propafenone):** Have a pronounced effect on blocking sodium channels but little effect on repolarization or action potential duration.


* **Class II (Beta-Blockers, e.g., Atenolol, Esmolol, Metoprolol):** Reduce activity in the SA node, slow conduction through the AV node, and reduce myocardial contractility.
* **Class III (Potassium Channel Blockers, e.g., Amiodarone, Sotalol, Ibutilide, Dofetilide):** Prolong repolarization in phase 3, increasing the action potential duration. Often used for difficult-to-treat dysrhythmias like life-threatening ventricular tachycardia or resistant atrial fibrillation.
* **Class IV (Calcium Channel Blockers, e.g., Verapamil, Diltiazem):** Inhibit calcium channels, slowing conduction through the AV node. Primarily used for supraventricular tachycardias (PSVT) and rate control in atrial fibrillation/flutter.
* **Unclassified (e.g., Adenosine):** Slows electrical conduction through the AV node. Used specifically to convert PSVT to sinus rhythm.

**2. Critical Nursing Assessments and Monitoring**

* **Vital Signs:** Always check pulse rate and blood pressure before administration. Withhold medication and notify the prescriber if the pulse is <60 or >100 bpm, or if systolic blood pressure is <90 mm Hg.
* **Cardiac Monitoring:** Assess for ECG changes such as prolonged QT intervals (Quinidine, Amiodarone), prolonged PR intervals, or AV blocks.
* **Organ Function:** * **Pulmonary:** Monitor respiratory rate and breath sounds (especially for Amiodarone and beta-blockers).
* **Hepatic:** Monitor bilirubin and liver enzymes for Amiodarone.
* **Thyroid:** Monitor thyroid function tests for patients on Amiodarone due to its iodine content.
* **Renal/Hematologic:** Monitor CBC for patients on Procainamide to detect agranulocytosis.



**3. Drug-Specific Adverse Effects**

* **Quinidine:** Cinchonism (tinnitus, hearing loss, blurred vision, GI upset).
* **Procainamide:** Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)–like syndrome.
* **Amiodarone:** Pulmonary toxicity (hacking cough, dyspnea), visual halos, photophobia, dry eyes, and bluish skin discoloration.
* **Lidocaine:** Central nervous system toxic effects (twitching, seizures, confusion) and bradycardia.
* **Beta-Blockers:** Bronchospasm, wheezing, and masking of hypoglycemia or fatigue.

**4. Emergency Management**

* **Overdose:** There are no specific antidotes for most antidysrhythmics. Management is supportive, focusing on maintaining ABCs (airway, breathing, circulation). In the event of a pulseless overdose, the priority is initiating CPR and defibrillation.
* **Adenosine Administration:** Due to a half-life of less than 10 seconds, it must be given as a very rapid IV push (undiluted) followed immediately by a saline flush through a large-bore IV catheter.

### Nuances for Advanced Understanding

**1. Critical Drug-Drug Interactions**

* **Amiodarone with Digoxin/Warfarin:** When starting Amiodarone, the dose of Digoxin or Warfarin must typically be reduced by 50% because Amiodarone significantly increases the serum levels of these drugs.
* **Antacids:** Antacids can interfere with the absorption of oral antidysrhythmics. They should be taken 2 hours before or 2 hours after the heart medication.

**2. Administration Nuances**

* **Salt Forms:** Different salt forms of drugs (especially Quinidine) are not interchangeable.
* **IV Delivery:** Antidysrhythmic infusions should always be administered via an infusion pump to ensure precise dosing and prevent life-threatening toxicity.
* **Beta-Blocker Post-MI:** Metoprolol is specifically given after a myocardial infarction not just for blood pressure, but to reduce the risk of sudden cardiac death.

**3. Diet and Lifestyle Factors**

* **Alkaline Ash Foods:** Patients should avoid excessive consumption of alkaline ash foods (e.g., citrus fruits, vegetables, milk) as they can affect drug excretion. A balanced diet, such as a turkey and cheese sandwich, is preferred over high-fat or high-alkaline meals.
* **Caffeine:** Patients must avoid caffeine (chocolate, tea, soda, energy drinks) as it can exacerbate dysrhythmias.
* **Photosensitivity:** Amiodarone requires patients to use sunscreen and wear protective clothing/sunglasses to prevent solar dermatitis and eye damage.

**4. Evaluation of Therapeutic Effectiveness**

* Mastery requires looking beyond just the heart rate. Effectiveness is evidenced by improved cardiac output, which manifests as:
* Increased urinary output (e.g., 400 mL in an 8-hour shift).
* Improved skin color and warmth.
* Decreased fatigue and increased activity tolerance.
* Conversion to a regular heart rhythm.



**5. Distinguishing "Normal" from "Critical" Symptoms**

* **Fatigue:** While some patients assume fatigue is normal with aging or exercise, "excess fatigue" in a patient on beta-blockers or amiodarone must be reported immediately as it may indicate worsening heart failure or bradycardia.
* **Cough:** A hacking cough in an Amiodarone patient is never "just a cold"; it is a potential sign of life-threatening pulmonary toxicity.
