# Chapter 9, Photo Atlas of Drug Administration

### **Main Points and Educational Foundations**

**1. General Medication Safety and Standards**

* **Patient Identification:** Always use at least two patient identifiers (e.g., name and date of birth) before administration.
* **Infection Control:** Hand hygiene must be performed after touching a patient or performing assessments. Gloves should be worn whenever contact with mucous membranes or body fluids is possible (e.g., injections, suppositories, buccal administration), but are not mandatory for all oral medications.
* **Documentation:** Medication should only be documented after the patient has actually taken it.
* **Refusal of Medication:** Patients have the right to refuse. If a medication is already opened/prepared and then refused, it must be disposed of according to facility protocol and never returned to the original container.
* **Pre-administration Assessment:** Specific medications require targeted assessments, such as checking the apical pulse for a full 60 seconds before administering Digoxin to monitor for bradycardia.

**2. Oral and Enteral Administration**

* **Liquid Medications:** Always measure at eye level on a flat surface. For volumes less than 5 mL, use a calibrated oral syringe rather than a medicine cup or teaspoon for accuracy.
* **Oral Mucosa Routes:** * **Sublingual:** Placed under the tongue.
* **Buccal:** Placed between the cheek and the lower/upper molar teeth.
* *Note:* Patients must not swallow or drink water until these are completely dissolved.


* **Tube Administration (NG/Gastrostomy):** Keep the head of the bed elevated (Fowler’s position) during and for at least 30 minutes after to prevent aspiration. Verify tube placement before use. Flush the tube with 10 mL of water between each drug.
* **Pediatric Considerations:** Never mix medications with essential foods like milk or orange juice, as the child may develop an aversion to them. Do not use honey to sweeten medications for infants due to botulism risk. Use a plastic oral syringe and administer while the child is not crying to avoid aspiration.

**3. Parenteral (Injectable) Administration**

* **Intramuscular (IM):** * **Angle:** 90 degrees.
* **Sites:** The ventrogluteal site is the preferred and safest site for adults. The vastus lateralis is preferred for pediatric patients. The deltoid is used for small volumes but carries a risk of nerve injury. The dorsogluteal site is no longer recommended due to proximity to the sciatic nerve.
* **Volume:** Limit to 1 mL for children; adults can generally receive up to 3 mL.


* **Subcutaneous (SC):** * **Angle:** 45 degrees for thin patients or 90 degrees for those with average fatty tissue.
* **Medications:** Insulin and Heparin are common. Do not aspirate when administering these.


* **Intradermal (ID):** * **Angle:** 5 to 15 degrees (nearly parallel to the skin) with the bevel up.
* **Goal:** Create a small bleb (mosquito-bite appearance). Commonly used for TB skin tests in the forearm. Max volume is 0.1 mL.


* **Intravenous (IV):** * **Speed:** Some drugs like Adenosine must be pushed very rapidly (2-3 seconds).
* **Compatibility:** Phenytoin must be diluted with Normal Saline, never Dextrose or Lactated Ringer's.
* **Piggyback (IVPB):** When using a vial-to-bag system, snap the connector, squeeze fluid into the vial, rotate to dissolve, then ensure all medication returns to the bag.



**4. Topical and Inhalation Administration**

* **Ophthalmic (Eye):** Drop medication into the conjunctival sac, not directly on the cornea. Apply gentle pressure to the nasolacrimal duct for 30–60 seconds afterward to prevent systemic absorption and unpleasant tastes. Clean eyes from the inner to the outer canthus.
* **Otic (Ear):** Warm drops to room temperature before administration to prevent pain, nausea, and dizziness.
* **Nasal:** Position the head based on the target (e.g., tilted back for the posterior pharynx; tilted back and over the edge of the bed for ethmoid/sphenoid sinuses).
* **Inhalation:** When both a bronchodilator and a corticosteroid are prescribed, administer the bronchodilator first to open the airways. Patients must rinse their mouths after corticosteroid use to prevent fungal infections.

### **Nuances for Mastery**

* **The Z-Track Method:** This is a specific IM technique used for irritating or staining medications (like iron dextran or hydroxyzine). Mastery requires:
1. Pulling the skin laterally.
2. Aspirating for 5–10 seconds.
3. Injecting slowly.
4. Waiting exactly 10 seconds before withdrawing the needle to allow the drug to disperse.
5. Releasing the skin immediately to seal the track. Never massage the site.


* **Air Lock Technique:** When preparing an IM injection, withdrawing 0.2 mL of air into the syringe creates an "air lock" that follows the medication and prevents it from leaking into subcutaneous tissue.
* **Syringe Specifics:** In a 100-unit insulin syringe, each line represents 2 units. In a 50-unit syringe, each line represents 1 unit. Never use a tuberculin or hypodermic syringe for insulin.
* **Medication Integrity:** Enteric-coated (EC), sustained-release (SR), and extended-release (ER) medications must never be crushed or chewed, as this destroys their absorption profile and can lead to toxicity. If a patient with dysphagia is prescribed these, the nurse must withhold the dose and request a different formulation.
* **Ampule Handling:** Always use a filter needle to withdraw medication to prevent glass shards from being drawn into the syringe, but replace it with a regular needle before injecting the patient. Tap the ampule so fluid leaves the neck, and snap the neck quickly away from the body using gauze.
* **Vial Management:** Once a multidose vial is opened, it must be labeled with the date, time, and the discard date.
* **IV Backpriming Contraindications:** Do not backprime the primary IV line if it contains medications that are incompatible with others, such as Heparin, Aminophylline, vasopressors, or multivitamins.
* **Nebulizer Maintenance:** At home, nebulizer parts should be soaked in a solution of one part white vinegar to four parts water for 30 minutes once a week to prevent microbial growth.
