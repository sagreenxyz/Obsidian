# Chapter 40, Antiviral Drugs

### Main Points for Students

* **Drug-Specific Indications:**
* **Acyclovir:** The drug of choice for Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV-1 and HSV-2), Varicella-Zoster Virus (shingles), and chickenpox. Intravenous administration is used for severe cases.
* **Ganciclovir/Valganciclovir:** Primarily used for Cytomegalovirus (CMV), particularly CMV retinitis. Valganciclovir is a prodrug of ganciclovir with better oral bioavailability.
* **Oseltamivir:** Used for Influenza A and B. It must be started within 48 hours of symptom onset to be effective.
* **Ribavirin:** Used for Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV) in infants (inhalation) and Hepatitis C (oral, often in combination).
* **Amantadine:** Historically used for Influenza A, though no longer recommended for this by the CDC; it is still used as a dopamine modulator.


* **Dose-Limiting and Severe Toxicities:**
* **Bone Marrow Suppression:** A critical adverse effect for Ganciclovir and Zidovudine (an HIV medication). Regular Complete Blood Counts (CBC) are mandatory.
* **Nephrotoxicity:** A major concern for Foscarnet and Cidofovir. Nurses must monitor BUN and creatinine levels closely.
* **Teratogenicity:** Ribavirin is strictly contraindicated in pregnant women and their male sexual partners due to severe birth defect risks.


* **HIV/AIDS Therapy (HAART):**
* The goal is to reduce the "viral load" (copies of viral RNA in the blood).
* Prophylactic treatment for opportunistic infections typically begins when the CD4 count drops below 200 cells/mm³.
* **Fusion Inhibitors:** Enfuvirtide is the primary example and is the first of its class.
* **Protease Inhibitors (e.g., Indinavir):** Can cause metabolic issues, including lipodystrophy (fat redistribution/hump) and exacerbation of diabetes/hyperglycemia.



### Nuances to be Mastered

* **Administration Requirements:**
* **Indinavir:** Must be taken on an empty stomach to ensure proper absorption; high-fat or high-protein foods impair its effectiveness. Patients should also drink at least 48 oz of fluid daily to prevent nephrolithiasis (kidney stones).
* **Zidovudine:** Patients should remain upright for 30 minutes after taking the drug to prevent esophageal ulceration.
* **Topical Acyclovir:** To prevent autoinoculation or transmission, patients must use a finger cot or gloved hand, apply to each lesion individually, and never touch the medication container after touching a lesion.


* **Drug-Drug Interactions:**
* **Zidovudine + Acyclovir:** Increases the risk of neurotoxicity (seizures or tremors).
* **Zidovudine + Ganciclovir:** Greatly increases the risk of hematologic (blood) toxicity.
* **Cidofovir + Cyclosporine:** Synergistic nephrotoxicity; this combination is contraindicated.
* **Sofosbuvir + Amiodarone:** A dangerous interaction used in Hepatitis C treatment that must be avoided.


* **Assessment Timing and Cues:**
* **Symptom Onset:** For influenza antivirals, the nurse's first question must always be "When did your symptoms begin?" because of the 48-hour efficacy window.
* **Muscle Pain:** In patients taking Tenofovir, new muscle pain or weakness can indicate life-threatening lactic acidosis and requires holding the dose immediately.
* **Creatinine Clearance:** For drugs like Remdesivir, the dose may need to be held if the creatinine clearance falls below a specific threshold (e.g., 30 mL/min).


* **Patient Education Realities:**
* Students must understand that antivirals do not cure viral infections; they only manage symptoms and viral replication.
* Patients with HIV must be taught that they can still transmit the virus even if their viral load is low, and they must continue using protection (condoms).
* Early adherence is critical because viruses are usually well-established in the host before clinical symptoms even appear.
