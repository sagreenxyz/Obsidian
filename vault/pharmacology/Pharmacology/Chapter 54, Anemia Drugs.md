# Chapter 54, Anemia Drugs

To achieve proficiency in the pharmacology of anemia drugs, a student must master both the broad therapeutic principles and the specific clinical nuances that govern safe administration and patient education.

### Main Points for Mastery

**1. Erythropoiesis-Stimulating Agents (ESAs): Epoetin Alfa and Darbepoetin**

* **Mechanism and Indications:** These are biosynthetic forms of the hormone erythropoietin. They are used to treat anemia associated with end-stage renal disease, chemotherapy, and HIV treatment (zidovudine).
* **Key Contraindication:** The most critical contraindication is **uncontrolled hypertension**. ESAs can further increase blood pressure, leading to severe cardiovascular events.
* **Thresholds for Administration:** Therapy must be closely monitored via hemoglobin levels. For patients with renal failure, the drug should be held if hemoglobin exceeds 11 g/dL. For cancer patients, the limit is typically 10 g/dL. Exceeding these levels significantly increases the risk of myocardial infarction, stroke, and death.
* **Supportive Therapy:** ESAs are often ineffective without adequate iron stores. They are frequently prescribed alongside iron supplements like ferrous fumarate.

**2. Iron Preparations: Oral and Parenteral**

* **Oral Iron (Ferrous Fumarate, Ferrous Sulfate):** Ferrous fumarate contains the highest concentration of elemental iron (33%).
* **Parenteral Iron (Iron Dextran, Iron Sucrose, Ferric Gluconate, Ferumoxytol):**
* **Iron Dextran:** Carries a high risk of anaphylaxis and **requires a test dose** before the full infusion.
* **Iron Sucrose:** A common side effect is hypotension, particularly in elderly or underweight patients.
* **Ferumoxytol:** Notable because it can be given as an undiluted IV push over one minute and does not require a test dose.


* **Toxicity and Overload:** Iron toxicity can lead to coma, shock, or seizures. **Deferoxamine** is used as a chelating agent to treat severe iron overdose. Iron supplements are contraindicated in patients with hemochromatosis or hemolytic anemia.

**3. Folic Acid (Vitamin B9) and Cyanocobalamin (Vitamin B12)**

* **Folic Acid:** Essential for DNA synthesis and preventing neural tube defects in pregnancy. It is used to treat megaloblastic anemia.
* **Cyanocobalamin (B12):** Used to treat pernicious anemia, which is often caused by a lack of intrinsic factor (common after gastrectomy).

### Nuances for Clinical Mastery

**1. Absorption Logistics and Diet**

* **Enhancers:** Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) significantly increases iron absorption. Patients should be encouraged to take iron with orange juice.
* **Inhibitors:** Calcium, dairy products, antacids, and sodas decrease iron absorption. These should be avoided for at least two hours before and after taking iron.
* **The Food Dilemma:** While taking iron with food reduces gastrointestinal upset (a common side effect), it also decreases total absorption. Clinical judgment is needed to balance tolerance with efficacy.

**2. Administration Specifics**

* **Onset of Action:** Subcutaneous epoetin alfa has a slower onset of action compared to intravenous administration.
* **Positioning:** Patients should remain upright for at least 30 minutes after taking oral iron to prevent esophageal irritation.
* **Technique:** Liquid oral iron should be diluted and taken through a straw to prevent staining of the tooth enamel. For intramuscular iron, the Z-track method must be used.
* **IV Precautions:** Before administering IV iron, the line should be flushed with normal saline, and resuscitative equipment (including epinephrine for anaphylaxis) should be nearby.

**3. Laboratory and Diagnostic Nuances**

* **Anemia Morphology:** * **Iron-deficiency anemia** is microcytic (small cells) and hypochromic (pale cells) due to cytoplasmic maturation defects.
* **Megaloblastic/Pernicious anemia** is macrocytic (large cells) and normochromic (normal color) due to nuclear maturation defects.


* **Masking Effect:** Folic acid can improve the blood count in a patient with Vitamin B12 deficiency but will not stop the associated neurological damage. Therefore, the specific cause of megaloblastic anemia must be identified before starting therapy.

**4. Side Effects vs. Toxicities**

* **Expected Side Effects:** Black, tarry stools and constipation are normal, expected findings with oral iron and do not indicate toxicity.
* **Signs of ESA Abuse:** Athletes may abuse epoetin alfa to increase oxygen capacity, which increases blood viscosity and the risk of stroke.
* **Drug Interactions:** Iron supplements can significantly decrease the absorption of certain antibiotics, specifically tetracyclines.
