# Chapter 22, Antihypertensive Drugs

To master the pharmacology of antihypertensive drugs, a student must understand the distinct mechanisms, side effects, and nursing implications for several major drug classes.

### Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme (ACE) Inhibitors

* **Mechanism:** These drugs (typically ending in **"-pril"**) block the conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II, a potent vasoconstrictor. They also prevent the breakdown of bradykinin.
* **Clinical Nuances:**
* **The "ACE Cough":** A dry, nonproductive, persistent cough is a common side effect due to bradykinin accumulation. If intolerable, patients are often switched to an ARB.
* **Angioedema:** This is a rare but life-threatening emergency involving swelling of the face and airway. It requires immediate medical attention.
* **Liver Dysfunction:** Most ACE inhibitors are "prodrugs" that must be metabolized by the liver. **Captopril and Lisinopril** are exceptions; they are not prodrugs and are preferred for patients with liver failure.
* **Potassium Levels:** These drugs can cause hyperkalemia. They should be used cautiously with potassium-sparing diuretics (like spironolactone) or potassium supplements.
* **Drug Interactions:** They can cause lithium toxicity and increase the risk of renal failure when taken with NSAIDs (like ibuprofen).
* **Heart Failure:** ACE inhibitors are the choice for hypertensive patients with heart failure because they reduce afterload. Effectiveness is often monitored by assessing for clearer breath sounds.



### Angiotensin II Receptor Blockers (ARBs)

* **Mechanism:** These drugs (ending in **"-sartan"**) block the receptor sites for angiotensin II.
* **Clinical Nuances:**
* **Cough:** They do not cause the dry cough associated with ACE inhibitors.
* **Renal Health:** While they protect the kidneys in some patients, they can cause renal failure if taken with NSAIDs. Monitoring creatinine levels is essential for patients with pre-existing renal dysfunction.



### Beta Blockers

* **Mechanism:** These drugs (ending in **"-lol"**) decrease heart rate, contractility, and conduction velocity.
* **Clinical Nuances:**
* **Respiratory Risk:** Nonselective beta blockers (like propranolol) can cause bronchospasms. A respiratory assessment is a priority, and they are generally contraindicated in patients with **asthma**.
* **Diabetes:** Beta blockers can mask the symptoms of hypoglycemia (tachycardia), so diabetic patients must monitor blood glucose closely.
* **Compliance:** They must never be stopped abruptly, as this can cause dangerous **rebound hypertension**.
* **Patient Education:** Patients should avoid antacids (which delay absorption) and hot tubs/saunas (which can cause excessive hypotension).



### Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)

* **Mechanism:** CCBs (such as **Amlodipine**) cause vasodilation by blocking calcium influx into smooth muscle cells.
* **Clinical Nuances:**
* **Administration:** Always check blood pressure before giving. If the systolic BP is below 100 mmHg, the dose should typically be held.



### Adrenergic Drugs (Alpha Blockers and Agonists)

* **Alpha 1 Blockers (e.g., Doxazosin, Prazosin):**
* **First-Dose Syncope:** The first dose can cause a sudden drop in blood pressure. It should be taken at bedtime, and the patient should remain supine.
* **Extended-Release Nuance:** With extended-release Doxazosin, the matrix of the capsule may appear in the stool. This is normal and does not mean the drug wasn't absorbed.
* **BPH:** These drugs are uniquely beneficial for men with benign prostatic hyperplasia as they improve urinary flow.


* **Alpha 2 Agonists (e.g., Clonidine, Methyldopa):**
* **Clonidine:** Common side effects include dry mouth, constipation, and sedation. It carries a high risk for orthostatic hypotension.
* **Methyldopa:** This is a primary choice for treating hypertension during pregnancy.



### Vasodilators

* **Hydralazine:** Adverse effects include tachycardia, edema, headache, and dizziness.
* **Sodium Nitroprusside:** Used in intensive care for hypertensive emergencies.
* **Monitoring:** Requires continuous blood pressure monitoring and must be administered via an infusion pump.
* **Toxicity:** If used for more than 4 days, there is a risk of **cyanide/thiocyanate toxicity**. If toxicity or excessive hypotension occurs, the infusion must be stopped immediately.


* **Minoxidil:** Toxicity or overdose is treated primarily with intravenous fluids to counteract excessive hypotension.

### Diuretics

* **First-line Therapy:** Thiazide diuretics (like hydrochlorothiazide) are considered the first-line treatment for hypertension.
* **Potassium Management:** Thiazides are potassium-wasting. Patients should be encouraged to eat potassium-rich foods and take the medication in the morning to avoid sleep disruption from urination (nocturia).

### Essential Patient Education and Safety

* **Orthostatic Hypotension:** This is a risk for almost all antihypertensives. Patients must be taught to change positions slowly and sit on the edge of the bed before standing.
* **Lifestyle & Compliance:** Hypertension is often asymptomatic (the "silent killer"). Patients must take medication routinely even if they feel fine. Alcohol and other CNS depressants should be avoided as they increase the risk of hypotension and sedation.
* **Risk Factors:** Students should remember that untreated hypertension is a direct risk factor for stroke, heart failure, and cardiovascular disease.
