# Chapter 42, Antifungal Drugs

To master the pharmacology of antifungal drugs, a student must understand the biological differences between fungal types, the specific indications for various drug classes, and the complex nursing considerations required for safe administration.

### Main Points for Mastery

#### 1. Fungal Classification and Pathogenesis

* **Yeasts vs. Molds:** Yeasts are single-celled organisms that reproduce by budding. Molds are multicellular and characterized by long, branching, filamentous structures.
* **Infection Types:**
* **Dermatomycoses:** Fungal infections of the skin, hair, or nails (e.g., athlete’s foot, onychomycosis). These can range from mild to fatal if they become systemic.
* **Systemic Mycoses:** Severe infections involving internal organs. These are most common in immunocompromised patients, such as those with AIDS, cancer patients undergoing chemotherapy, or organ transplant recipients on immunosuppressants.
* **Candidiasis:** Caused by *Candida albicans*. Oral candidiasis (thrush) is common in newborns and immunocompromised patients. Vaginal candidiasis (yeast infection) is frequently seen in pregnant women, patients using oral contraceptives, or those with diabetes (hyperglycemic conditions).



#### 2. Major Antifungal Drug Classes

* **Polyenes (Amphotericin B, Nystatin):**
* **Amphotericin B:** The "gold standard" for severe systemic mycoses. It works by binding to sterols in fungal cell membranes, causing cell death.
* **Nystatin:** Primarily used for oral or vaginal candidiasis; it is not available for intravenous administration.


* **Azoles (Fluconazole, Voriconazole, Itraconazole, Ketoconazole):** These inhibit fungal cytochrome P-450 enzymes, preventing the production of ergosterol.
* **Fluconazole:** Excellent for vaginal candidiasis (single oral dose) and cryptococcal meningitis due to its ability to pass into the cerebrospinal fluid.
* **Voriconazole:** Effective against tenacious fungi like *Fusarium*.


* **Echinocandins (Caspofungin, Micafungin):** These prevent the synthesis of glucans, essential components of fungal cell walls. Caspofungin is specifically used for invasive aspergillosis in patients who do not tolerate other drugs.
* **Antimetabolites (Flucytosine):** Often used in combination with Amphotericin B for synergistic effects against cryptococcal infections.
* **Allylamines (Terbinafine):** Used primarily for nail infections (onychomycosis).
* **Mitotic Inhibitors (Griseofulvin):** Inhibits fungal mitosis by binding to microtubules.

#### 3. Nursing Interventions and Monitoring

* **Renal Function:** This is the highest priority when administering Amphotericin B. Nurses must monitor blood urea nitrogen (BUN), creatinine, and urinary output.
* **Electrolyte Balance:** Antifungals (especially Amphotericin B) can cause significant potassium loss (hypokalemia) and low magnesium (hypomagnesemia).
* **Hepatic Monitoring:** Azoles and echinocandins are often hepatotoxic; liver enzyme levels must be monitored throughout therapy.
* **Infusion Safety:** Amphotericin B requires long infusion times (2 to 6 hours) and the use of an inline filter to prevent complications from precipitates.

### Nuances to be Mastered

#### 1. Medication-Specific Side Effects

* **Voriconazole:** Students must know that this drug can cause unique central nervous system effects, specifically hallucinations. It is also highly teratogenic (Category D), carrying a high risk of fetal malformation.
* **Amphotericin B:** Known for "infusion reactions," including fever, chills, hypotension, tachycardia, and muscle/joint pain. It can also cause peripheral neuropathy (numbness and tingling in extremities).
* **Terbinafine:** While effective for nails, it can cause alopecia (hair loss) at high doses.
* **Caspofungin:** Can cause blood profile alterations (anemia, leukopenia) and cardiovascular issues like tachycardia.

#### 2. Critical Drug Interactions

* **Fluconazole and Glipizide:** Fluconazole reduces the metabolism of oral hypoglycemic agents like glipizide. This can lead to dangerously low blood sugar (hypoglycemia).
* **Voriconazole and Quinidine:** Coadministration can lead to prolongation of the QT interval on an ECG, worsening cardiac status.
* **Amphotericin B and Digitalis/Thiazide Diuretics:** Due to the risk of severe hypokalemia, combined use increases the risk of digitalis toxicity.

#### 3. Contraindications and Synergies

* **Porphyria:** Griseofulvin is strictly contraindicated in patients with porphyria, as it can precipitate an acute worsening of the condition.
* **Synergy:** The combination of Flucytosine and Amphotericin B is a classic example of drug synergy, where the two drugs together eliminate cryptococcal brain infections more effectively than either drug alone.

#### 4. Administration Nuances

* **Nystatin Lozenges (Troches):** Patients must be instructed to let these dissolve slowly and completely in the mouth. They should never be chewed or swallowed whole, as local contact is necessary for effectiveness.
* **Amphotericin B Premedication:** To minimize infusion reactions, nurses should expect to administer a "cocktail" of premedications, including antiemetics, antihistamines, and corticosteroids.
* **Treatment Duration:** Fungal nail infections (onychomycosis) require long-term commitment; for example, oral terbinafine for toenails must be taken daily for 12 weeks.

#### 5. Reservoirs and Specific Pathogens

* **Cryptococcus neoformans:** This fungus, often found in bird droppings, is a primary cause of fungal meningitis.
* **Fusarium:** This fungus is notoriously resistant to most azoles (like fluconazole and itraconazole), making voriconazole the drug of choice.
* **Aspergillus:** Requires specific coverage; while amphotericin and itraconazole are effective, griseofulvin has no effect on this genus.
