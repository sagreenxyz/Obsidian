# Chapter 16, Psychotherapeutic Drugs

To master the pharmacology of psychotherapeutic drugs, a student must understand the distinct classifications, mechanisms of action, safety protocols, and the specific physiological nuances that differentiate these medications.

### 1. Anxiolytic Drugs

Anxiolytics are primarily used to treat anxiety disorders, but their specific profiles dictate their clinical application.

* **Benzodiazepines (e.g., Alprazolam, Diazepam, Lorazepam, Clonazepam):**
* **Mechanism:** They increase the activity of gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA) receptors to block nerve transmission in the CNS.
* **Main Points:** Used for anxiety, alcohol withdrawal, and seizure management. Adverse effects include somnolence, ataxia, confusion, and dizziness.
* **Nuances:** * **Interactions:** Their effects are intensified by other CNS depressants like alcohol, opioids, antihistamines, and muscle relaxants. Oral contraceptives can reduce their elimination, increasing serum levels.
* **Pregnancy:** These are lipid-soluble and cross the placenta; therapy should be tapered before conception.
* **Elderly Patients:** High risk for "rebound disinhibition," where sedation is followed by severe agitation and confusion upon withdrawal.
* **Overdose:** Flumazenil is the specific antidote, though it may trigger seizures in long-term users.
* **Administration:** Lorazepam must be diluted when given intramuscularly to reduce irritation.




* **Buspirone:**
* **Mechanism:** Promotes agonist activity at both serotonin and dopamine receptors.
* **Main Points:** It is non-sedating and non-habit-forming, making it ideal for patients with a history of substance abuse.
* **Nuance:** It must never be given with Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs) due to the risk of hypertension; a 14-day washout period is required after stopping an MAOI.



### 2. Antidepressant Therapy

Antidepressants are categorized into several "generations," each with specific safety profiles.

* **Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs) (e.g., Amitriptyline, Nortriptyline):**
* **Current Use:** Though original first-generation antidepressants, they are now frequently used for insomnia, neuropathic pain, and childhood enuresis (specifically Imipramine).
* **Safety/Overdose:** Overdoses are often lethal. Sodium bicarbonate is used to alkalize the urine and speed elimination, while multiple doses of activated charcoal reduce absorption.
* **Nuance:** Nortriptyline interacts with anticholinergics to cause severe constipation and urinary retention.


* **Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs) (e.g., Phenelzine, Isocarboxazid):**
* **Dietary Nuance:** The most critical point is the "Tyramine Effect." Patients must avoid aged cheeses, red wine, fava beans, and corned beef to prevent a life-threatening hypertensive crisis. Cottage cheese is a safe alternative.


* **Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs) and SNRIs (e.g., Fluoxetine, Sertraline, Citalopram, Duloxetine):**
* **Timeline:** Full therapeutic effects take 4 to 6 weeks.
* **Serotonin Syndrome:** A major risk characterized by delirium, myoclonus (muscle spasms), tachycardia, hyperreflexia, shivering, and hyperthermia. Tramadol and St. John's wort significantly increase this risk.
* **Specific Nuances:**
* **Duloxetine:** Requires baseline Liver Function Tests (LFTs) due to hepatotoxicity and interacts with NSAIDs to increase bleeding risk.
* **Administration:** Fiber supplements can interfere with SSRI absorption and should be taken 2 hours apart.





### 3. Mood Stabilizers

Primarily used for bipolar disorder (affective disorders) to manage cycles of mania and depression.

* **Lithium:**
* **Mechanism:** Regulates catecholamine metabolism.
* **The Sodium Connection:** Lithium and sodium are both monovalent ions. If sodium levels are low, the body retains lithium, leading to toxicity. Stable sodium intake is mandatory to maintain therapeutic levels.
* **Nuance:** A unique adverse effect is "choreoathetotic movements" (involuntary wavelike movements of the legs).



### 4. Antipsychotic Drugs

Used for schizophrenia and drug-induced psychoses.

* **Conventional/First-Generation (e.g., Phenothiazines, Haloperidol):**
* **Mechanism:** Primarily block dopamine receptors in the CNS.
* **Side Effects:** High risk of Extrapyramidal Symptoms (EPS), specifically Dystonia (abnormal muscle contractions).
* **Life-Threatening Risk:** Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS), characterized by high fever, unstable blood pressure, and myoglobinemia.


* **Atypical/Second-Generation (e.g., Clozapine, Risperidone, Ziprasidone, Quetiapine):**
* **Mechanism:** Block both D2 dopamine and serotonin receptors.
* **Clozapine Nuance:** Can cause fatal agranulocytosis. Patients must have weekly White Blood Cell (WBC) monitoring and report flulike symptoms (fever/sore throat) immediately.
* **Metabolic Syndrome:** Many atypical antipsychotics cause weight gain, abnormal cholesterol, and hyperglycemia.
* **Ziprasidone Nuance:** It is the preferred choice for patients with diabetes or obesity as it has the least effect on weight and glucose metabolism.



### 5. Essential Clinical Mastery Points

* **Hypotheses of Depression:** Students must distinguish between the **Biogenic Amine Hypothesis** (deficiency of catecholamines), the **Permissive Hypothesis** (serotonin as the predisposing factor), and the **Dysregulation Hypothesis** (failure of system regulation).
* **Safety Monitoring:** Always monitor postural blood pressure in patients on anxiolytics or antipsychotics to prevent falls.
* **Herbal Interactions:** Ginseng is contraindicated in patients with hypertension or cardiovascular disease. St. John's wort is a major trigger for serotonin syndrome when combined with SSRIs.
* **Schizophrenia Symptoms:** "Positive" symptoms include hallucinations, delusions, and conceptual disorganization. "Negative" symptoms include apathy, social withdrawal, and poverty of speech.
