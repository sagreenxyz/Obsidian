# Chapter 31, Thyroid and Antithyroid Drugs

To master the pharmacology of thyroid and antithyroid medications, a student must understand the physiological feedback loops, the distinct clinical presentations of thyroid dysfunction, and the specific nursing implications for drug administration and safety.

### 1. Understanding Thyroid Dysfunction

Students must distinguish between the different origins and presentations of thyroid disorders:

* **Hypothyroidism:** Characterized by a low metabolic rate. Symptoms include weight gain, cold intolerance, depression, fatigue, and dry, brittle hair/nails.
* **Cretinism:** Hyposecretion of thyroid hormone in children, leading to low metabolic rate, retarded growth, sexual development issues, and potential mental impairment.
* **Myxedema:** Severe hypothyroidism in adults, often characterized by yellowing of the skin and memory loss.
* **Classifications:** * **Primary:** Abnormality in the thyroid gland itself (most common).
* **Secondary:** Pituitary gland fails to release sufficient Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH).
* **Tertiary:** Hypothalamus fails to secrete thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH).




* **Hyperthyroidism:** Characterized by an increased metabolic rate. Symptoms include weight loss, diarrhea, flushing, heat intolerance, altered menstrual cycles, tremors, and palpitations.
* **Graves’ Disease:** A specific type of hyperthyroidism.
* **Thyroid Storm (Thyroid Crisis):** A life-threatening exacerbation of hyperthyroidism symptoms (tachycardia, hypertension, fever) that requires immediate intervention.



### 2. Thyroid Replacement Drugs

**Key Medication:** Levothyroxine (Synthetic T4) and Liotrix (T3 and T4 combination).

* **Pharmacokinetics:** Levothyroxine has a very long half-life (6 to 10 days). This means it takes time to reach a steady state, and toxic effects can persist for days after the drug is stopped. Onset of action is typically 3 to 5 days.
* **Administration Nuances:**
* Must be taken in the morning on an empty stomach (30 to 60 minutes before breakfast) to maximize absorption and prevent insomnia.
* Brand consistency is vital; switching manufacturers can destabilize hormone levels due to differences in bioavailability.
* Dosing is typically in micrograms (mcg), not milligrams (mg). A common and dangerous medication error is confusing these units.


* **Adverse Effects:** These mimic hyperthyroidism, including palpitations, chest pain, irritability, tremors, and insomnia.
* **Interactions:** * Estrogen supplements can decrease thyroid drug activity.
* Thyroid drugs can decrease serum digitalis levels, increasing the risk of arrhythmias.
* Food and antacids interfere with absorption.



### 3. Antithyroid Drugs

**Key Medications:** Propylthiouracil (PTU), Methimazole, and Radioactive Iodine.

* **Mechanism of Action:** These drugs inhibit the synthesis of new thyroid hormones but do not inactivate existing hormones already in circulation.
* **Administration Nuances:**
* Unlike thyroid replacements, antithyroid drugs should be taken with meals to decrease gastrointestinal upset.
* Therapeutic benefits (such as suppressed hunger or weight stabilization) may not be noticed for approximately 2 weeks.


* **Dietary Restrictions:** Patients must avoid foods high in iodine, such as seafood (shrimp, lobster), spinach, and iodized salt, as iodine can interfere with the drug's effectiveness.
* **Adverse Effects:** * **Agranulocytosis:** A serious reduction in white blood cell count (leukopenia). Monitoring baseline and serial WBC counts is a priority.
* **Liver/Heme Toxicity:** PTU can cause hepatotoxicity and thrombocytopenia (low platelet count).
* **Other:** Vertigo, loss of taste, and skin rashes.


* **Radioactive Iodine:** Used for thyroid cancer or ablation. It is strictly contraindicated in pregnancy due to the risk of fetal follicle destruction.

### 4. Special Populations and Clinical Nuances

* **Pregnancy:** * Hypothyroidism must be treated because untreated low thyroid levels can restrict fetal growth. Levothyroxine is the preferred treatment.
* Hyperthyroidism treatment is complex because antithyroid drugs can be teratogenic (e.g., methimazole is linked to fetal scalp abnormalities). In some cases, a thyroidectomy is preferred over medication for pregnant patients.


* **Elderly Patients:** Requirements for thyroid replacement are approximately 25% lower in patients over age 60. They are more sensitive to adverse effects (palpitations, chest pain) due to decreased renal and hepatic function.
* **Surgical Considerations:** When switching from oral to intravenous (IV) levothyroxine (e.g., when a patient is NPO for surgery), the IV dose is generally 50% to 75% of the oral dose to avoid toxicity.
* **Diagnostic Interference:** Thyroid replacement medications should be discontinued about 4 weeks before a radioactive iodine isotope study to ensure accurate diagnostic results.

### 5. Priority Nursing Assessments

* **Vital Signs:** Always check the heart rate before administering thyroid drugs; hold the dose and notify the provider if tachycardia is present.
* **Laboratory Monitoring:** TSH is the primary lab used to determine the effectiveness and dosage of thyroid replacement. For antithyroid drugs, monitor the leukocyte (WBC) count and platelets.
* **Patient Education:** Teach patients that thyroid therapy is usually lifelong and that they must not abruptly discontinue the medication or take over-the-counter drugs without consulting a provider.
