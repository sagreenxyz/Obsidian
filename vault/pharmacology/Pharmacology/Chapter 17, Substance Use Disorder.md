# Chapter 17, Substance Use Disorder

To master the topic of Substance Use Disorder as presented in this chapter, a student must understand the pharmacological classifications, clinical manifestations of withdrawal and toxicity, and specific nursing interventions for various substances.

### Core Concepts and Definitions

* **Tolerance:** The state where a particular drug dose elicits a smaller response than it did initially, requiring increased doses to achieve the desired effect.
* **Addiction:** Psychological or physical dependence on a drug or psychoactive substance.
* **Physical Dependence:** A condition characterized by physiologic reliance on a substance, indicated by tolerance and the development of withdrawal symptoms upon termination.
* **Habituation:** The development of tolerance to a substance after prolonged medical use without psychological or physical dependence.

### Opioids

* **Therapeutic Uses:** Relieving pain, reducing cough, relieving diarrhea, and inducing anesthesia.
* **Signs vs. Symptoms of Withdrawal:** A critical nuance is distinguishing objective signs from subjective symptoms.
* **Symptoms (Subjective):** Arthralgia (joint pain), muscle cramps, anxiety, nausea, malaise, and intense drug cravings.
* **Signs (Objective):** Mydriasis (dilated pupils), diaphoresis (sweating), rhinorrhea (runny nose), lacrimation (tearing), piloerection (goosebumps), vomiting, diarrhea, insomnia, and elevated blood pressure and pulse.


* **Withdrawal Timeline:** Symptoms typically peak within 1 to 3 days and can last 5 to 7 days.
* **Key Medications:**
* **Naltrexone:** An opioid antagonist used to treat abuse by blocking receptors so use does not produce euphoria. **Nuance:** The patient must be opioid-free for at least one week before administration to avoid precipitated withdrawal.
* **Clonidine:** Used to reduce withdrawal symptoms like nausea. **Nuance:** It is strictly contraindicated in patients with hypotension, as it further lowers blood pressure.
* **Methadone:** An opioid agonist used for detoxification and long-term management to decrease withdrawal symptoms.
* **Flumazenil:** **Note:** This is *not* for opioids; it is for benzodiazepine reversal. Using the wrong antagonist is a common pitfall.



### Alcohol (Ethanol)

* **Prevalence:** Alcohol is the substance most abused by the adolescent population.
* **Nutritional Complications:** Chronic abuse leads to B-vitamin deficiencies (especially Thiamine/B1). This can result in **Wernicke’s encephalopathy**, Korsakoff psychosis, and polyneuritis.
* **Safety Nuances:**
* **Acetaminophen:** Patients must avoid acetaminophen if alcohol is present in their system due to a high risk of lethal liver damage.
* **Hypothermia:** Alcohol causes vasodilation, increasing the risk of rapid heat loss and hypothermia, particularly in cold environments.


* **Withdrawal Severity:**
* **Mild/Moderate:** Increased blood pressure, pulse, and temperature (around 100°F).
* **Severe (Delirium Tremens):** Temperature >101°F, pulse >140 beats/min, systolic BP >200 mm Hg, and diastolic BP >140 mm Hg.


* **Withdrawal Treatment:** Supplements include Thiamine and Magnesium. Severe cases require intravenous benzodiazepines (e.g., Diazepam, Alprazolam).
* **Aversion and Maintenance Therapy:**
* **Disulfiram:** Causes an unpleasant reaction (nausea, vomiting, flushing, throbbing headache, hypotension) if any alcohol (as little as 7 mL) is consumed. **Nuance:** Metronidazole therapy can cause a similar disulfiram-like reaction.
* **Acamprosate:** Used specifically to maintain abstinence.



### Stimulants (Amphetamines, Cocaine, Nicotine)

* **Therapeutic Uses:** Treatment of ADHD and narcolepsy. They may be used for obesity, but rapid tolerance limits this use.
* **Withdrawal Symptoms:** Peak in 1 to 3 days. Includes hypersomnia (excessive sleep), hyperphagia (excessive hunger), paranoid delusions, and suicidal thoughts.
* **Toxicity:** Overdose results in anxiety, delirium, hallucinations, and cardiac dysrhythmias. Immediate treatment often involves sedation.
* **Methamphetamine Nuance:** Crystallized methamphetamine is smoked and more powerful than the powdered form, which is swallowed or snorted.

### Nicotine

* **Withdrawal Manifestations:** Cravings, irritability, restlessness, and *decreased* heart rate and blood pressure.
* **Smoking Cessation Pharmacotherapy:**
* **Varenicline (Chantix):** A partial nicotine agonist. Common side effects include nausea and vomiting.
* **Bupropion:** The first nicotine-free prescription medicine for dependence; it is a noncompetitive nicotine antagonist.
* **Nicotine Replacement:** Transdermal patches start at a 21 mg/24 hr dose for the first step (4–8 weeks). Nicotine gum is best for immediate cravings because it releases the dose rapidly.



### Depressants (Benzodiazepines and Barbiturates)

* **Flunitrazepam ("Roofies"):** A benzodiazepine that creates a sleepy, relaxed, drunken feeling; it is banned in the United States.
* **Overdose:** Benzodiazepines are typically only lethal when combined with alcohol or barbiturates, leading to respiratory arrest.
* **Withdrawal Management:** Characterized by tremors (eyelids, tongue, hands), agitation, and involuntary muscle contractions. Treatment involves a tapered regimen of a benzodiazepine like Diazepam.

### Cannabis (Marijuana)

* **Acute Effects:** Mild euphoria, hallucinations, memory lapses, increased appetite, and motor awkwardness.
* **Perceptual Nuance:** It causes a *distorted* or *slowed* sense of time, not a heightened one.
* **Chronic Use:** Primarily leads to respiratory failure and obstructive lung disease due to smoke inhalation. It does not typically cause liver or renal failure.

### Assessment Tools

* **MAST-G:** Specifically designed and validated for older adults (geriatric).
* **POSIT:** Specifically for screening adolescents (teenagers).
* **SASSI:** A general screening inventory for adults.
* **CAGE-AID:** A standard assessment adapted to include drug use.
