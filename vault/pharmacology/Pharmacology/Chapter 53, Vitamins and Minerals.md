# Chapter 53, Vitamins and Minerals

To master the pharmacology of vitamins and minerals, a student must understand the physiological roles, deficiency states, toxicity profiles, and specific nursing considerations for each substance. Below are the main points and critical nuances derived from the study of this topic.

### 1. Classification and General Characteristics

* **Fat-Soluble Vitamins (A, D, E, K):** These are stored in the liver and adipose tissue for long periods. Because they are not easily excreted in the urine, they carry a significantly higher risk of toxicity (hypervitaminosis) when taken in megadoses. They generally require bile for absorption.
* **Water-Soluble Vitamins (B-complex, C):** These are not stored in the body in significant amounts (with the exception of B12) and are excreted in the urine. They must be consumed daily. While generally safer, megadoses of B6 (pyridoxine) can still cause nerve damage.

### 2. Fat-Soluble Vitamins: Main Points and Nuances

* **Vitamin A (Retinol):**
* **Role:** Essential for night vision and maintenance of epithelial surfaces.
* **Deficiency:** Leads to night blindness, xerophthalmia, and keratomalacia (softening of the cornea).
* **Nuance:** Toxicity manifests differently by age. In infants, it causes increased intracranial pressure leading to exophthalmos (bulging eyeballs). In children, it can cause vertigo, delirium, and coma.


* **Vitamin D:**
* **Activation Pathway:** It must be biotransformed. Synthesis begins in the skin (sunlight), moves to the liver to form calcifediol, and is finally converted in the kidneys to calcitriol (the most active form).
* **Deficiency:** Causes rickets in children (bowlegs, knock knees, organ enlargement) and osteomalacia in adults.
* **Nuance:** Contraindicated in hypercalcemia, hyperphosphatemia, and renal dysfunction. If a patient is taking lubricant laxatives, oral absorption is impaired, necessitating IV administration.


* **Vitamin E:**
* **Role:** An antioxidant that protects cell membranes and inhibits platelet aggregation.
* **Nuance:** Significant deficiency is primarily seen in premature infants, manifesting as hemolytic anemia, edema, and thrombosis. Toxicity is rare compared to other fat-soluble vitamins.


* **Vitamin K:**
* **Role:** Essential for the synthesis of coagulation factors II, VII, IX (Christmas factor), and X (Stuart-Prower factor).
* **Nuance:** It is the specific antidote for Warfarin. However, excessive doses can cause brain hemorrhage or aneurysms. It is synthesized by intestinal flora; thus, a reduction in gut flora (e.g., due to antibiotics) leads to deficiency.



### 3. Water-Soluble Vitamins: Main Points and Nuances

* **Vitamin B1 (Thiamine):**
* **Deficiency:** Causes Beriberi or Wernicke’s encephalopathy. Key signs include brain lesions, cardiac anatomic changes, and serous effusions.
* **Nuance:** Interaction with alkaline drugs causes thiamine to be excreted too rapidly, negating its effect.


* **Vitamin B3 (Niacin):**
* **Role:** Involved in tissue respiration and lipid metabolism. Used to treat hyperlipidemia (lowers triglycerides).
* **Deficiency:** Causes Pellagra (manifested by the "3 Ds": Dermatitis, Diarrhea, and Dementia/Neurasthenic syndrome; also glossitis).
* **Nuance:** High doses cause flushing and pruritus, which are expected side effects, not necessarily toxicity. It is contraindicated in patients with peptic ulcers, hypotension, or coronary artery disease.


* **Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine):**
* **Deficiency:** Often drug-induced by Isoniazid (INH) or Hydralazine. Manifests as sideroblastic anemia or stomatitis.
* **Nuance:** It inhibits the action of Levodopa in Parkinson’s disease. However, if given with Carbidopa, this interaction is neutralized.


* **Vitamin B12 (Cyanocobalamin):**
* **Role:** Required for erythropoiesis (red blood cell formation) and myelin synthesis.
* **Deficiency:** Leads to pernicious/megaloblastic anemia and degenerative CNS lesions. Common in chronic alcoholics.
* **Nuance:** Requires Intrinsic Factor (secreted by gastric parietal cells) and gastric acid for absorption; therefore, oral doses should be taken with meals. It is strictly contraindicated in Leber’s disease (hereditary optic nerve atrophy) as it can cause rapid blindness.


* **Vitamin C (Ascorbic Acid):**
* **Role:** Tissue repair and collagen synthesis.
* **Deficiency:** Scurvy (gingivitis, bleeding gums, bone lesions, hardening of leg muscles).
* **Nuance:** Megadoses can cause renal stones. It acidifies the urine, which can deactivate certain drugs like Penicillin G and Erythromycin. Conversely, drugs like oral contraceptives and barbiturates can deplete Vitamin C levels.



### 4. Minerals: Main Points and Nuances

* **Calcium:**
* **Role:** Bone structure and blood coagulation.
* **Deficiency:** Osteoporosis and osteomalacia.
* **Nuance:** It binds with tetracycline antibiotics, negating their effect. Rapid IV infusion can cause cardiac arrest; if extravasation occurs, undiluted calcium can cause severe tissue necrosis.


* **Magnesium:**
* **Role:** Energy metabolism and nerve/muscle function. Used therapeutically for preeclampsia and seizures.
* **Nuance:** Toxicity (hypermagnesemia) causes loss of deep tendon reflexes (DTRs), CNS depression, and respiratory distress. The antidote for magnesium toxicity is IV Calcium Gluconate.


* **Zinc:**
* **Role:** Crucial for enzymatic reactions, tissue growth, and specifically wound healing.


* **Phosphorus:**
* **Nuance:** Long-term use of aluminum or calcium-based antacids can lead to phosphorus deficiency because they bind together in the gut.



### 5. Critical Mastery Nuances for Nursing

* **Assessment Priorities:** Always assess for allergies (e.g., cobalt allergy for B12) and baseline conditions (e.g., night vision for Vitamin A) before administration.
* **Administration Routes:** IM is preferred for B12 when absorption is an issue (e.g., pernicious anemia or anticonvulsant use). IV is required for Vitamin D if intestinal absorption is blocked by laxatives.
* **Laboratory Monitoring:** Monitor Prothrombin Time (PT) when giving Vitamin K for Warfarin toxicity, and monitor EKG/cardiac rhythm during IV Calcium infusions.
