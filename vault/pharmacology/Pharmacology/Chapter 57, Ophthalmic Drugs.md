# Chapter 57, Ophthalmic Drugs

To master the pharmacology of ophthalmic drugs, a student must understand the various classifications of medications used to treat ocular disorders, their mechanisms of action, specific administration techniques, and unique adverse effect profiles.

### Main Points for Student Mastery

#### 1. Glaucoma Management and Intraocular Pressure (IOP)

Glaucoma is characterized by increased IOP that can damage the optic nerve. Treatment focuses on either decreasing the production of aqueous humor or increasing its outflow.

* **Prostaglandin Agonists (e.g., Latanoprost):** These are first-line agents that reduce IOP. They have a long duration of action (24 hours) and are typically administered once daily in the evening.
* **Beta-Adrenergic Blockers (e.g., Betaxolol, Timolol):** These decrease aqueous humor formation. Betaxolol is notable for being a beta-1 selective blocker.
* **Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors (e.g., Dorzolamide, Brinzolamide):** These inhibit the enzyme carbonic anhydrase to decrease aqueous humor production.
* **Cholinergic Drugs (Miotics):** * **Direct-acting (e.g., Pilocarpine):** Mimics acetylcholine to cause pupillary constriction (miosis) and ciliary muscle contraction, enhancing drainage.
* **Indirect-acting (e.g., Echothiophate):** Inhibits cholinesterase, allowing acetylcholine to accumulate.


* **Sympathomimetics (e.g., Epinephrine, Dipivefrin):** Used to reduce IOP in chronic open-angle glaucoma.
* **Osmotic Diuretics (e.g., Mannitol):** Administered intravenously to rapidly reduce IOP before surgery or during acute angle-closure episodes.

#### 2. Ophthalmic Antimicrobials

* **Antibacterials:** Sulfacetamide is used for general infections; Bacitracin is often applied as an ointment to the lower conjunctival sac; Ciprofloxacin is used for bacterial keratitis.
* **Antifungals:** Natamycin is the primary agent for Candida and Aspergillus infections.
* **Antivirals:** Trifluridine is used for ocular herpes simplex infections.
* **Newborn Prophylaxis:** Erythromycin ointment is standard protocol for newborns to prevent blindness caused by gonorrhea transmission during birth.

#### 3. Anti-inflammatory and Diagnostic Agents

* **Corticosteroids:** Used for inflammatory conditions but carry risks of cataracts and increased IOP with prolonged use.
* **NSAIDs:** Ketorolac and Flurbiprofen are used for post-operative inflammation and to inhibit miosis during surgery.
* **Topical Anesthetics:** Proparacaine and Tetracaine provide rapid pain relief for procedures or foreign body removal.
* **Diagnostic Dyes:** Fluorescein highlights corneal defects in bright green or yellow-orange and identifies foreign bodies by creating a green halo around them.

### Nuances to be Mastered

#### 1. Unique Adverse Effects and Patient Teaching

* **Iris Color Change:** Prostaglandin agonists like latanoprost can permanently turn hazel, green, or bluish-brown eyes **brown**. This is a cosmetic effect and does not require discontinuation of the drug.
* **Crystalline Precipitates:** Ophthalmic ciprofloxacin can cause white crystalline precipitates to form in corneal lesions. This is self-limiting and generally does not require stopping the medication.
* **Secondary Glaucoma:** The antiviral drug trifluridine can cause secondary glaucoma; any change in visual acuity while on this drug must be reported immediately.
* **Systemic Absorption:** To prevent systemic absorption of potent eye drops, patients must be taught to apply pressure to the **inner canthus (lacrimal duct)** for at least one minute after instillation.

#### 2. Specific Administration Requirements

* **Spacing Medications:** When two different eye drops are prescribed, the nurse/patient must wait **5 minutes** between them to ensure proper absorption.
* **Gels vs. Drops:** Ophthalmic gels (like pilocarpine gel) are typically applied at bedtime in a half-inch ribbon to the lower conjunctival sac.
* **Natamycin Frequency:** Unlike many other drops, natamycin often requires **hourly** instillation initially for 14 to 21 days.
* **Sterility:** Droppers must never touch the eye or any other surface (including tap water) to prevent contamination.

#### 3. Contraindications and Assessments

* **Sulfa Allergy:** Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (dorzolamide, brinzolamide) are chemically related to sulfonamide antibiotics. They are contraindicated or must be used with extreme caution in patients with a **sulfa allergy**.
* **Renal Function:** Osmotic diuretics like mannitol are contraindicated in anuric patients (e.g., end-stage renal disease).
* **Cataract Assessment:** Students should recognize that cataracts present as a **gray-white opacity** within the lens and may cause **diplopia** (double vision) and blurred vision, but typically not severe pain.
* **Mydriatic Burning:** Sympathomimetic mydriatics often cause temporary burning or stinging. This is a harmless side effect, and the patient should be reassured rather than having the eye rinsed.
