# Chapter 23, Antianginal Drugs

To master the pharmacology of antianginal drugs, a student must understand the mechanisms of action, administration protocols, and safety considerations for three primary classes: Nitrates, Beta Blockers, and Calcium Channel Blockers.

### Main Points for Mastery

#### 1. Nitroglycerin and Nitrates

* **Mechanism and Goal:** These drugs cause systemic vasodilation, which increases blood flow to the heart muscle and reduces the heart's workload. The primary therapeutic goal is the complete absence of chest pain.
* **Acute Administration:** Sublingual tablets are used for immediate relief of sudden chest pain. They should be placed under the tongue to bypass the "first-pass effect."
* **Dosing Protocol:** For an acute attack, a patient may take one tablet every 5 minutes for a maximum of three doses. If pain persists after the first dose, the patient should seek emergency medical care while continuing the protocol.
* **Prophylactic Use:** Sublingual nitroglycerin can be taken before engaging in known stressful behaviors or physical activities to prevent an expected angina episode.
* **Storage and Potency:** Nitroglycerin is highly sensitive to light, heat, and humidity. It must be kept in its original brown, light-resistant glass bottle. Prescriptions should be replaced every 3 to 6 months to ensure potency.

#### 2. Beta Blockers (e.g., Atenolol, Metoprolol)

* **Therapeutic Effect:** These are used as maintenance therapy to prevent chest pain by minimizing the negative effects of catecholamines on the heart, especially after a myocardial infarction.
* **Priority Assessment:** Nurses must always check the patient’s pulse before administration.
* **Contraindications:** They are generally avoided in patients with systolic heart failure and must be used with extreme caution in patients with diabetes.

#### 3. Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs) (e.g., Nifedipine, Diltiazem, Verapamil)

* **Mechanism:** CCBs decrease the workload of the heart by promoting muscle relaxation and decreasing afterload. They are often the drug of choice for Prinzmetal (variant) angina.
* **Dietary Interactions:** Grapefruit and grapefruit juice must be avoided as they reduce the metabolism of certain CCBs (like nifedipine), leading to potential toxicity.
* **Contraindications:** CCBs are contraindicated in patients with hypotension and those with second- or third-degree atrioventricular (AV) blocks who do not have a pacemaker.

### Critical Nuances to Master

#### Drug Tolerance Prevention

The body can develop a tolerance to nitrates, rendering them ineffective. To prevent this, patients must have a "nitrate-free period" of 8 to 12 hours every day. For example, transdermal patches should be applied in the morning and removed at night.

#### Safety and Vasodilation

Because antianginals cause vasodilation, they carry a high risk of orthostatic hypotension and syncope.

* **Environmental Triggers:** Patients must avoid hot showers, hot tubs, saunas, and hot weather, as heat exacerbates vasodilation.
* **Positioning:** Patients should always sit or lie down before taking nitroglycerin and should change positions slowly to prevent dizziness.

#### Diabetes and Beta Blockers

A critical nuance is the interaction between beta blockers and blood glucose. Beta blockers can cause both hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia. More importantly, they can mask the clinical signs of hypoglycemia (like tachycardia), making it difficult for a diabetic patient to realize their blood sugar is dangerously low.

#### Contraindications and Fatal Interactions

* **PDE5 Inhibitors:** Taking nitrates with erectile dysfunction medications (like sildenafil) is strictly contraindicated and can be fatal due to extreme hypotension.
* **Anemia:** Nitrates are contraindicated in patients with severe anemia because the vasodilation can worsen tissue oxygenation.
* **Head Injury:** The vasodilatory effects can increase intracranial pressure, making nitrates dangerous for patients with severe head injuries.

#### IV Administration Nuances

* **Nitroglycerin IV:** This must be regulated via an infusion pump and requires continuous blood pressure monitoring. If the blood pressure drops too low (e.g., systolic below 90 mm Hg), the nurse should slow the infusion rather than stopping it abruptly, as stopping it could precipitate an immediate anginal attack.
* **Route Accuracy:** Some CCBs, like amlodipine, are only available for oral use. Students must be vigilant about clarifying orders that specify incorrect routes (such as amlodipine IV).

#### Side Effect Management

* **Headaches:** Headache is the most common side effect of nitrate therapy due to vasodilation. Patients should be taught that this usually diminishes with continued therapy and can be managed with acetaminophen.
* **Constipation:** This is a common side effect of CCBs. Patients should be instructed to increase their intake of high-fiber foods and fluids.
* **Contact Dermatitis:** When using topical or transdermal nitrates, a rash at the site is common. This is managed by rotating administration sites rather than stopping the medication.
