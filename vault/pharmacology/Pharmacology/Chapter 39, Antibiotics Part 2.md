# Chapter 39, Antibiotics Part 2

To master the concepts of complex antibiotic therapy, a student must understand the specific indications, severe adverse effects, and critical nursing interventions associated with various drug classes, particularly aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones, and glycopeptides.

### Main Points for Mastery

#### 1. Aminoglycosides (Amikacin, Gentamicin, Tobramycin, Neomycin, Streptomycin)

* **General Characteristics:** These are potent antibiotics primarily used for serious gram-negative infections (e.g., *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*) and specific gram-positive infections like bacterial endocarditis.
* **Toxicity Profiles:** The two most significant concerns are **nephrotoxicity** (renal damage) and **ototoxicity** (hearing and vestibular damage).
* **Nephrotoxicity:** Indicated by rising serum creatinine and blood urea nitrogen (BUN). Aminoglycosides should be held if renal function tests are significantly elevated.
* **Otototoxicity:** Symptoms include tinnitus (ringing in ears), vertigo, headache, and hearing loss. Risk increases when combined with loop diuretics (e.g., furosemide).


* **Therapeutic Drug Monitoring:** Because of their narrow therapeutic index, monitoring **trough levels** is essential. Trough levels should be drawn immediately (about 10 minutes) before the next dose. For gentamicin, a normal trough is typically between 1 and 2 mcg/mL.
* **Synergistic Effect:** Aminoglycosides are often given with beta-lactam antibiotics (like penicillin). The penicillin should be administered first to break down the bacterial cell wall, allowing the aminoglycoside to enter the cell more effectively.
* **Specific Agent Uses:**
* **Neomycin:** Used for bacterial decontamination of the GI tract before surgery (oral or rectal) and to reduce ammonia-producing bacteria in hepatic encephalopathy.
* **Amikacin:** Often reserved for infections resistant to gentamicin or tobramycin.
* **Tobramycin:** Commonly administered via inhalation for pulmonary infections in patients with cystic fibrosis.
* **Streptomycin:** Indicated for endocarditis and tuberculosis.



#### 2. Fluoroquinolones (Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin)

* **Indications:** Ciprofloxacin is notably the drug of choice for treating **anthrax**.
* **Black Box Warning/Major Risks:**
* **Tendonitis and Tendon Rupture:** The risk is significantly increased in elderly patients, those with renal failure, and those concurrently taking **glucocorticoids** (e.g., prednisone).
* **QT Prolongation:** These drugs can cause dangerous cardiac rhythms; patients should report any palpitations or irregular heartbeats.


* **Drug Interactions:** They chelate with divalent minerals. Patients must avoid taking them simultaneously with antacids, iron, or dairy; antacids should be spaced at least 2 hours apart from the antibiotic.
* **Liver Injury:** Patients should monitor for signs of hepatotoxicity, such as a yellowish hue to the skin (jaundice).

#### 3. Vancomycin

* **Indications:** The primary treatment for MRSA and other serious gram-positive infections.
* **Administration Safety:** Must be infused slowly over **60 to 120 minutes**.
* **Red Man Syndrome:** Rapid infusion causes a histamine release resulting in flushing/itching of the head, neck, face, and upper trunk. If this occurs, the nurse should **slow the infusion rate**, not necessarily stop it.
* **Hypotension:** Rapid infusion can also lead to a dangerous drop in blood pressure.


* **Monitoring:** Similar to aminoglycosides, vancomycin is nephrotoxic and ototoxic. Trough levels are typically maintained between 10 and 20 mcg/mL.

#### 4. Miscellaneous Antibiotics

* **Linezolid:** Used for MRSA and VRE.
* **Serotonin Syndrome:** Contraindicated with SSRIs (e.g., sertraline) as it can cause fatal hypertension and tachycardia.
* **Tyramine Interaction:** Patients must avoid tyramine-rich foods (smoked meats, aged cheeses, red wine) to prevent severe hypertension.
* **Myelosuppression:** Can cause bone marrow suppression; monitoring **platelet counts** is a priority.


* **Nitrofurantoin:** Primarily for UTIs. It should be taken with food or milk to enhance absorption and decrease GI distress. It can cause peripheral neuropathy and rare hepatotoxicity.
* **Metronidazole:** Effective against anaerobic bacteria (e.g., *Clostridium*, *Bacteroides*) and protozoans.
* **Clindamycin:** Active against anaerobes but carries a risk of neuromuscular blockade; assess respiratory rate if used with neuromuscular blocking drugs like vecuronium.
* **Colistimethate:** Used via inhalation for sensitive infections; necessitates having emergency respiratory equipment nearby due to risks of renal and respiratory failure.

### Critical Nuances for Students

* **Assessment Timing:** Trough levels are the "lowest" point of drug concentration. Always draw these just before the next scheduled dose to ensure the kidneys are clearing the drug effectively and to prevent toxicity.
* **Superinfection Prevention:** For drugs like neomycin that disrupt GI flora, consuming yogurt or buttermilk can help restore normal flora and prevent complications.
* **Surgical Prophylaxis:** Neomycin is used orally or rectally *before* abdominal surgery to "cleanse" the bowel of bacteria. Using it after surgery for this purpose is ineffective.
* **Adherence Strategies:** For drugs like nitrofurantoin that cause significant nausea, the most important instruction for adherence is to take the medication with food. Simply telling a patient to "finish the prescription" is less effective if they are too nauseated to take the pills.
* **Hypersensitivity vs. Side Effect:** Pruritus (itching) during vancomycin is usually Red Man Syndrome (infusion-related) rather than a true IgE-mediated allergy. However, a comprehensive pre-administration assessment must always include a history of cardiac issues and hypersensitivity because antibiotics can trigger cardiac arrest in severe allergic reactions.
