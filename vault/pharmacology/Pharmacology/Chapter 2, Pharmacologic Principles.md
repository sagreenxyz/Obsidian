# Chapter 2, Pharmacologic Principles

To master the foundational principles of pharmacology, a student must understand how drugs move through the body (pharmacokinetics), how they affect the body (pharmacodynamics), and how various factors—such as formulation, route, and patient-specific conditions—alter these processes.

### Main Points for Mastery

#### 1. Pharmacokinetics: The Life Cycle of a Drug

* **Absorption:** This is the movement of a drug from its site of administration into the bloodstream. It is heavily influenced by the drug’s formulation (e.g., liquids absorb faster than tablets), the presence of food, and the pH of the stomach.
* **Distribution:** Drugs are carried by the blood to their sites of action. A key factor here is **protein binding**. Highly protein-bound drugs compete for binding sites on albumin; if a patient has low albumin or is taking two highly bound drugs, the amount of "free" active drug increases, leading to potential toxicity.
* **Metabolism (Biotransformation):** Primarily occurring in the liver, this process chemically alters the drug. The **First-Pass Effect** is critical: drugs taken orally are often metabolized by the liver before reaching systemic circulation, which significantly reduces their bioavailability.
* **Excretion:** The elimination of drugs from the body, primarily through the kidneys. Patients with renal failure require lower doses to prevent toxic accumulation.

#### 2. Pharmacodynamics: Mechanisms of Action

* **Receptor Interaction:** Drugs bind to specific reactive sites on or within cells to elicit a response.
* **Enzyme Interaction:** Drugs can inhibit or enhance the action of specific enzymes (selective interaction).
* **Nonselective Interaction:** Some drugs do not interact with receptors or enzymes but instead target cell structures (like bacterial cell walls) or metabolic processes.

#### 3. Drug Formulations and Onset of Action

* **Speed of Absorption:** For oral medications, the hierarchy of absorption speed is generally: Oral soluble wafers (fastest) > Elixirs/Syrups > Suspensions > Powders > Capsules > Tablets > Coated tablets > Enteric-coated tablets (slowest).
* **Enteric Coating:** Designed to dissolve in the alkaline environment of the small intestine rather than the acidic stomach. These must never be crushed.

#### 4. Therapeutic Timing and Monitoring

* **Half-life:** The time required for 50% of a drug to be eliminated from the body. It takes approximately four to five half-lives for a drug to be effectively removed or to reach a **steady state** (where drug intake equals drug elimination).
* **Therapeutic Index (TI):** The ratio between a drug's toxic level and its therapeutic level. A **low TI** means the margin of safety is narrow, requiring frequent monitoring to avoid overdose.

#### 5. Adverse Drug Reactions

* **Pharmacologic:** A known, predictable extension of the drug's therapeutic effect (e.g., an antihypertensive causing hypotension).
* **Allergic:** An unpredictable immunologic hypersensitivity.
* **Idiosyncratic:** An unexpected, genetically determined response (e.g., G6PD deficiency).
* **Teratogenic:** Structural defects in a developing fetus.
* **Mutagenic:** Changes in genetic structure.
* **Carcinogenic:** Cancer-causing.

### Nuances and Advanced Mastery

#### 1. Circumventing the First-Pass Effect

To bypass the liver's initial metabolism and ensure higher bioavailability, drugs must be administered via routes that do not enter the portal circulation. These include:

* Intravenous (IV), Intramuscular (IM), and Subcutaneous (SC) injections.
* Sublingual and Buccal (absorbed via oral mucosa).
* Transdermal (patches), Intranasal, and Intraocular.

#### 2. Critical Food-Drug Interactions

* **Grapefruit Juice:** Inhibits the metabolism of many drugs (like amiodarone or carbamazepine), leading to increased drug levels and toxicity.
* **Dairy Products:** Can have an **antagonistic effect** on certain antibiotics (like ciprofloxacin) by binding to the drug and preventing absorption.
* **Aged Cheeses/Tyramine:** Interacts with Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs) to cause a potentially fatal hypertensive crisis.

#### 3. Nuances of Formulation Integrity

* **Enteric-Coated Tablets:** Mastery requires knowing that **anticholinergic drugs** (which slow GI motility) or large amounts of food can cause the coating to dissolve prematurely in the stomach, neutralizing the drug's effect or causing gastric irritation.
* **Extended-Release Granules:** If a patient cannot swallow a capsule, the granules inside can often be sprinkled on soft food, but they must **not be chewed**, as this destroys the timed-release mechanism and leads to "dose dumping."

#### 4. Specific Therapeutic Strategies

* **Empiric Therapy:** Treating based on clinical probability before lab results are back (e.g., starting antibiotics before a culture is finished).
* **Prophylactic Therapy:** Preventing an infection or complication before it occurs (e.g., preoperative antibiotics).
* **Palliative Therapy:** Focuses on comfort and symptom relief rather than curing the disease (e.g., high-dose opioids for end-stage cancer).
* **Maintenance Therapy:** Used for chronic conditions to prevent disease progression (e.g., oral contraceptives or blood pressure meds).

#### 5. Synergy vs. Additive Effects

* **Additive Effect:** 1 + 1 = 2 (Two drugs with similar actions given together).
* **Synergistic Effect:** 1 + 1 = 3 (The combination of two drugs results in an effect greater than the sum of their individual parts, such as HCTZ and Lisinopril for blood pressure).
* **Antagonistic Effect:** 1 + 1 = less than 2 (One drug interferes with the action of another).

#### 6. Genetic Nuances (G6PD Deficiency)

Students must recognize that patients with **Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency** are at high risk for life-threatening **hemolysis of red blood cells** when exposed to certain oxidizing drugs (like primaquine, sulfonamides, or aspirin). This is a classic example of an idiosyncratic reaction.
