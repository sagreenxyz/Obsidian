# Chapter 1, The Nursing Process and Drug Therapy

To master the nursing process as it relates to drug therapy, a student must understand both the structural framework of the process and the specific, high-stakes nuances of medication safety.

### 1. The Assessment Phase: Data Collection

The foundation of the nursing process relies on the accurate collection and categorization of data.

* **Subjective Data:** This includes information shared or reported by the patient that cannot be measured by the nurse. Examples include reports of pain, "decreased sensation," "difficulty breathing," or "feeling nauseated."
* **Objective Data:** This consists of observable and measurable facts. Examples include vital signs (blood pressure, temperature), laboratory results (BUN, CT scans), physical findings (edema, pale skin, drooping eyelid), and medical records.
* **Data Sources:** While the patient is the primary source, the nurse must also consult family members, caregivers, and previous medical investigation reports.
* **Medication History:** A thorough history must include allergies, current drugs, the patient's ability to swallow, and their ability to afford or obtain their medications.

### 2. Human Needs Statements (Nursing Diagnosis)

Instead of using medical diagnoses (like "Dementia"), nurses use human needs statements to guide care.

* **Structure:** These statements are created using both subjective and objective data.
* **Parts of the Statement:**
* **Part 1:** The human response to a condition (e.g., declining cognition).
* **Part 2:** The factors related to that response.
* **Part 3:** The evidence supporting the statement.



### 3. The Planning Phase: Goals and Prioritization

Planning involves setting realistic targets and determining the order of care.

* **The Primary Goal:** The most important goal of any medication care plan is the safe and effective administration of drugs.
* **Outcome Criteria:** Goals must be SMART: Specific, Measurable, Attainable (Realistic), Relevant (Patient-centered), and Time-bound.
* **Prioritization:** In emergency or complex situations, always follow the ABCs: Airway, Breathing, and Circulation. For example, oxygen administration for hypoxia takes priority over wound care or antibiotics.

### 4. The Implementation Phase: Medication Administration

This phase requires strict adherence to the "Rights" of medication administration to prevent errors.

* **Verification of Orders:** A valid prescription must contain seven elements: patient name, date written, drug name, dosage, frequency, route, and the prescriber’s signature.
* **Clarification:** If any part of an order is missing, vague, or contains a suspected error (e.g., missing route or unspecified tablet strength), the nurse **must** contact the health care provider. Never assume a route or ask a peer for a "common" dose.
* **Identifying the Patient:** Always use at least two identifiers. The most accurate method is checking the patient's identification band against the medication order. Do not rely on calling the patient by name if they are confused or disoriented.
* **The "Right Time" Window:** Routine medications should generally be administered within 30 minutes before or after the scheduled time.
* **Dosing Nuances:**
* **Pediatrics:** Pediatric patients are more sensitive to drugs; doses must be calculated based on age and size.
* **Decimals:** Never use trailing zeros (e.g., write "5 mg," not "5.0 mg") to avoid ten-fold dosing errors. Leading zeros are required (e.g., "0.5 mg").


* **The Right to Refuse:** Patients have the autonomy to refuse medication. The nurse should determine the reason for refusal, notify the provider, and document the refusal and its reason.

### 5. Documentation Standards

Proper documentation is a legal and professional requirement that must be performed **after** the medication is given, never before.

* **What to Include:** The facts of the administration, the time, the route, the patient’s response, and any patient education provided.
* **Medication Errors:** If an error occurs, document only the facts, the patient's response, and that the provider was notified.
* **What to Omit:** Never include negative language, staffing problems, or mentions of internal "incident reports" in the patient’s medical record. Use of abbreviations (like "tid" or "prn") should be avoided in favor of writing out "three times a day" or "as needed."

### 6. The Evaluation Phase: Monitoring Outcomes

The nursing process is cyclical. Evaluation involves determining if the goals were met and monitoring the patient for:

* Therapeutic responses to the medication.
* Adverse effects and toxicities.
* The need for changes in the subsequent dosage schedule if a dose was missed or delayed.

# Chapter 1, The Nursing Process and Drug Therapy

Based on the performance details provided, here are the main points and critical nuances a student must master regarding the nursing process and its application to drug therapy.

### 1. Fundamental Steps of the Nursing Process

* **Assessment:** Distinguishing between **subjective data** (what the patient reports, like "chest pain" or "palpitations") and **objective data** (what the nurse observes or measures, like a "rapid heart rate").
* **Medication Reconciliation:** Mastery of the four distinct stages is required:
* **Verification:** Collecting all medication history (the step where you ask about pre-hospitalization prescriptions).
* **Clarification:** Professional review of the medications.
* **Reconciliation:** Investigating discrepancies.
* **Transition:** Transferring the patient between facilities or to home.


* **Implementation Priorities:** In emergency or high-risk situations (like IV phenytoin), the priority is often the **method of administration** (e.g., slow IV push) over secondary tasks like monitoring serum levels.

### 2. Pharmacokinetics and Safety Nuances

* **Organ Function:** Understanding that the renal system is primarily responsible for the **excretion** phase. A compromised renal system leads to drug accumulation and toxicity.
* **Half-life vs. Duration:** Students must differentiate between a drug’s **elimination half-life** (e.g., 1–1.5 hours for Amoxicillin), its **onset of action** (0.5–1 hour), and its **duration of action** (6–8 hours).
* **Therapeutic Index:** Recognizing that **drug concentration** in the blood is the primary tool for evaluating clinical response, as it indicates whether a drug is within a therapeutic or toxic range.

### 3. Medication Administration and Legalities

* **Controlled Substances:** Mastery of the scheduling system is vital. For example, **Schedule II (C-II)** drugs (like methylphenidate) require a written prescription and cannot be refilled.
* **Drug Forms and Storage:** * Knowing the physical signs of drug degradation (e.g., a **vinegar odor** in aspirin indicates chemical breakdown).
* Handling ampules: Tapping the neck to move liquid, snapping away from the body, and using a **filter needle** for withdrawal (though never for delivery).


* **Injections:** The **ventrogluteal site** is the preferred and safest site for adult IM injections (like tetanus toxoid) because it is deep and away from major nerves/vessels, whereas the deltoid carries higher risk for nerve injury.

### 4. Special Populations and Genetics

* **Elderly Patients:** Vigilance is required for specific drug-class risks, such as **hyperosmotic laxatives** (polyethylene glycol) being generally avoided, and the increased risk of **urinary retention** when taking antihistamines like diphenhydramine.
* **Pediatrics:** Diarrhea in children is an emergency if accompanied by **lethargy, bloody stools, or a rigid abdomen**, as they are highly sensitive to rapid fluid/electrolyte loss.
* **Genetics in Nursing:** Nurses must be able to conduct a **three-generation** medical history to uncover genetic risks. Knowledge of "genetic chip technology" and how genetics influence drug reactions is now a baseline requirement for all nurses, not just specialists.

### 5. Critical Drug Interactions and Contraindications

* **Serotonin Syndrome:** A nuance often missed is the risk of combining drugs like **TCAs and MAOIs** (phenelzine) or **Sumatriptan and Ergotamines**. Symptoms to master include shivering, hyperthermia, muscle spasms, and mental status changes.
* **Nitrate Interactions:** Nitrates have life-threatening interactions with **PDE5 inhibitors** (erectile dysfunction drugs like tadalafil), which can result in fatal hypotension.
* **Sulfa Allergies:** Students must identify drugs structurally similar to sulfonamides, such as **furosemide**, which should be used cautiously or avoided in patients with sulfa allergies.

### 6. Mastery of "Drug of Choice" and Rationales

* **Specific Antidotes:** Deferiprone for iron overdose; Leucovorin for high-dose methotrexate toxicity.
* **Condition-Specific Meds:** Bethanechol for post-op bladder atony; Pyridostigmine for Myasthenia Gravis; Sulfasalazine for Ulcerative Colitis.
* **Mechanism Nuances:** Adsorbents (anti-diarrheals) work by **binding to bacteria** for elimination, not by decreasing motility or increasing absorption.
